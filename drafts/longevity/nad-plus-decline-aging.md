Inside every cell in your body, a molecule called NAD+ is working without rest. It ferries electrons through the metabolic reactions that convert food into energy. It activates enzymes that repair damaged DNA. It regulates the sirtuins — proteins that govern gene expression in response to stress and nutrient availability. It participates in more than 500 enzymatic reactions.

Without adequate NAD+, these systems degrade. Cells lose repair capacity. Mitochondria become less efficient. Sirtuin activity drops. The downstream effects touch nearly every aspect of cellular aging.

And between ages 40 and 60, NAD+ levels in human cells decline by roughly 50%.

This is why NAD+ has become one of the most intensely studied molecules in longevity research — and why supplements that claim to raise it have become a multi-billion-dollar industry.

## What NAD+ Does

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) functions primarily as a coenzyme — a molecular carrier that transfers electrons in oxidation-reduction reactions. In mitochondria, this electron transfer is the engine of ATP production: NAD+ accepts electrons from food molecules, carries them to the electron transport chain, and helps generate the ATP your cells run on. Without NAD+, cellular energy production collapses.

But NAD+ has a second, equally important role as a signaling molecule. Three major longevity-linked protein families depend on it:

**PARP enzymes (poly ADP-ribose polymerases):** The primary DNA repair proteins in your cells. When DNA is damaged — by oxidative stress, radiation, or replication errors — PARP enzymes detect the breaks and coordinate repair. PARP activity consumes NAD+. A cell under chronic oxidative stress (as aging cells typically are) depletes NAD+ through constant PARP activation, creating a vicious cycle: more damage, more PARP activity, less NAD+ available for other functions.

**Sirtuins:** Seven sirtuin proteins (SIRT1-7) are sometimes called the "longevity proteins." They deacetylate histones and transcription factors, regulating gene expression in response to metabolic state. SIRT1 activates autophagy and stress resistance. SIRT3 maintains mitochondrial function. SIRT6 promotes DNA repair and genomic stability. All sirtuins require NAD+ to function — when NAD+ drops, sirtuin activity drops with it, removing a layer of cellular protection.

**CD38:** An enzyme that degrades NAD+, and which becomes more active with age and chronic inflammation. CD38 is a major driver of NAD+ decline in aging — the body is increasingly destroying its own NAD+ faster than it can be replenished.

## Why NAD+ Declines With Age

The decline has multiple causes working simultaneously:

- PARP activity increases with age as DNA damage accumulates, consuming more NAD+
- CD38 expression increases with chronic inflammation (a hallmark of aging), accelerating NAD+ degradation
- NAD+ biosynthesis slows as the salvage pathway enzymes become less efficient
- Metabolic dysfunction (obesity, insulin resistance) further depletes NAD+ through chronic PARP activation

The result is a progressive NAD+ deficit that worsens across midlife and accelerates late-life cellular dysfunction.

## The Precursor Supplements: NMN and NR

The body synthesizes NAD+ through several pathways, but the most important for supplementation purposes is the salvage pathway: tryptophan → NMN → NAD+, with NR (nicotinamide riboside) as an alternative entry point.

Two supplements have dominated the NAD+ precursor market:

**NR (nicotinamide riboside):** The earlier-to-market option with more published human trials. Multiple randomized controlled trials have confirmed that oral NR supplementation raises blood NAD+ levels in humans. A 2018 trial found 250-500 mg/day NR produced dose-dependent increases in blood NAD+. The human safety profile is well-established.

**NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide):** More proximal to NAD+ in the biosynthetic pathway. A 2022 human RCT showed NMN supplementation (250 mg/day) raised blood NAD+ and improved muscle insulin sensitivity in older women with prediabetes. David Sinclair at Harvard has been the most prominent advocate for NMN.

**The NMN vs. NR debate:** Both raise NAD+. They enter cells through different transporter pathways. NMN requires conversion to NR before cellular uptake (or uses a dedicated NMN transporter discovered in 2019). NR enters cells via nucleoside transporters. No head-to-head human RCT has directly compared their efficacy for clinically meaningful endpoints. The choice between them remains genuinely uncertain, and advocates on both sides overstate their confidence.

## What the Supplements Have and Haven't Shown

Honest assessment of the human evidence:

**What's established:** Both NR and NMN reliably raise circulating and tissue NAD+ levels in humans at commonly used doses (250-500 mg/day). This is not disputed.

**What's not established:** Whether raising NAD+ with supplements produces the functional benefits — improved healthspan, reduced disease risk, meaningful metabolic improvement — that the NAD+ biology predicts. Most human trials are 8-12 weeks long and underpowered for clinical outcomes. The animal data (where NAD+ precursors extend lifespan and reverse metabolic dysfunction dramatically) has not been replicated in human longevity endpoints. We don't have 30-year human studies on NMN because NMN has barely existed as a supplement for 10 years.

**Sinclair's position vs. the field:** David Sinclair's public advocacy for NMN (plus resveratrol, plus other longevity supplements) has been influential and commercially significant. His peers in aging biology have been more skeptical — not of the NAD+ biology, which is well-established, but of whether the specific supplement protocol produces meaningful human benefits at realistic doses. This is a live scientific disagreement, not settled fact.

## The Free Alternatives

Here is what the supplement industry would prefer you not contemplate too long: you can raise NAD+ significantly without buying anything.

**Exercise** is one of the most potent NAD+ inducers known. Aerobic exercise activates AMPK, which stimulates NAD+ biosynthesis via NAMPT upregulation. Resistance training produces similar effects through different mechanisms. Multiple studies have confirmed that regular exercisers have higher tissue NAD+ than sedentary controls — the difference is comparable in magnitude to supplement effects.

**Intermittent fasting and caloric restriction** raise NAD+ through the same AMPK pathway. The metabolic shift toward fat oxidation during fasting increases NAD+/NADH ratio and stimulates salvage pathway activity.

**Reducing chronic inflammation** slows CD38 activity, the main NAD+ consumer. The same anti-inflammatory lifestyle factors that support overall health — Mediterranean diet, sleep quality, stress management — support NAD+ maintenance.

These aren't alternatives that require choosing between supplements and lifestyle. They're the non-negotiable baseline. If someone is sedentary, eating poorly, sleeping badly, and managing chronic stress inadequately — NMN supplementation layered on top of those deficits will not move the needle in any meaningful way.

## The Honest Bottom Line

The NAD+ biology is real and important. The decline with aging is real and significant. The downstream effects on DNA repair, mitochondrial function, and sirtuin activity are mechanistically coherent with the aging phenotype.

NMN and NR supplements raise NAD+ in humans. This is established. Whether that biochemical effect translates into meaningful clinical outcomes at commercially available doses remains an open question. The supplement industry prices these products based on the biology and animal data; the human outcome data is still catching up.

For people who want to act on the NAD+ biology now: maximize the lifestyle interventions that raise NAD+ endogenously. Then, if you want to supplement after establishing that baseline, NR or NMN at 250-500 mg/day has a reasonable safety profile and biological plausibility, even if the human outcome evidence is still developing.

The most expensive supplement in the world won't substitute for the metabolic signaling that exercise and fasting produce. Start there.
