You sent in a saliva sample six weeks ago. The results came back with a number that wasn't your age — it was five years older. What does that actually mean? Should you be alarmed? And more importantly, is there anything you can do about it?

Epigenetic clocks have moved from academic curiosity to consumer product in under a decade, and the science behind them is genuinely remarkable. But the gap between what these tests *can* tell you and what the companies selling them *imply* they tell you is wide enough to drive a longevity supplement startup through. Here's what your score actually means — and what it doesn't.

---

## What Epigenetic Clocks Are Actually Measuring

Your DNA sequence is essentially fixed from birth. But layered on top of it is a second system — **epigenetic marks**, particularly methylation patterns, where methyl groups attach to cytosine bases at sites called CpG sites. These marks regulate gene expression: which genes get turned on, which stay silent, and how loudly they broadcast.

What Steve Horvath discovered in 2013, publishing in *Genome Biology*, was that a specific subset of these methylation patterns changes with age in a highly predictable way — across tissues, across people, and across mammalian species. By measuring roughly 353 CpG sites, his clock could estimate a person's age with a median error of about 3.6 years. The implications were immediate: if methylation patterns predict chronological age so reliably, deviations from that prediction — being "older" or "younger" than expected — might reflect something real about biological wear and tear.

The Horvath clock was the first-generation model. It was trained to predict *chronological* age as accurately as possible. What followed was a second generation of clocks trained not on age, but on **health outcomes** — which is where things get more interesting, and more clinically relevant.

---

## The Clock Landscape: First, Second, and Third Generation

Understanding which clock your test is using matters more than most companies acknowledge.

**The Horvath Clock (2013)** and the related **Hannum Clock** — developed by Gregory Hannum and colleagues at UC San Diego, published in *Molecular Cell* in 2013, using blood methylation data from 656 individuals — are first-generation tools. They're good at estimating age, less good at predicting mortality or disease. Accelerated aging on these clocks correlates weakly with outcomes in most studies.

**GrimAge**, developed by Ake Lu, Steve Horvath, and colleagues and published in *Aging* in 2019, is a significant leap. Rather than predicting chronological age, GrimAge was trained on time-to-death. It incorporates methylation-based proxies for smoking history, plasma proteins, and other biomarkers of physiological decline. In the Lu 2019 paper, a one-year increase in GrimAge acceleration was associated with a **hazard ratio of approximately 1.16 for all-cause mortality** — a clinically meaningful signal. GrimAge has since been independently validated in multiple cohorts.

**DunedinPACE** (Pace of Aging Computed from the Epigenome) is the most conceptually distinct. Developed by Dan Belsky, Avshalom Caspi, and colleagues at Duke and Columbia, published in *eLife* in 2022, it doesn't give you an estimated age — it gives you a **pace of aging score**. A score of 1.0 means you're aging at the average rate. A score of 1.2 means 20% faster than average. Crucially, it was trained on longitudinal data from the Dunedin cohort, following individuals from birth and measuring 19 biomarkers of organ system aging over time. This makes DunedinPACE a measure of *velocity*, not position — arguably more useful for tracking whether an intervention is working.

| Clock | Generation | Trained to predict | Mortality association |
|---|---|---|---|
| Horvath | 1st | Chronological age | Weak |
| Hannum | 1st | Chronological age | Weak |
| GrimAge | 2nd | Time to death | Strong (HR ~1.16 per year) |
| DunedinPACE | 3rd | Pace of aging | Moderate-strong |

## The Consumer Test Landscape

Two companies dominate the direct-to-consumer market for epigenetic age testing in the US.

**TruAge** (from TruDiagnostic) offers one of the more comprehensive panels available, reporting multiple clock scores including GrimAge, DunedinPACE, and their proprietary TruAge Complete. Their tests are blood-based via finger prick, which is important: blood methylation data is what most validated clocks were trained on, and using saliva or other tissues introduces noise. TruDiagnostic has published several preprints and collaborated with academic researchers, which gives them more scientific credibility than most consumer longevity companies — though their proprietary algorithms are not fully peer-reviewed.

**Elysium Index** uses the PhenoAge clock, developed by Morgan Levine and Steve Horvath and published in *Aging* in 2018. PhenoAge was trained on a composite of clinical biomarkers associated with mortality (including C-reactive protein, creatinine, and white blood cell count) and then mapped to methylation. In the original paper, PhenoAge acceleration was associated with increased mortality risk, with effect sizes comparable to GrimAge in some analyses. Elysium's test is saliva-based, which is less ideal for algorithmic precision.

For most users serious about tracking biological age over time, a blood-based test reporting GrimAge and/or DunedinPACE currently offers the most validated signal.

## What a Five-Year Acceleration Actually Means

Here's where precision matters. If your GrimAge score says you're biologically five years older than your chronological age, that is a **statistical association with mortality and disease risk** — not a diagnosis, not a fate, and not a precise prediction for you individually.

What the Lu 2019 GrimAge paper suggests is that a five-year acceleration in GrimAge corresponds to roughly a 60–80% increase in all-cause mortality hazard over follow-up periods of 5–20 years, depending on the cohort. That's meaningful — comparable to the risk associated with moderate smoking. But hazard ratios from population data tell you about groups, not individuals. Your score is a signal, not a sentence.

There's also the problem of **test-retest variability**. A 2022 analysis by Higgins-Chen and colleagues, published in *Nature Aging*, found that within-person variability in several popular clocks was large enough that a single test result contains substantial noise. DunedinPACE showed better reliability than most first-generation clocks, but even here, a single measurement shouldn't be over-interpreted. Trends across multiple tests — taken 6–12 months apart under consistent conditions — are far more meaningful than any one data point.

### What These Tests Cannot Tell You

Current epigenetic clocks don't capture the aging of every tissue equally. Blood methylation reflects hematopoietic aging; it may not accurately represent brain aging, musculoskeletal decline, or cardiovascular biological age. Organ-specific clocks are in development but not yet widely available in consumer products. A low GrimAge score doesn't mean your arteries are young.

## A Practical Protocol for Using Epigenetic Testing

If you're going to invest in epigenetic age testing, here's how to do it in a way that generates actionable signal rather than expensive anxiety:

**Before you test:**
- Avoid testing during periods of acute illness, intense exercise training blocks, or significant caloric restriction — these can temporarily shift methylation and confound your baseline
- Establish a true baseline before making major lifestyle changes, not after

**Choosing a test:**
- Prioritize blood-based tests over saliva for greater comparability to validated datasets
- Look for tests that report GrimAge and/or DunedinPACE specifically — not just proprietary composite scores without published validation
- TruDiagnostic's TruAge panel currently meets these criteria more reliably than most competitors

**Interpreting your results:**
- A 1–3 year acceleration from chronological age is within normal variation for most adults; treat it as informational, not alarming
- A consistent acceleration of 5+ years on GrimAge across multiple tests warrants a conversation with a physician — not because it predicts your death, but because the same lifestyle factors that drive epigenetic aging (poor sleep, sedentary behavior, smoking, metabolic dysfunction) are independently dangerous
- DunedinPACE is most useful as a **tracking tool** — test every 6–12 months to assess whether interventions (exercise, sleep improvement, dietary change) are shifting your pace of aging

**What actually moves the needle:**
Per the available intervention literature — including a 2021 study by Fitzgerald and colleagues in *Aging* (n=43, randomized controlled) — a combination of high-quality sleep, regular vigorous exercise (150+ minutes per week of Zone 2 plus 2x strength training), Mediterranean-style diet, and stress reduction reduced DNAmAge by an average of 3.23 years versus controls over eight weeks. These aren't surprising interventions — but having a measurable number attached to them changes the psychology of adherence for many people.

The value of epigenetic testing isn't that it tells you something your doctor couldn't otherwise detect. It's that it can make the invisible consequences of daily habits visible — and for the right person, that visibility is genuinely motivating.
