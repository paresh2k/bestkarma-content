The blood panel your GP orders once a year was designed to catch disease, not prevent it. A standard lipid panel, a fasting glucose, a TSH — these are tools built around a 1970s model of medicine, one that intervenes when you're already sick. A growing number of clinics are now selling something different: a comprehensive, proactive, data-rich picture of your biology, years or decades before conventional medicine would notice a problem. Some of them are delivering exactly that. Others are selling expensive anxiety dressed up as prevention.

The longevity clinic industry has grown fast enough to outpace its own evidence base. Knowing how to distinguish genuine clinical value from wellness theatre — and which specific tests and interventions are worth your money — is not optional if you're going to engage with this space seriously.

## What Legitimate Longevity Medicine Actually Looks Like

The term "longevity clinic" covers an enormous range — from rigorous, physician-led preventive medicine practices to IV drip lounges with a DEXA scanner in the back room. At the serious end, the work looks like what Peter Attia has described as "Medicine 3.0": a shift from reactive disease management toward probabilistic, individualized risk reduction across the major causes of premature death — cardiovascular disease, cancer, metabolic dysfunction, and neurodegenerative disease.

Clinically legitimate longevity medicine typically centers on a few categories of genuine value. **Advanced lipid testing** goes beyond LDL cholesterol to measure apolipoprotein B (ApoB), Lp(a), and particle number — markers that are substantially more predictive of cardiovascular events than standard LDL. A 2021 meta-analysis by Sniderman et al. in *JAMA Cardiology*, pooling data from 29 studies and over 200,000 participants, found that ApoB was a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than LDL-C across virtually all patient subgroups. Lp(a), a genetically determined lipoprotein that affects roughly 20% of the population at high-risk levels, is missed by every standard lipid panel ever ordered in a routine GP appointment.

**Metabolic and insulin dynamics** are another domain where longevity medicine adds genuine signal. Fasting glucose catches frank diabetes. It largely misses insulin resistance, which can smolder for a decade before glucose rises meaningfully. A fasting insulin, HOMA-IR calculation, or — better yet — an oral glucose tolerance test with insulin measurement gives a far earlier window into metabolic dysfunction. A 2019 study by Facchini et al. in *Diabetes Care* demonstrated that insulin resistance, measured by this method, predicts cardiovascular disease risk independently of blood pressure, smoking status, and lipid levels.

**Body composition analysis** via DEXA scan rounds out a legitimate baseline. It distinguishes lean mass from fat mass with precision that a BMI calculation or even a bioimpedance scale cannot approach, and quantifies visceral adipose tissue — the metabolically active fat depot most strongly linked to systemic inflammation and metabolic disease.

## The Tests Worth Paying For (and the Ones That Aren't)

The difference between a good longevity clinic and an expensive one often comes down to whether the diagnostics they order are likely to change what you do.

| Test | Evidence Tier | Worth It? |
|---|---|---|
| ApoB | Strong — outperforms LDL in multiple large meta-analyses | Yes |
| Lp(a) | Strong — genetically determined, missed by standard panels | Yes, once in life |
| HOMA-IR / fasting insulin | Moderate-strong — excellent early metabolic signal | Yes |
| DEXA body composition | Moderate — clinically useful, clear clinical action | Yes |
| VO2 max testing | Strong — one of the single best predictors of all-cause mortality | Yes |
| CGM (continuous glucose monitor) | Emerging — useful for metabolic insight in some populations | Situational |
| Full-body MRI | Emerging — high sensitivity, unclear net benefit, high false positive rate | Proceed with caution |
| Biological age clocks (e.g., DunedinPACE, GrimAge) | Emerging — research-grade, not yet clinically actionable for most | Research interest only |
| Telomere length testing | Weak — high variability, poor individual-level predictive value | No |
| Microbiome sequencing | Weak-to-emerging — population-level insights don't translate to individual guidance yet | No |

The VO2 max entry deserves emphasis. A 2022 study by Mandsager et al. in *JAMA Network Open*, following 122,007 patients over a median of 8.4 years, found that low cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with a higher risk of all-cause mortality than any other modifiable risk factor measured — including smoking, hypertension, and diabetes. A clinic that doesn't assess VO2 max (via maximal treadmill or cycle ergometer test, not an estimate) is leaving the most predictive number on the table.

Full-body MRI screening is the test that generates the most controversy. It sounds like pure upside — a whole-body scan that might catch something early. In practice, a 2023 review by Koh et al. in *Radiology* found that commercially available whole-body MRI protocols generate incidental findings in roughly 30–40% of asymptomatic adults, the overwhelming majority of which are clinically insignificant but which trigger downstream imaging, biopsies, and anxiety. Whether this is acceptable depends heavily on how well a clinic prepares you for that reality and how rigorously they apply evidence-based follow-up protocols. Without that, it's a revenue driver dressed as prevention.

## The Mechanism: Why Early Signal Matters So Much

The logic behind longevity medicine is straightforward but easy to underestimate. The major killers — cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer's — develop across decades. The pathological process that produces a heart attack at 62 typically begins in earnest in the late 30s and 40s. By the time conventional medicine notices it, you've lost much of the window for meaningful intervention.

The underlying biology involves the slow accumulation of arterial plaque (driven by ApoB-containing particles and inflammation), progressive insulin resistance that degrades vascular and neurological function, and the gradual loss of muscle mass and aerobic capacity that dramatically narrows physiological reserve as you age. What makes early detection powerful is that many of these processes are genuinely reversible in their early stages — particularly metabolic dysfunction, where lifestyle intervention can normalize insulin sensitivity — and manageable with low-intervention approaches before they require aggressive pharmacological or surgical treatment.

## Red Flags: Wellness Theatre in a Lab Coat

Not everything calling itself longevity medicine is practicing medicine. Here is what to watch for before you hand over a credit card.

**Red flags to watch for:**

- **Mandatory package pricing** that bundles 30+ tests regardless of your age, risk profile, or history. Legitimate preventive medicine is individualized. A 34-year-old woman with no family history of cardiovascular disease does not need the same panel as a 52-year-old man with a father who died of a heart attack at 57.
- **Heavy emphasis on supplements**, particularly proprietary "longevity stacks" sold in-house. As of 2024, no supplement has demonstrated mortality benefit in adequately powered randomized controlled trials in healthy populations. Clinics that sell you diagnostics and then sell you their supplements have a conflict of interest that should be named explicitly.
- **No licensed physician in a clinical oversight role.** A clinic staffed entirely by nurse practitioners, health coaches, or "wellness consultants" — with no physician available to interpret results and manage follow-up — is not practicing medicine, regardless of how many CEOs are on the website.
- **Biological age tests as a headline service.** Epigenetic clocks like GrimAge and DunedinPACE are genuinely interesting research tools. They are not yet validated for individual clinical decision-making. A clinic that sells you a "biological age" as a primary outcome, without contextualizing the measurement's limitations, is prioritizing your emotional engagement over your clinical outcomes.
- **No conversation about false positives and downstream consequences** before you consent to imaging. This is a basic informed consent issue. If a clinic books your full-body MRI without explaining the incidentaloma problem, that's a sign of either poor clinical standards or deliberate omission.

## How to Evaluate a Clinic Before You Commit

Before your first appointment — or before you pay a deposit — ask these specific questions:

- Who is the clinical lead, and what are their credentials? (Board-certified in internal medicine, cardiology, or a recognized preventive medicine specialty is a meaningful signal.)
- Will my results be reviewed by a physician, and will I have time to ask questions about their clinical significance?
- Can I see a sample protocol? Is it individualized to patient history and risk profile, or is it the same panel for everyone?
- What is your process if imaging generates an incidental finding?
- Do you sell supplements, and do you have a financial interest in my buying them?
- What is your follow-up model — are you available between annual appointments if my results raise questions?

A legitimate clinic will welcome every one of those questions. One that becomes evasive or defensive is telling you something.

## Practical Protocol: Getting Real Value

If you're going to engage with longevity medicine, here's a defensible starting framework for a healthy adult in their 40s or 50s with no major diagnosed conditions:

**Year one — establish your baseline:**
- ApoB, Lp(a), full lipid panel, hsCRP
- Fasting insulin and glucose (HOMA-IR)
- HbA1c, comprehensive metabolic panel, CBC
- DEXA body composition
- VO2 max test (formal, not estimated)
- Blood pressure measured correctly (seated, rested, multiple readings)
- For men over 50, a PSA conversation with your physician. For women, age-appropriate cancer screening review.

**Ongoing:**
- VO2 max testing annually — it is the number most responsive to training and most predictive of outcomes
- Metabolic markers every 6–12 months if any are abnormal
- Repeat DEXA every 1–2 years to track muscle mass, especially if you've changed your training

The goal is a small number of high-signal tests, reviewed by someone qualified to interpret them, linked to interventions you're actually going to make. More data is not the same as better health. What matters is whether the information changes what you do — and whether what you do changes the biology.
