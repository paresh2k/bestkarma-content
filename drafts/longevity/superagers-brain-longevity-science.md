Most people treat cognitive decline as inevitable — a slow, predictable erosion that comes with gray hair and reading glasses. But a small group of octogenarians is quietly upending that assumption. They're in their 80s and 90s, and when researchers at Northwestern University put them through rigorous memory tests, they scored as well as healthy adults thirty years younger. Not approximately as well. Not "pretty good for their age." Equivalently. These people — now called **SuperAgers** — don't just have sharper minds than their peers. They appear to have fundamentally different brains.

Understanding why isn't just an academic exercise. If some people can sustain 50-year-old cognitive performance into their ninth decade, the question stops being "is decline inevitable?" and becomes "what are they doing that we're not?"

---

## What Makes Someone a SuperAger

The term was coined and the cohort defined by **Dr. Emily Rogalski** and her colleagues at Northwestern University's Mesulam Center for Cognitive Neurology and Alzheimer's Disease. To qualify as a SuperAger, participants must be 80 or older and perform at or above normative levels for **50-to-65-year-olds** on at least one standardized memory test — specifically, the Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test (RAVLT), which measures the ability to encode and recall word lists. Critically, they must also show no performance below age-average on other cognitive domains. This is a high bar. Most people who try to enroll in the study don't make the cut.

The project, now running for over a decade, has enrolled more than 100 SuperAgers alongside age-matched control groups: **cognitively average 80-year-olds** and **cognitively average 50-to-65-year-olds**. This three-way comparison is what makes the research design so powerful. It lets researchers ask not just "what's different about SuperAgers compared to old people?" but "what makes them look like middle-aged brains?"

---

## What Brain Imaging Reveals

The most striking findings come from neuroimaging, and they are not subtle.

A **2012 study by Rogalski et al. in the Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society** compared cortical thickness in SuperAgers versus cognitively average older adults and middle-aged adults. The cortex — the brain's outermost layer, responsible for memory, attention, language, and executive function — normally thins with age at a measurable rate. The average 80-year-old shows significant cortical thinning compared to a 50-year-old. SuperAgers showed cortical thickness statistically indistinguishable from the middle-aged group in key regions, including the **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** and **medial prefrontal cortex**. These aren't minor differences in measurement noise. The average older adults' cortex was meaningfully, visibly thinner.

Longitudinal follow-up made this even more remarkable. **A 2017 paper by Sun et al. in JAMA Internal Medicine** tracked cortical thickness over 18 months in SuperAgers versus average older adults. The average group lost cortical volume at a rate roughly three times faster than SuperAgers. The SuperAger cortex wasn't just thicker at baseline — it was more resistant to the ongoing attrition that defines typical aging. They weren't starting from a higher point and falling at the same rate. The rate of decline itself was different.

Beyond thickness, researchers found that SuperAgers also have **larger-than-average neurons** in a specific brain region: the entorhinal cortex, a critical hub for memory consolidation and the first area typically ravaged by Alzheimer's disease. A **2018 study by Gefen et al. in the Journal of Neuroscience** performed post-mortem analysis of SuperAger brains donated to science and found that entorhinal neurons were not only more numerous but significantly larger than in typical older adults — and more comparable in size to those of middle-aged donors. Larger neurons, in this context, generally correlate with greater functional reserve and more robust synaptic connectivity.

## The Role of Von Economo Neurons

Perhaps the most intriguing biological signature of SuperAgers involves a rare class of cells called **von Economo neurons (VENs)** — large, spindle-shaped neurons found almost exclusively in humans (and a few other highly social mammals, including great apes and elephants). VENs are concentrated in the anterior cingulate cortex and the frontoinsular cortex, regions involved in social awareness, emotional processing, self-regulation, and — critically for SuperAgers — certain aspects of memory and attentional control.

The **2018 Gefen et al. study in the Journal of Neuroscience** found that SuperAgers had significantly higher counts of von Economo neurons in the ACC compared to cognitively average older adults. This is striking for several reasons. First, VENs are particularly vulnerable in early Alzheimer's disease — they're among the first cells to die. Second, VENs appear to play a role in rapid, intuitive social and emotional processing — the kind of fast, integrated judgment that experienced people seem to have. Third, their relative abundance in SuperAgers suggests that whatever is protecting these brains is doing so at the level of specific, fragile cell populations, not just general structural preservation.

We don't yet know why some people retain more VENs. It could reflect genetic factors, a lifetime of specific behaviors, reduced neuroinflammation, or — most likely — some combination. This is an area of active investigation, and the honest answer is that the mechanism isn't fully established.

## What SuperAgers Actually Do Differently

Brain scans reveal the *what*. The harder question is the *why* — specifically, the behavioral and lifestyle factors that distinguish SuperAgers from their peers.

Rogalski's team has published survey and interview data from the cohort alongside the neuroimaging. A **2019 paper by Cook et al. in the Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society** examined psychosocial factors in the SuperAger cohort and found several consistent themes. SuperAgers reported **stronger social relationships and more satisfying social engagement** than average older adults. They described their lives as having more purpose and were less likely to report feelings of loneliness. This aligns with a substantial body of external research — including the 85-year **Harvard Study of Adult Development** — linking quality social connection to cognitive preservation and reduced dementia risk.

SuperAgers also reported **higher levels of physical activity** than the average older adult cohort. Importantly, this wasn't elite athleticism — it was consistent, moderate-to-vigorous activity sustained over decades. This resonates with what we know about exercise and brain health: aerobic activity upregulates **BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor)**, promotes neurogenesis in the hippocampus, and reduces systemic inflammation — all mechanisms that would support the kind of structural preservation we see in SuperAger imaging.

What's notable about SuperAgers is not just that they do things that are good for them. It's that they appear to embrace **productive discomfort**. Rogalski has noted in interviews and commentary that many SuperAgers actively pursue cognitively demanding activities — learning new instruments, engaging in complex social negotiations, taking on new creative challenges — rather than retreating into comfortable routine. Neuroscientifically, this makes sense: cognitive challenge drives synaptic strengthening and may stimulate reserve capacity in ways that passive engagement doesn't.

## Nuance and What We Don't Yet Know

The SuperAger research is genuinely exciting, but it carries real limitations worth naming. The cohort is **self-selected and relatively small** — over 100 participants is meaningful for a specialized study, but not large enough to draw population-level conclusions with certainty. The group skews toward higher education and socioeconomic status, which are themselves associated with cognitive reserve. It's possible that some SuperAgers' advantage is built in from birth — genetic factors influencing neural development, inflammation, or cell maintenance — rather than primarily driven by lifestyle choices in adulthood.

The **causal arrows are also unclear**. Do SuperAgers have better social lives because their sharp minds make them better companions, or do rich social lives help maintain sharp minds? Probably both, in a reinforcing loop — but the directionality matters enormously for intervention. Longitudinal work is continuing, and biomarker studies (including amyloid and tau imaging) are beginning to shed light on whether SuperAgers are also resistant to Alzheimer's pathology, or whether they're compensating for it through reserve. Early data suggests the former, but this is not yet settled.

## The Practical Protocol

You are probably not 80. But the behaviors associated with SuperAger status appear to compound over decades, which means the time to start is now.

### What the evidence supports:

| Domain | Specific target | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Aerobic exercise** | 150+ min/week moderate or 75 min vigorous; sustained over years | BDNF upregulation, hippocampal neurogenesis, reduced neuroinflammation |
| **Social engagement** | High-quality relationships, not just contact frequency; seek meaningful connection | VEN preservation, stress regulation, purpose-driven cognition |
| **Cognitive challenge** | Learn something genuinely hard — a language, instrument, or complex craft | Synaptic strengthening, reserve capacity, neural plasticity |
| **Purpose and meaning** | Identify and actively pursue long-term goals beyond daily routine | ACC engagement, emotional regulation, reduced cortisol burden |
| **Sleep** | 7–9 hours; prioritize slow-wave sleep for memory consolidation | Glymphatic clearance of amyloid and tau |

The SuperAger research doesn't promise immortality of the mind. What it does offer is something arguably more useful: evidence that the ceiling for cognitive aging is far higher than most of us assume, and a set of consistent patterns in the people who reach it. The brain you have at 80 is not a fixed destiny. It is, in large part, the accumulated consequence of choices made across a lifetime — starting with the ones you make today.
