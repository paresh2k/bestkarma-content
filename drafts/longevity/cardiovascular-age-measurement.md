Two people walk into a clinic, both 52 years old. One has the coronary arteries of a 38-year-old. The other has the arteries of a 71-year-old. Their birthdays are the same. Their biological futures are not. The gap between chronological age and cardiovascular age is arguably the most consequential number in preventive medicine — and for most people, it goes completely unmeasured.

Cardiovascular disease kills more people globally than any other cause, but the usual screenings — total cholesterol, resting blood pressure, a standard ECG — are blunt instruments. They catch disease that has already declared itself. The more interesting question is: how fast is your cardiovascular system aging right now, and what can you actually do to slow it down?

---

## The Five Clocks Inside Your Cardiovascular System

Cardiovascular age isn't a single number. It's a composite picture assembled from several biological signals, each measuring a different dimension of how your heart and vasculature are holding up.

### Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) Score

The CAC score is the closest thing medicine has to a direct audit of plaque burden in the coronary arteries. A low-radiation CT scan quantifies calcified atherosclerotic plaque using the **Agatston score** — and the results are startlingly predictive.

The **MESA study (Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis)**, which followed 6,814 adults free of cardiovascular disease across a median of 10+ years, established that a CAC score of zero predicts a 10-year risk of major cardiovascular events below 1% — even in individuals with conventional risk factors like mild hypertension or elevated LDL. Conversely, a CAC score above 300 is associated with a hazard ratio of roughly 7.7 for cardiovascular events compared to a CAC of zero (Detrano et al., *NEJM*, 2008). A 2022 analysis of the MESA cohort published in *JACC* confirmed that CAC reclassifies risk meaningfully in roughly 25% of patients deemed "intermediate risk" by standard calculators.

**What to know about your score:**
| CAC Score | Interpretation | Approximate 10-Year CV Risk |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | No detectable plaque | <1% (very low) |
| 1–99 | Mild plaque burden | Low to moderate |
| 100–299 | Moderate burden | Moderate to high |
| 300+ | Extensive plaque | High |

The limitation: CAC only captures *calcified* plaque. Soft, non-calcified plaque — which may be more prone to rupture — is invisible to this scan. A CAC of zero in a 35-year-old with a terrible lifestyle is not a green light; it's a narrow window before the calcification clock starts running.

### Pulse Wave Velocity (PWV): The Arterial Stiffness Clock

If CAC measures what's already accumulated, **pulse wave velocity** measures how well your arteries are aging functionally. PWV quantifies how fast a pressure wave travels from the heart to peripheral vessels — specifically from the carotid to the femoral artery. Stiff arteries conduct pressure faster. That stiffness forces the heart to work harder and is independently predictive of mortality.

A landmark meta-analysis by **Ben-Shlomo et al. (European Heart Journal, 2014)** pooled data from 17,635 individuals across 12 studies and found that each 1 m/s increase in aortic PWV was associated with a 14% increase in cardiovascular events and a 15% increase in all-cause mortality. The reference range for aortic PWV in healthy adults under 50 is roughly 6–9 m/s; values above 10 m/s in that age group signal meaningful arterial aging. PWV rises naturally with age, but the slope of that rise varies enormously — and is modifiable.

### High-Sensitivity Troponin (hs-cTnT): Subclinical Cardiac Stress

High-sensitivity troponin T (hs-cTnT) was originally a marker of acute heart attack. At low, "normal-range" concentrations, it turns out to be something else entirely: a signal of ongoing, subclinical myocardial stress. Even values well below the clinical threshold for myocardial injury carry prognostic weight.

**Everett et al. (Circulation, 2015)** analyzed hs-cTnT in over 9,000 adults from the ARIC study and found that participants with hs-cTnT in the top quartile of the normal range had a **2.3-fold higher risk** of incident heart failure and a significantly elevated risk of coronary events over 10 years, compared to the lowest quartile — independent of traditional risk factors. Think of chronically elevated hs-cTnT as your heart sending a quiet distress signal at a level most clinicians aren't listening for.

### Heart Rate Variability (HRV): The Autonomic Window

**Heart rate variability** — the beat-to-beat variation in the intervals between heartbeats — is a proxy for autonomic nervous system function, specifically the balance between sympathetic and parasympathetic tone. A higher HRV generally indicates a healthier, more adaptable cardiovascular system. Lower HRV is associated with increased all-cause mortality, poorer recovery from cardiac events, and accelerated cardiovascular aging.

A major prospective study by **Tsuji et al. (Circulation, 1996)** in the Framingham Heart Study cohort found that a one-standard-deviation decrease in HRV predicted a 32% increase in all-cause mortality. HRV declines with age but is also exquisitely sensitive to modifiable factors: sleep quality, chronic stress, alcohol consumption, and aerobic fitness. It's one of the few cardiovascular biomarkers you can shift meaningfully within weeks.

### VO₂ Max: The Single Strongest Predictor

Of all the metrics discussed here, **VO₂ max** — the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during maximal exertion — is arguably the most powerful predictor of long-term cardiovascular and all-cause mortality.

**Mandsager et al. (JAMA Network Open, 2018)** analyzed 122,007 patients who underwent exercise treadmill testing at Cleveland Clinic and found that being in the bottom quartile of cardiorespiratory fitness (low VO₂ max) carried a **hazard ratio of 5.04** for all-cause mortality compared to the top performers — a risk burden exceeding smoking, hypertension, diabetes, and end-stage renal disease. Each metabolic equivalent (MET) improvement in fitness was associated with an approximately 13% reduction in mortality risk. No drug has that kind of effect size.

## Why Cardiovascular Systems Age at Different Rates

The divergence between biological and chronological cardiovascular age begins at the cellular level. Arterial stiffness accumulates when **vascular smooth muscle cells** increasingly cross-link with glycated collagen — a process accelerated by high glucose, chronic inflammation, and oxidative stress. Endothelial dysfunction, the failure of blood vessel walls to properly dilate and regulate blood flow, is the earliest structural change and is driven largely by reductions in nitric oxide bioavailability.

At the cardiac level, the autonomic nervous system gradually loses its dynamic range — the "gas and brake" balance between sympathetic drive and parasympathetic recovery degrades. This is reflected in falling HRV. Chronic low-grade inflammation, measured by markers like IL-6 and hsCRP, accelerates this process by promoting both plaque instability and myocardial fibrosis.

What's striking about cardiovascular aging is how **non-linear** the lifestyle inputs are. The damage from a decade of poor sleep, chronic stress, and physical inactivity doesn't show up on a standard lipid panel — it shows up in arterial stiffness, in the quiet elevation of hs-cTnT, in a VO₂ max that's dropped into the bottom quartile. These clocks don't wait for symptoms.

## What Actually Moves the Numbers

The good news is that several of these measures are genuinely reversible — not just stabilizable, but improvable.

**For VO₂ max and HRV,** the intervention with the strongest evidence is **Zone 2 aerobic training** combined with **VO₂ max intervals**. A 2023 review in *Cell Metabolism* by Ioannou et al. confirmed that high-intensity interval training (4×4-minute intervals at near-maximal effort, 3x per week) improved VO₂ max by 5–10% in previously sedentary middle-aged adults within 8–12 weeks. Zone 2 work — sustained effort at 60–70% of max heart rate — improves mitochondrial density and is the primary driver of long-term aerobic base. HRV responds most rapidly to improved sleep consistency and reductions in alcohol; gains of 10–20 ms in resting RMSSD (a standard HRV metric) are achievable within 4–6 weeks.

**For arterial stiffness (PWV),** aerobic exercise is again the primary lever. A meta-analysis by **Ashor et al. (JACC, 2014)** of 10 RCTs found that aerobic training reduced aortic PWV by approximately 1 m/s on average — a clinically meaningful reduction. Sodium restriction (to below 2,000 mg/day) and achieving systolic blood pressure below 120 mmHg via lifestyle or medication also independently reduce PWV. Emerging evidence, primarily from small RCTs, suggests that **dietary nitrates** (found in beetroot juice and leafy greens) may improve endothelial function, though this evidence remains preliminary.

**For CAC,** the honest answer is that you cannot meaningfully reverse existing calcified plaque. What you can do is stop it from progressing — and evidence from the MESA cohort suggests that achieving a CAC score that doesn't progress over 5 years is associated with very low event rates, regardless of baseline score. The primary tools are statin therapy (which stabilizes plaque rather than removing it), aggressive blood pressure control, and the elimination of smoking.

## Your Practical Protocol

*This article is for informational purposes. Consult a qualified healthcare provider before making changes to your health routine.*

If you've never measured your cardiovascular age, here's where to start:

- **Request a CAC scan** if you're 40–75 with any conventional risk factors (hypertension, dyslipidemia, family history, smoking history, diabetes). Cost is often $100–$200 out of pocket where not covered. This is a one-time test, not annual.
- **Estimate VO₂ max** using a validated non-exercise prediction tool (e.g., the Rockport Walk Test) or a wearable device with optical HR sensing, then get a formal test via a metabolic cart or graded exercise test if you want precision.
- **Measure HRV** using a validated consumer device (Polar H10, Garmin, Whoop, or Oura Ring) over a consistent 4-week baseline — morning HRV on waking is the most reproducible measurement.
- **Training protocol:** Aim for 150–180 minutes per week of Zone 2 cardio plus 1–2 sessions of high-intensity intervals (4 x 4 minutes at near-maximal effort with 4-minute recovery). This is the minimum effective dose for measurably improving VO₂ max in adults.
- **Ask your physician** about hs-cTnT and hsCRP if you're building a comprehensive cardiovascular risk picture — these are not standard panels but are increasingly available.

The most important thing to understand is that the number on your birth certificate is fixed. The number representing your cardiovascular age is not.
