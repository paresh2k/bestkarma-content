Most longevity researchers study aging. Valter Longo is trying to reverse it. The USC gerontologist and director of the Longevity Institute has spent three decades arguing that the body already knows how to repair itself — it just needs the right metabolic signal to start. That signal, his research suggests, is periodic near-starvation. Not chronic caloric restriction, which tends to make people miserable and immunocompromised, but brief, strategic drops in energy intake that trick the body into a cellular housecleaning mode. The clinical data behind this idea is more compelling than most longevity nutrition research — and more complicated.

---

## The Framework: Two Levers, Not One

Longo's approach has two distinct components that are often conflated. The first is a **baseline diet** — what he calls the Longevity Diet — built around a mostly plant-based, pescatarian eating pattern with deliberately low protein intake. The second is a periodic **fasting-mimicking diet (FMD)**: five consecutive days of reduced calories, designed to capture the benefits of prolonged fasting without requiring complete food abstinence. Understanding them separately matters, because the evidence for each is different in quality and scope.

The baseline diet draws on epidemiological comparisons across long-lived populations — the "Blue Zones" work popularized by Dan Buettner and supported by Longo's own analysis of centenarian dietary patterns. In a 2022 review in *Cell*, Longo and colleagues synthesized data from longevity cohorts and intervention studies to propose a target macronutrient framework: **protein at roughly 0.7–0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight** for adults under 65, skewed heavily toward legumes and fish rather than red meat; carbohydrates from whole, complex sources; and fats predominantly from olive oil and nuts. After 65, the protein recommendation inverts — Longo explicitly advocates for higher protein intake in older adults to counter sarcopenia and frailty, a nuance often lost in popular summaries of his work.

The logic for low protein in midlife centers on **IGF-1 suppression**. In a landmark 2014 study in *Cell Metabolism*, Levine, Longo, and colleagues analyzed data from 6,381 adults in the NHANES cohort and found that adults aged 50–65 who consumed high protein (≥20% of calories) had a **73% increase in all-cause mortality** and a fourfold increase in cancer mortality compared to low-protein consumers over an 18-year follow-up. Critically, this association disappeared — and even reversed — in adults over 65. Correlational? Yes. Confounded? Possibly. But Longo's mouse studies showing that protein restriction reduces IGF-1, slows tumor growth, and extends lifespan provide a plausible mechanism that makes the epidemiology harder to dismiss.

## The Fasting-Mimicking Diet: What the Trials Show

The FMD protocol is specific: **five consecutive days per month**, consuming roughly **800–1,100 kcal daily**, with a macro composition designed to keep insulin and IGF-1 suppressed while providing enough micronutrients to prevent deficiency. Days 1 through 5 follow a gradient — slightly higher calories on day one (~1,100 kcal), tapering down for days two through five (~800 kcal), with a macronutrient ratio approximately 9–10% protein, 44–56% fat, 34–47% carbohydrate.

The first major human RCT on this protocol was published by Brandhorst, Longo, and colleagues in *Science Translational Medicine* in 2017. In 100 generally healthy adults, three monthly FMD cycles produced significant reductions in body weight, trunk fat, blood pressure, IGF-1, glucose, and a regenerative marker called **IGF-binding protein 1 (IGFBP-1)** — all without adverse effects on lean mass. Importantly, the effect was most pronounced in participants who started in the at-risk range for metabolic markers, while those with already-normal values showed minimal change. This dose-response pattern — benefits concentrated in those with the most room for improvement — is biologically plausible and recurs in subsequent trials.

In metabolic disease, a 2024 trial published in *Nature Medicine* by Wei and colleagues (n=101, 3-month follow-up) randomized patients with type 2 diabetes to FMD cycles versus a control diet. The FMD group showed a **remission rate of approximately 65%** in terms of HbA1c returning to prediabetic or normal range, compared to around 40% in controls. Fasting glucose and insulin resistance markers improved substantially. These are striking numbers for a dietary intervention in established type 2 diabetes — though the follow-up period was short and larger trials are needed before this should influence clinical practice.

In oncology, the case is intriguing but must be read carefully. A 2020 pilot trial in *Nature Communications* by de Groot, Longo, and colleagues enrolled 129 women with early-stage HER2-negative breast cancer undergoing chemotherapy. Patients randomized to FMD before and during chemotherapy cycles showed improved tumor response rates on imaging and, in subgroup analysis, higher rates of pathological complete response. The hypothesis is that FMD creates a **differential stress resistance** effect — cancer cells, which have lost the ability to downregulate growth pathways, can't adapt to low-nutrient conditions, while healthy cells enter a protective dormancy. This is mechanistically elegant and has solid support in animal models. But the human trial was a pilot. Sample sizes in oncology subgroups were small, and no one should be modifying cancer treatment without oncologist involvement on the basis of a pilot.

The most recent large-scale evidence comes from a 2022 study in *Cell Reports Medicine* by Choi and colleagues, examining FMD effects in multiple sclerosis. In a randomized crossover trial of 60 patients, three monthly FMD cycles significantly reduced markers of inflammation and fatigue, and produced modest but measurable improvements on patient-reported outcomes. The mechanistic story here involves regulatory T-cell dynamics and gut microbiome changes — both of which are active research areas in MS.

## The Biology: Why Near-Starvation Triggers Repair

The mechanism Longo emphasizes most is **autophagy** — literally "self-eating." When nutrient sensors like mTOR and AMPK detect an energy deficit, cells begin breaking down damaged organelles, misfolded proteins, and dysfunctional mitochondria, recycling their components. This cellular quality control system declines with age and is implicated in the accumulation of senescent cells, neurodegeneration, and cancer initiation.

Prolonged fasting activates autophagy more potently than caloric restriction alone, but it also activates another pathway: **hematopoietic stem cell mobilization**. In Cheng and colleagues' 2014 mouse and human study in *Cell Stem Cell*, prolonged fasting cycles caused significant reduction and subsequent rebound in white blood cell counts — suggesting that fasting depletes old immune cells and triggers regeneration from stem cell reserves when feeding resumes. Whether this "immune reset" translates meaningfully in otherwise healthy humans is still being worked out, but it provides a compelling framework for why the FMD effect appears to go beyond simple caloric restriction.

IGF-1 suppression is the third pillar. IGF-1 is a growth-promoting hormone that, in the context of chronic elevation, is associated with accelerated cellular aging, higher cancer risk, and reduced stress resistance in multiple model organisms. Low protein intake suppresses IGF-1 independently of caloric intake — which is why Longo argues that the type of dietary restriction matters as much as the amount.

## Honest Limitations

The evidence is promising, not conclusive. Most FMD human trials are small (under 200 participants), short in follow-up (3–6 months), and several originate from Longo's own lab or close collaborators — a conflict of interest that doesn't invalidate the findings but warrants independent replication. The NHANES protein-mortality finding is observational and cannot establish causation; protein intake may be a proxy for other dietary patterns or socioeconomic factors.

ProLon — the commercial FMD kit sold by L-Nutra, a company Longo co-founded — has created legitimate questions about financial incentives in the research program. Longo has stated he donates his salary from L-Nutra to the nonprofit Create Cures Foundation, but the relationship between the company and the science deserves scrutiny. The diet can also be approximated with whole foods at far lower cost; ProLon is not clinically required.

FMD is also not universally appropriate. People with a history of eating disorders, underweight individuals, pregnant or breastfeeding women, and those with certain metabolic conditions should not attempt it without medical oversight.

## Practical Protocol

If you're considering integrating this framework, here is what the evidence supports:

**Baseline diet (daily):**
- Prioritize legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas) as primary protein source
- Include fish 2–3 times per week; minimize red and processed meat
- Target protein at ~0.7–0.8 g/kg body weight if under 65; increase to ~1.0–1.2 g/kg if over 65
- Use extra-virgin olive oil as primary fat source (Longo recommends ~3 tablespoons daily)
- Limit added sugars and refined carbohydrates; emphasize whole grains and vegetables

**Fasting-mimicking cycles (periodic):**
- 5 consecutive days per month (or every 2–3 months, depending on health goals and baseline metabolic status)
- Caloric target: ~1,100 kcal day one, ~800 kcal days two through five
- Macro composition: low protein (~10% of calories), moderate complex carbohydrates, higher healthy fat
- Resume normal eating on day six; no extended post-fast restriction needed
- Start conservatively — one cycle — and assess tolerability before committing to monthly repetition

The evidence for benefit is strongest in people with elevated metabolic risk markers (high fasting glucose, elevated IGF-1, excess trunk fat). If your biomarkers are already optimal, the marginal benefit is less clear.
