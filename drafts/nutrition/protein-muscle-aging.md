The US recommended daily allowance for protein is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight. For a 70-kilogram adult, that's 56 grams per day — a number set by the Institute of Medicine to prevent deficiency in a sedentary adult. It is explicitly not an optimal intake for health. It is a floor.

For adults over 40, particularly those trying to maintain muscle mass, exercise capacity, and metabolic health into their later decades, the RDA for protein is inadequate. The gap between the minimum-to-not-be-deficient and the optimum-for-healthy-aging is large, consequential, and not well communicated.

## What Sarcopenia Is — and Why It Matters

Sarcopenia is the progressive loss of skeletal muscle mass and function with aging. It is not a disease in the traditional sense — it is a biological process that begins in most people around age 30, proceeds at 3-8% of muscle mass per decade through midlife, and accelerates sharply after 60. By age 80, the average person has lost 30-40% of the muscle mass they had at peak.

This loss matters far beyond aesthetics or athletic performance.

Skeletal muscle is the primary site of glucose uptake in the body. Adequate muscle mass is essential for insulin sensitivity and metabolic health. As muscle declines, glucose disposal capacity falls, driving the insulin resistance that underlies type 2 diabetes.

Muscle is the body's primary protein reservoir — the tissue the body draws on during illness, injury, or starvation to maintain organ function and immune response. People with low muscle mass have dramatically worse surgical outcomes, slower recovery from illness, and higher complication rates from nearly every medical intervention.

Falls and fractures — the leading cause of injury-related death in adults over 65 — are substantially driven by sarcopenia. Muscle strength and mass predict balance, stability, and fall risk. Grip strength (a proxy for systemic muscle health) is a more powerful predictor of cardiovascular mortality than blood pressure in large population studies.

Finally, muscle mass is metabolically expensive tissue — it consumes energy at rest. Losing muscle mass lowers resting metabolic rate, creating the conditions for gradual weight gain and metabolic deterioration even without changes in eating habits.

## The Problem With "Anabolic Resistance"

In younger adults, consuming protein stimulates muscle protein synthesis (MPS) — the process by which amino acids are assembled into new muscle tissue. In older adults, this response is blunted. A given amount of protein stimulates less MPS in a 65-year-old than in a 25-year-old. This phenomenon is called anabolic resistance, and it is the fundamental reason protein needs increase with age.

The mechanism involves reduced sensitivity to leucine — the amino acid that functions as the primary trigger for MPS — and reduced activity of the mTOR signaling pathway in older muscle tissue. The system becomes less responsive to the same signal. The solution is either more signal (higher protein intake per meal) or a more potent signal (resistance exercise dramatically restores MPS sensitivity in aging muscle).

This is why the research consensus for older adults converges on meaningfully higher protein intake than the RDA — and why resistance training and protein intake are synergistic interventions for muscle preservation, not alternatives.

## What You Actually Need

The PROT-AGE consensus statement — developed by an international group of geriatric nutrition researchers — provides the most evidence-based guidance:

- **Adults 50-65:** 1.0-1.2g protein per kilogram of body weight per day
- **Adults 65+:** 1.2-1.6g per kilogram per day
- **Adults 65+ with acute illness, active disease, or sarcopenia:** up to 2.0g per kilogram per day

For a 70-kilogram adult over 65, that translates to 84-112 grams of protein per day — considerably more than the 56g RDA suggests, and more than most people in this age group actually eat.

For active adults under 65 engaged in regular resistance training, 1.4-2.0g/kg/day is supported by the exercise science literature as the range that optimally supports muscle adaptation and maintenance.

## The Distribution Question

Total daily protein matters. How that protein is distributed across meals may matter equally.

Each meal containing protein stimulates a discrete bout of muscle protein synthesis. For MPS to be fully activated, a meal needs to deliver approximately 2.5-3 grams of leucine — the anabolic trigger amino acid. The protein amount required to reach this leucine threshold varies by protein source, but approximately 25-40 grams of protein per meal from most complete sources delivers the necessary leucine.

The problem with typical eating patterns: many people eat protein asymmetrically — a small amount at breakfast (eggs or yogurt), a moderate amount at lunch, and the majority at dinner (a large portion of meat). This concentrates most protein in one meal, which cannot stimulate multiple bouts of MPS throughout the day.

Distributing protein more evenly — roughly 25-40 grams per meal across three meals — consistently outperforms the same total protein eaten skewed toward dinner in studies measuring 24-hour MPS rates. The body cannot "save" the protein from a large dinner meal to use for morning MPS; each meal represents an independent anabolic opportunity.

A practical framework by age:

| Age Group | Target per Meal | Daily Total (70kg person) |
|---|---|---|
| 30–50, active | 25–30g | 1.2–1.4g/kg (~84–98g) |
| 50–65 | 30–35g | 1.4–1.6g/kg (~98–112g) |
| 65+, especially if not exercising | 35–40g | 1.6g/kg+ (~112g+) |

## Protein Sources: Animal, Plant, and the Nuances

**Animal protein** (meat, fish, poultry, eggs, dairy) contains complete amino acid profiles with high leucine content and high digestibility. Per gram of protein, animal sources more efficiently stimulate MPS. For older adults with anabolic resistance, where leucine threshold is harder to reach, animal protein's higher leucine density is a meaningful advantage.

**Plant protein** (legumes, tofu, tempeh, edamame, whole grains, nuts) can absolutely meet protein needs for MPS, but requires more attention. Most plant proteins are lower in leucine than animal sources, meaning more total protein is needed to hit the 2.5-3g leucine threshold. Soy protein (tofu, edamame, tempeh) is closest to animal protein in leucine content and MPS-stimulating capacity. Combining plant sources — legumes plus whole grains, for example — creates more complete amino acid profiles.

A plant-based diet that meets protein needs is entirely achievable for muscle health; it requires planning and typically higher total protein intake to compensate for lower leucine density and digestibility.

**Protein supplements** — whey, casein, pea protein, soy protein — stimulate MPS identically to whole food protein when matched for protein and leucine content. They are not superior to food; they are a convenience option when meeting protein targets through whole food is difficult. Whey protein has the highest leucine content of common supplements and the fastest absorption rate, making it effective for post-workout recovery.

## The "High Protein Is Bad for Kidneys" Myth

The concern about high protein damaging healthy kidneys originates from observations in patients with pre-existing chronic kidney disease (CKD), where reduced protein intake is genuinely indicated to slow progression. This finding was inappropriately generalized to healthy adults.

Multiple large prospective cohort studies have followed healthy adults consuming high-protein diets (1.5-2.5g/kg/day) without finding adverse kidney function changes. The kidneys adapt to higher protein intake in healthy individuals by increasing filtration rate — this is a functional response, not a pathological one. The consensus in sports medicine and geriatric nutrition is unambiguous: high protein intake does not damage healthy kidneys.

The exception: anyone with existing kidney disease should follow their physician's protein guidance, which is a specific clinical situation distinct from general population recommendations.

## Practical Implementation

**Breakfast protein is the most important meal to fix.** Most people eat their lowest-protein meal in the morning — toast, cereal, fruit, coffee. Replacing or augmenting this with 25-35g of protein (3-4 eggs, Greek yogurt with nuts, cottage cheese, a protein shake alongside other food) activates morning MPS and reduces hunger and caloric intake throughout the day.

**Audit your meals, not just your total.** If your daily protein is adequate but concentrated at dinner, you're leaving morning and afternoon MPS opportunities unused. Spread it.

**Resistance training amplifies everything.** Exercise restores MPS sensitivity in aging muscle — making protein work harder than it would in a sedentary person. Protein intake and resistance training together are substantially more effective than either alone for preserving muscle mass with age.

**Protein first, then fill the plate.** Planning meals with protein as the anchor — and adding vegetables, whole grains, and healthy fats around it — is more reliable than trying to add protein to a carbohydrate-centric meal plan.

Muscle is not a cosmetic concern. It is the metabolic and structural foundation of functional aging. Protecting it with adequate protein is one of the clearest, most evidence-supported nutritional investments available.
