Vitamin D is called a vitamin, but it functions as a hormone. It is synthesized in the skin from cholesterol when exposed to ultraviolet B radiation from sunlight, transported to the liver for a first modification, then to the kidneys for conversion to its active hormonal form — calcitriol. Vitamin D receptors are present in virtually every tissue in the body: bone, muscle, brain, immune cells, pancreatic beta cells, and more.

The roles of vitamin D extend far beyond the calcium absorption function it's classically taught for. And approximately 1 billion people worldwide — including 40% of Americans — are deficient in it.

## Why Deficiency Is So Common

The primary source of vitamin D is synthesis in the skin from UVB radiation. This source is severely limited for most people in most modern circumstances.

**Latitude.** UVB radiation at wavelengths necessary for vitamin D synthesis (290-315nm) reaches the earth's surface only when the sun is at a sufficient angle. At latitudes above approximately 35° North or South — which includes most of the continental US, all of Canada, all of Europe, and much of Asia — UVB intensity is insufficient for vitamin D synthesis for several months in winter. Boston residents in January produce essentially no vitamin D from sun exposure, regardless of time spent outdoors.

**Indoor lives.** The average American spends approximately 90% of their time indoors. Window glass blocks UVB radiation; you cannot synthesize vitamin D through a window. People who work indoors during the day and live in urban environments may have near-zero sun-based synthesis year-round.

**Skin pigmentation.** Melanin is an effective UV filter — which means darker skin requires 3-5 times more sun exposure to synthesize equivalent vitamin D compared to lighter skin. South Asian, East Asian, African, Hispanic, and Middle Eastern populations living at northern latitudes are at substantially higher risk of deficiency. This disparity is clinically documented: vitamin D deficiency is far more prevalent in these groups in high-latitude countries.

**Sunscreen.** SPF 30 sunscreen reduces cutaneous vitamin D synthesis by approximately 95%. This is the necessary trade-off for skin cancer prevention — the recommendation to wear sunscreen is correct — but it eliminates sun-based vitamin D synthesis as a meaningful source for people using it consistently.

**Age.** The skin's capacity to synthesize vitamin D from UVB declines with age. Older adults produce approximately 25-50% less vitamin D per unit of UV exposure compared to young adults, at a time when absorption and renal conversion efficiency are also declining.

**Diet.** Very few foods contain meaningful vitamin D. Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) provide 300-450 IU per serving. Egg yolks provide 40 IU each. Fortified foods (milk, orange juice, cereals) add approximately 100 IU per serving. Even a well-designed diet provides 400-600 IU per day — well below the 1,000-2,000 IU per day most adults need to maintain sufficient status without sun exposure.

## What Deficiency Does

**Bone health** is the classical domain. Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption in the small intestine; without adequate vitamin D, even high calcium intake cannot prevent the bone demineralization that produces rickets in children and osteomalacia and osteoporosis in adults. This relationship is causal, well-established, and was the basis for the original fortification of milk.

**Immune function** is increasingly recognized as equally important. Vitamin D receptors are present on T cells, B cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells — essentially the entire adaptive and innate immune system. Active vitamin D (calcitriol) enhances the killing capacity of macrophages against pathogens, modulates T cell differentiation, and regulates inflammatory cytokine production. Vitamin D deficiency correlates with higher susceptibility to respiratory infections — the seasonal pattern of influenza outbreaks closely tracks with winter vitamin D depletion in northern hemisphere populations.

**Muscle function.** Vitamin D receptors are present in muscle tissue and regulate protein synthesis and fast-twitch fiber function. Deficiency is associated with muscle weakness, increased fall risk in older adults, and impaired athletic performance. Supplementation improves muscle strength in deficient individuals — not a modest effect, but a clinically meaningful one in older populations at fall risk.

**Mood and mental health.** Vitamin D receptors are present in brain regions involved in mood regulation, including the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. Deficiency is associated with depression in cross-sectional studies; the causal direction is less clear. Supplementation trials in deficient individuals show modest but consistent mood improvement. Seasonal affective disorder — depression with a winter onset pattern — has a plausible biological link to seasonal vitamin D depletion.

**Cancer risk.** The VITAL trial — the largest vitamin D supplementation trial ever conducted, with 25,871 participants — found a 25% reduction in cancer mortality in the vitamin D supplementation group (2,000 IU/day D3 plus omega-3s) compared to placebo over 5.3 years. This was an unexpected and striking finding: not a reduction in cancer incidence, but in cancer death rates — suggesting that vitamin D may influence tumor progression and treatment response. The finding requires replication but is consistent with mechanistic evidence that vitamin D regulates cell cycle checkpoints and apoptosis in cancer cells.

## Testing Your Status

The standard test is a serum 25(OH)D — 25-hydroxyvitamin D — level, available from any laboratory. Cost is $30-50 without insurance; it is often included in comprehensive metabolic or wellness panels.

**Threshold interpretations:**
- Below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L): deficient — the threshold used by most guidelines
- 20-30 ng/mL: insufficient — adequate for bone health but potentially suboptimal for immune and other functions
- 30-50 ng/mL: most experts consider this the optimal functional range
- Above 50 ng/mL: can be achieved with high supplementation; some researchers target this range, but benefit above 50 ng/mL is not well-established
- Above 100 ng/mL: potential toxicity threshold (vitamin D toxicity from supplementation alone, not sun exposure, is rare but possible)

## Supplementation: Getting It Right

**Form.** Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is significantly more effective than D2 (ergocalciferol) at raising serum 25(OH)D. D3 is derived from lanolin (sheep's wool) or lichen (for vegan formulations). D2 is plant-derived but pharmacokinetically inferior.

**Dose for deficiency correction.** For people starting below 20 ng/mL: 4,000-6,000 IU/day for 8-12 weeks, followed by retesting. For people in the insufficient range (20-30 ng/mL): 2,000-4,000 IU/day for correction, then maintenance dosing.

**Maintenance dose for most adults.** 1,000-2,000 IU/day D3 is sufficient for most people with adequate sun exposure in summer to maintain status above 30 ng/mL year-round. For people with minimal sun exposure or dark skin at high latitudes: 2,000-4,000 IU/day year-round.

**Take with fat.** Vitamin D is fat-soluble; absorption is improved when taken with a fat-containing meal. The difference is meaningful — taking D3 with a fatty meal can increase absorption by 50% compared to fasting.

**Vitamin K2 pairing.** At higher doses of vitamin D supplementation (2,000+ IU/day), many clinicians recommend co-supplementing with vitamin K2 (MK-7 form, 100-200mcg/day). The rationale: vitamin D increases calcium absorption, and K2 directs absorbed calcium to bones rather than soft tissues and arteries, potentially reducing arterial calcification risk from high-dose D3. The evidence for this interaction is mechanistically plausible though not conclusively established in large trials.

**Monitor.** Vitamin D toxicity from supplementation is rare below 10,000 IU/day in most adults, but testing every 6-12 months if taking doses above 2,000 IU/day is reasonable clinical practice.

## The Sun Exposure Balance

Brief, unprotected sun exposure on a large area of skin (arms and legs) during midday (10am-2pm) provides 10,000-20,000 IU equivalent vitamin D synthesis in fair-skinned people — far more than any supplement. Ten to twenty minutes, 2-3 times per week in summer, can meaningfully contribute to status.

The skin cancer risk from this brief exposure is very low. Extended unprotected exposure, or any unprotected exposure in people with a history of skin cancer or high genetic risk, is a different calculation that should involve a dermatologist.

The practical approach for most people: some strategic midday sun exposure in summer when latitude allows, daily D3 supplementation in winter or for those with consistently low sun exposure, and testing to confirm status is in the optimal range.

Vitamin D may be the easiest significant deficiency to fix. The barrier is not lifestyle change — it is simply knowing you're deficient.
