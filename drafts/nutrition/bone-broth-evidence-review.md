There's a version of bone broth that exists in marketing copy — a golden elixir that heals your gut, rebuilds your joints, tightens your skin, and adds years to your life. And then there's the version that exists in your pot: a liquid made from simmered animal bones that contains some genuinely interesting compounds, considerable variability, and a body of evidence that is real but far more modest than the labels suggest. The gap between those two versions is worth understanding, because bone broth isn't a scam — but it isn't a supplement either.

---

## What's Actually in the Bowl

The appeal of bone broth starts with its chemistry. When you simmer bones for hours, collagen — the most abundant protein in connective tissue — begins to break down into its component amino acids and peptide chains. The most notable of these are **glycine**, **proline**, and **hydroxyproline**, the three amino acids that form collagen's distinctive triple-helix structure. You also extract some minerals (calcium, phosphorus, magnesium) and, depending on how long you cook it, small amounts of proteoglycans like chondroitin sulfate and glucosamine from cartilage.

Here is the first problem: composition varies enormously. A **2017 analysis published in the *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*** by Alcock and colleagues measured the protein content of commercial bone broths and found values ranging from 2.5g to 8.4g of protein per cup — a threefold difference. Homemade broth shows similar variability depending on bone type (chicken feet versus marrow bones behave differently), cooking duration, and water-to-bone ratio. The mineral content is often overstated in marketing; a **2017 study in *Food & Nutrition Research*** by Heaney and colleagues found that bone broth contributes minimally to daily calcium intake compared to dairy. The point isn't that bone broth is nutritionally empty — it's that "bone broth" doesn't describe a standardized product. You may be drinking something meaningfully different every time.

## The Collagen Precursor Hypothesis — And Its Limits

The most coherent scientific argument for bone broth centers on its amino acid profile. The reasoning goes like this: collagen synthesis requires glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline as building blocks. Bone broth provides these building blocks. Therefore, consuming bone broth supports collagen synthesis in your joints, skin, and connective tissue.

This logic is not unreasonable — but it skips several steps. The body doesn't route dietary amino acids directly into specific tissues on demand. Glycine absorbed from broth enters a general amino acid pool and gets allocated based on competing metabolic priorities. Whether any meaningful fraction ends up in, say, your knee cartilage depends on factors the broth itself cannot control.

What the evidence actually shows is more nuanced. A **2019 randomized controlled trial in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine*** by Shaw and colleagues (77 participants, 12 weeks) found that **vitamin C–enriched collagen hydrolysate** taken before exercise increased collagen synthesis markers and reduced joint pain compared to placebo. The key word is hydrolysate: collagen that has already been enzymatically broken into short peptides, which appear to be absorbed and transported differently than free amino acids from broth. A **2021 systematic review in *Nutrients*** by Martínez-Puig and colleagues, covering 15 RCTs, found consistent benefit from **hydrolyzed collagen supplements** for joint pain and skin elasticity — with benefits appearing at doses of 2.5g to 10g per day of the hydrolysate specifically.

Bone broth is not hydrolyzed collagen. Some of its collagen breaks down into free amino acids, some into short peptides — but not in the controlled, standardized proportions used in the supplement studies. This is the honest comparison the broth industry would prefer you not make.

## Glycine: Where the Science Gets More Interesting

Strip away the collagen narrative, and something genuinely worth discussing remains: **glycine** specifically. Glycine is a conditionally essential amino acid — the body synthesizes it, but likely not in sufficient quantities for optimal function under conditions of high demand. A **2009 paper in *Amino Acids*** by Meléndez-Hevia and colleagues made the case that human glycine synthesis falls short of what metabolic processes actually require, by roughly 10g per day.

What does glycine do? A lot. At the cellular level, glycine is a key substrate for glutathione synthesis (your primary antioxidant defense), contributes to heme synthesis, is required for bile acid conjugation, and functions as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the central nervous system — explaining why it has a measurable effect on sleep architecture. A **2012 RCT in *Sleep and Biological Rhythms*** by Bannai and colleagues (11 participants) found that 3g of glycine taken before sleep improved subjective sleep quality and reduced daytime sleepiness, with polysomnography showing faster time to slow-wave sleep onset.

The doses that produce these effects — typically **3–10g per day** — are achievable through bone broth, but with an asterisk. A cup of bone broth contains roughly 1–2g of glycine depending on the batch. You'd need two to four cups daily to hit the low end of the studied therapeutic range, and you wouldn't know whether you were hitting it without lab testing the broth. A glycine supplement (available for a few dollars per hundred grams) gives you exactly 3g when you take 3g. This is not an argument against broth — it's an argument for knowing what you're actually getting.

## The Gut Health Claims: Proceed With Caution

No bone broth marketing roundup would be complete without the **"heals leaky gut"** claim. The mechanism proposed is that glutamine (found in bone broth, though not in large amounts) supports intestinal epithelial integrity, reducing intestinal permeability — the condition colloquially called leaky gut.

The underlying biology has some legitimacy. **Intestinal permeability** is a real phenomenon, measurable via lactulose-mannitol ratios, and genuinely elevated permeability has been associated with inflammatory bowel disease, critical illness, and certain chronic conditions. Glutamine does support enterocyte health; a **2021 review in *Nutrients*** by Kim and Kim found evidence for glutamine supplementation in critically ill patients and those with short bowel syndrome.

But here is what the evidence does not show: that drinking bone broth meaningfully reduces intestinal permeability in healthy or moderately symptomatic adults. No RCTs have tested bone broth itself against a control condition for this outcome. The glutamine content of broth is modest — typically 1–2g per cup — and far below the 20–30g doses studied in clinical populations. The leap from "glutamine supports gut lining in ICU patients" to "bone broth heals your gut" is a large one, made without supporting data.

## Nuance: Who Might Benefit, and Who Won't Notice

Bone broth is most likely to be useful as a **whole-food protein source** in the context of a varied diet — particularly for people who don't consume much connective tissue protein from other sources. If your protein intake skews toward muscle meat (chicken breast, lean beef, protein powder), you may be genuinely low in glycine and proline relative to methionine — an imbalance some researchers, including those at the **Baumann lab (Nutrients, 2020)**, have associated with elevated homocysteine and impaired collagen turnover.

It is least likely to be useful if you're hoping it will meaningfully replace a collagen peptide supplement, deliver reliable mineral doses, or resolve gut symptoms with a clinical cause. For those goals, the evidence points elsewhere.

The population most likely to see benefit: people over 40 with low dietary glycine intake, mild joint discomfort, and poor sleep quality — not because bone broth is medicine, but because it can contribute modest amounts of genuinely useful compounds in a form that happens to taste good as part of a meal.

## Practical Protocol

If you want to get the most out of bone broth without overpaying for mythology:

- **Make or buy it with gelatin in mind.** A broth that gels when refrigerated has meaningful collagen peptide content. A thin, watery broth does not.
- **Target 2 cups daily** if you're using it for glycine — this gets you to roughly 2–4g of glycine, meaningful but below the studied therapeutic dose for sleep.
- **Stack with vitamin C.** Shaw et al. (2019) found that collagen synthesis markers improved significantly only when vitamin C was co-administered. A small glass of citrus juice with your broth costs nothing and is mechanistically supported.
- **For joint pain or skin elasticity specifically**, the evidence favors hydrolyzed collagen peptide supplements at 5–10g/day over broth — more standardized, more studied, lower volume to consume.
- **Don't replace it with supplements reflexively.** A cup of broth with a meal contributes protein, warmth, and culinary satisfaction that a capsule doesn't. These things matter for dietary adherence.

Bone broth is real food with real compounds and a reasonable — if modest — evidence base. It is not a functional medicine intervention. Treat it like a nutritious addition to your diet rather than a cure, and you'll be appropriately positioned to benefit from what it actually offers.
