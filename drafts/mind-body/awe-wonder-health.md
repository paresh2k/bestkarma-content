In 2015, Dacher Keltner's lab at UC Berkeley published findings from a study that asked a simple question: among all positive emotions — joy, amusement, love, contentment, awe, pride — which one was most associated with reduced levels of IL-6, a key inflammatory cytokine linked to chronic disease?

The answer was awe. Not by a small margin.

This finding was surprising and, for researchers, revealing: it suggested that awe — the vast, disorienting emotion produced by encounters with extraordinary things — might have biological effects that go beyond mood. Ten years of subsequent research have clarified what awe does to the body, the brain, and behavior, and the picture is unusually coherent for a field as complex as positive emotion science.

## What Awe Actually Is

Awe is not a vague sense of wonderment. Keltner, whose lab has done more to scientifically characterize awe than anyone else, defines it precisely: awe is the experience of encountering something vast and not yet fully understood — something that stretches beyond our current mental frameworks.

The definition has two structural components. First, **vastness**: the object or experience is perceived as large — physically (a mountain range, an ocean), conceptually (the scale of cosmological time, the complexity of life), temporally (ancient architecture, deep history), or morally (a stunning act of human courage or skill). Second, **need for accommodation**: the experience resists easy assimilation. Something about it cannot be immediately processed and filed. It sits at the edge of comprehension.

This explains why awe feels different from simple pleasure. Pleasure is easy — the brain processes it efficiently and moves on. Awe produces a momentary cognitive disruption: the familiar frameworks don't quite fit, and something has to stretch.

Common awe triggers, documented across cultures, include: natural environments (particularly expansive ones — the night sky, the ocean, mountains, forests), live music and performance, art experienced in person, witnessing extraordinary human achievement or moral courage, profound spiritual or religious experience, mathematical elegance, and proximity to death or birth.

## The Health Evidence

**Inflammation.** The 2015 Keltner study — which found awe most strongly correlated with low IL-6 among positive emotions — has been followed by mechanistic work suggesting why. Awe activates the vagus nerve (the primary parasympathetic pathway) and appears to modulate cortisol secretion. Both effects suppress the inflammatory signaling cascade that chronically elevated stress hormones drive. The biological pathway runs: awe → parasympathetic activation → cortisol reduction → lower IL-6 and inflammatory tone.

**Daily stress and wellbeing.** A 22-day diary study of 269 adults, conducted by Keltner's lab, found that on days participants reported experiencing awe, they showed 20% lower daily stress, fewer physical complaints, and significantly greater psychological wellbeing — compared to their own average, controlling for other variables. The effect size was consistent enough to survive within-person controls, which are more stringent than between-person comparisons.

**Depression and long COVID.** A 2025 randomized controlled trial published in *Nature Scientific Reports* tested an online awe intervention in patients with long COVID — a population with among the highest rates of depression in any studied group, partly due to illness-related limitations on normal life. The intervention (online videos of awe-inspiring nature and human achievement, three sessions per week) produced significant reductions in depression symptoms compared to the control condition. The effect was meaningful in a population that is hard to treat and was achieved with no in-person contact, no medication, and no specialized equipment.

**Prosocial behavior.** Multiple studies find that awe reliably increases generosity, ethical behavior, and what researchers call "pro-social motivation" — the inclination to help others and act beyond narrow self-interest. This effect appears to operate through awe's characteristic reduction of preoccupation with the self.

## The Neuroscience: The Small Self Effect

The neurological signature of awe is unusually consistent: awe decreases activity in the default mode network (DMN), the brain network active during self-referential thought — the "what do I need to do," "how am I coming across," "am I okay" running commentary that constitutes much of resting human mental life.

The reduction in DMN activity during awe produces what Keltner calls the "small self" effect: the normal preoccupations of the self temporarily recede. You become less focused on your own narrative and more absorbed in what you're perceiving. This is the same neural signature observed during deep meditation, psychedelic experiences, and flow states — the common thread being a quieting of self-focused processing in favor of direct engagement with the present.

This is not merely an interesting observation. Elevated DMN activity is a characteristic of depression, anxiety, and rumination. Conditions that reduce DMN activity — meditation, mindfulness, awe, flow — consistently show therapeutic effects on these states. The mechanism for awe's antidepressant and anti-anxiety effects likely runs through this pathway.

## How to Cultivate Awe Intentionally

The original assumption in awe research was that awe was a rare, serendipitous experience — something that happened when circumstances aligned. What the research has revealed is that awe is more available than this, and that deliberate attention makes a significant difference in how often it occurs.

**Awe walks.** Walking in nature while deliberately attending to what is vast or extraordinary — rather than processing thoughts, listening to a podcast, or planning — reliably produces awe experiences. Keltner's research found that weekly awe walks produced anxiety reduction comparable to gratitude walks, with additional benefits on prosocial orientation. The key is directed attention: looking up, slowing down, noticing what is larger or older or more complex than you.

**Stargazing.** Among the most consistent awe triggers, and the one most frequently underused in contemporary life. Even 10-15 minutes under a clear sky — without a phone, without narration — shifts the temporal and spatial scale available to experience in a way that few other accessible activities can. The night sky is one of the few things that literally requires you to look at something billions of years old.

**Live music and performance.** Recorded music produces some awe; live performance, with the physical presence of other humans moved by the same experience, produces substantially more. The collective nature of live performance — the fact that others around you are being affected simultaneously — amplifies awe through the common humanity it makes visible.

**Perspective-shifting reading.** Cosmology, evolutionary biology, deep history, and mathematics can all produce awe through the conceptual vastness they invoke. Reading about the age of the universe, the complexity of multicellular evolution, or the history of civilizations shifts the scale of reference in ways that quiet the ordinary preoccupations of the self.

**The online alternative.** For people without easy access to nature, live performance, or awe-inducing environments, the 2025 long COVID trial demonstrated that video-based awe interventions — curated sequences of nature documentaries, footage of extraordinary human achievement, recordings of remarkable performances — produce measurable mental health benefits. The awe experience can be partially replicated through screen-mediated exposure, particularly when viewed attentively rather than as background content.

## What Awe Is Not — and Why That Matters

Awe is not the same as relaxation, entertainment, or distraction, and it doesn't work through those mechanisms. A streaming service designed to help you unwind is not producing awe. What distinguishes awe neurologically is the combination of positive affect and a degree of cognitive disruption — the momentary sense that something exceeds your current framework.

This is why the 2025 diary research found that passive content consumption (television, social media) did not produce the same wellbeing benefits as active awe experiences. The quality of attention matters: awe requires engagement, not just exposure.

For most people in routine modern life, the most reliable underutilized source of awe is the natural world — and the most common barrier is not access but habit. Looking at a screen while walking through a park is qualitatively different from setting the screen aside and looking at the park. The research on awe suggests this distinction is not trivial.
