You've probably heard the statistic about common diseases going undiagnosed. But the numbers for obstructive sleep apnea are striking enough to warrant their own category: approximately 1 billion people worldwide have OSA, with an estimated 80% undiagnosed. In the United States, roughly 30 million people have been diagnosed — against an estimated 50+ million who have it.

This gap is not explained by lack of symptoms. The symptoms of sleep apnea are prominent: loud snoring, daytime fatigue, morning headaches, waking unrefreshed, witnessed breathing pauses, difficulty concentrating. But they are also symptoms that people normalize, attribute to stress, aging, or busy schedules, or conceal from partners and doctors.

What's not visible — and what makes this gap medically consequential — is what happens inside a body during an untreated night of sleep apnea.

## What Happens During Each Episode

Obstructive sleep apnea is a mechanical problem. During sleep, the muscles of the upper airway relax. In OSA, they relax enough that the airway collapses partially or completely. Breathing stops.

The duration of each cessation — called an apnea — ranges from 10 seconds to over 90 seconds. The body detects the oxygen drop through peripheral chemoreceptors. The brain initiates an emergency arousal response: the sympathetic nervous system activates, cortisol and adrenaline are released, heart rate surges, and the person rouses (usually without full consciousness) just enough to restore muscle tone and resume breathing.

Then they fall back toward sleep. The airway collapses again. The cycle repeats.

In mild OSA, this happens 5-14 times per hour. In moderate OSA, 15-29 times per hour. In severe OSA, 30+ times per hour — meaning a cessation of breathing every two minutes throughout the entire night. Someone with severe untreated OSA is experiencing a cardiovascular stress event hundreds of times every night for years or decades.

Severity is measured by the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI): the number of apnea or partial obstruction events per hour of sleep.

## The Cardiovascular Consequences

The cardiovascular disease associations with OSA are among the strongest in sleep medicine, replicated across large cohort studies worldwide.

**Hypertension** is the most established consequence. OSA is an independent cause of high blood pressure — the repeated overnight sympathetic activation chronically elevates resting blood pressure. Notably, OSA is among the most common causes of treatment-resistant hypertension (blood pressure that doesn't respond adequately to medication). When clinicians encounter a patient with hypertension that doesn't respond to multiple medications, OSA is now on the standard differential diagnosis.

**Atrial fibrillation.** OSA is the most common comorbidity in patients with atrial fibrillation, and the bidirectional relationship is well-documented. OSA causes atrial stretch and sympathetic activation that promote AF; AF worsens hypoxemia and arousal. Treating OSA in patients with AF reduces recurrence rates after ablation procedures — a clinically meaningful effect that has changed electrophysiology practice in academic centers.

**Heart failure and coronary artery disease.** Large meta-analyses confirm OSA as an independent predictor of both heart failure and coronary events, with relative risks in the 1.5-3.0 range for moderate-to-severe untreated disease.

**Stroke.** The relationship between OSA and stroke is bidirectional and strong. Nocturnal hypoxemia, elevated clotting tendency, and hypertension all contribute. OSA prevalence in stroke patients exceeds 70%.

**Cognitive and mental health consequences.** Chronic hypoxemia — repeated oxygen desaturation throughout the night — causes hippocampal volume reduction detectable on MRI in moderate-to-severe OSA patients. Cognitive impairment, particularly in memory, attention, and executive function, is a consistent finding. Depression and anxiety are strongly associated with OSA, with bidirectional relationships.

## Who Should Be Tested

The clinical criteria for evaluation are well-established but under-applied:

- Loud, habitual snoring (reported by a partner, or documented on a recording)
- Witnessed breathing pauses during sleep
- Excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate sleep time — the Epworth Sleepiness Scale score above 10
- Waking unrefreshed most mornings
- Morning headaches (caused by nocturnal hypercapnia — elevated CO2)
- Neck circumference above 17 inches (men) or 16 inches (women)
- BMI above 30
- Established hypertension, particularly treatment-resistant
- Atrial fibrillation

The "snores and is overweight" stereotype of OSA is accurate statistically but misleading in its exclusivity. Approximately 25-30% of OSA patients are not obese. Women are substantially underdiagnosed — their symptoms often differ from the classic male presentation (less prominent snoring, more fatigue and insomnia rather than witnessed apneas), and the medical literature has historically been male-dominated in this area.

## Diagnosis: Home Test vs. Lab

Two testing approaches are available:

**Home sleep testing (HST):** A portable monitor worn at home measures airflow, breathing effort, oxygen saturation, and body position. Simpler, less expensive ($150-300), and accessible without a sleep lab. Sufficient for diagnosing moderate-to-severe OSA in patients with straightforward presentations. May miss mild OSA or OSA in patients with significant comorbidities.

**Polysomnography (PSG) in a sleep lab:** Full overnight monitoring including EEG, with technician oversight. Gold standard, particularly for complex cases, suspected periodic limb movement disorder, or when HST is negative but symptoms are strong. Most insurance covers PSG with physician referral.

For most patients presenting with classic symptoms, a home sleep test is a reasonable first step and produces results within days.

## Treatment Options

**CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure)** remains the most effective treatment for moderate-to-severe OSA. A small pump delivers pressurized air through a mask, creating a pneumatic splint that keeps the airway open throughout the night. It eliminates apnea events in most patients, restores normal sleep architecture, reduces daytime sleepiness, and lowers blood pressure.

The limitation is adherence: roughly 40-50% of CPAP prescriptions result in long-term regular use. The mask, the noise, the pressure, and the claustrophobia associated with wearing a device while sleeping are genuine barriers. Modern APAP devices (auto-titrating CPAP) adjust pressure breath-by-breath and have improved comfort and adherence significantly.

For patients who struggle with CPAP adherence, support matters: dedicated CPAP coaches, multiple mask trials, heated humidification, and regular follow-up substantially improve long-term use.

**Mandibular advancement devices (MADs):** Custom oral appliances that advance the lower jaw slightly, increasing airway space. Less effective than CPAP on AHI reduction but significantly better tolerated for mild-to-moderate OSA. Appropriate for patients who cannot tolerate CPAP.

**Weight loss:** In overweight patients, 10-15% body weight reduction can eliminate OSA entirely in many cases. This is the most durable and metabolically comprehensive treatment. Bariatric surgery produces remission in the majority of obese OSA patients. The challenge is that OSA disrupts the sleep quality and hormonal regulation needed for effective weight management — another bidirectional vicious cycle.

**Hypoglossal nerve stimulation (Inspire):** An implanted device that stimulates the hypoglossal nerve synchronously with breathing, preventing airway collapse. Effective, well-tolerated, and increasingly covered by insurance for patients who fail CPAP. An option worth discussing with a sleep specialist for CPAP-intolerant patients meeting anatomical criteria.

**Positional therapy:** Some patients have OSA predominantly when sleeping supine (on their back). Position-monitoring devices that gently vibrate to prompt side-sleeping are effective for this subset, as a primary or adjunctive treatment.

## The Diagnosis Gap as a Public Health Failure

The 80% undiagnosed figure represents a preventable burden of cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, and premature death. Many people are being treated for hypertension, AF, depression, and metabolic syndrome while the underlying cause — untreated OSA — continues unchecked.

The diagnostic tools are accessible, the treatments are effective, and the evidence base is strong. The bottleneck is awareness and physician screening rates.

If you snore habitually, feel unrefreshed after adequate sleep, or experience excessive daytime fatigue — ask specifically about a sleep apnea evaluation. If you have treatment-resistant hypertension or AF, it belongs on your diagnostic checklist. The recording feature on a smartphone left by the bed while you sleep can capture snoring and gasping patterns that make a compelling case for testing before a clinical visit.

The disease is common. The consequences are serious. The gap between those two facts is bridgeable — but only if people know to look.
