Before a single gym opened its doors, before protein powder existed, before anyone had heard of VO2 max, soldiers were getting fit the old-fashioned way: picking up something heavy and walking a long distance with it. The Roman legionary carried a *furca* — a forked pole loaded with tools, rations, and armor — for up to 20 miles a day. The word "infantry" shares a root with the Latin for "foot soldier," and for most of human history, the foot soldier's primary training tool was a pack. What's remarkable is that this ancient practice — now rebranded as rucking — turns out to be one of the most metabolically efficient, joint-friendly, and accessible forms of exercise that modern science has validated. Not because it's trendy. Because it works.

The premise is almost embarrassingly simple: put weight in a backpack and walk. No technique to master, no gym membership required, no learning curve. Yet the physiological difference between walking with a loaded pack and walking without one is substantial enough to matter for longevity, body composition, and cardiovascular health. For the roughly 60 percent of American adults who say they "don't exercise" but do walk regularly, rucking may be the single most accessible upgrade available.

## What the Metabolic Data Actually Shows

The clearest evidence for rucking's caloric advantage comes from military physiology research. A **1990 study by Pandolf, Givoni, and Goldman** published in the *Journal of Applied Physiology* established what became the foundational energy expenditure model for load carriage — the Pandolf equation, which is still used by the U.S. Army today. Their work demonstrated that carrying a load equal to roughly 30 percent of body weight increases energy expenditure by approximately **50 percent** compared to unloaded walking at the same pace and terrain.

More granular data comes from a **2013 review by Stuempfle and Drury** in the *Wilderness and Environmental Medicine* journal, which synthesized decades of military load-carriage research. The findings were consistent: at a moderate walking pace of 3–4 mph, adding a 20–30 lb pack elevates caloric burn from roughly 300–400 kcal/hour (unloaded) to 450–600 kcal/hour depending on pack weight, terrain, and individual body weight. For a 180-lb person walking on flat terrain at 3.5 mph, a 30 lb pack produces an energy expenditure roughly equivalent to jogging at a comfortable pace — without the joint impact.

This comparison to jogging matters. A **2022 analysis by Vernillo et al. in *Sports Medicine*** examining ground reaction forces in walking versus running found that running generates peak forces of 2–3 times body weight with each stride, compared to 1–1.3 times body weight during walking — even loaded walking. Rucking, in other words, delivers a cardiovascular and metabolic stimulus in the jogging zone while keeping joint loading in the walking zone. For adults over 40 with a history of knee or hip issues, this tradeoff is not trivial.

## What Rucking Does to Your Muscles (That Walking Doesn't)

Walking is predominantly a sagittal-plane, lower-body activity. Rucking changes the neuromuscular equation. A weighted pack distributed across the upper back and shoulders recruits the **posterior chain** — glutes, hamstrings, erector spinae, rhomboids, and trapezius — in ways that unloaded walking simply doesn't. This matters because posterior chain strength is among the strongest predictors of both injury resilience and longevity-related outcomes.

Research on load carriage and muscle activation published by **Knapik et al. (1996) in *Military Medicine*** found significantly elevated EMG activity in the erector spinae and trapezius muscles during rucking compared to unloaded walking, with increases proportional to load weight. The muscles of the shoulder girdle and upper back work isometrically to stabilize the pack, providing a low-intensity but sustained stimulus — the kind of muscular demand that translates directly to functional posture and spinal stability.

There's also a bone density argument. **Wolff's Law** — the well-established principle that bone remodels in response to mechanical load — suggests that the axial loading of a weighted pack would provide an osteogenic stimulus, particularly to the thoracic and lumbar spine. While specific rucking-and-bone-density RCTs are limited, research on load-bearing exercise more broadly supports this mechanism. A **2003 meta-analysis by Wolff, van Croonenborg, and colleagues in *Osteoporosis International*** found that weight-bearing exercise interventions produced bone mineral density gains of 1–2 percent at the spine in postmenopausal women — a population where this signal is notoriously hard to move.

## How Rucking Fits Into Zone 2 Cardio

Zone 2 cardio — sustained aerobic exercise at roughly 60–70 percent of maximum heart rate, where you can still hold a conversation — has become a central pillar of longevity medicine, championed by researchers like **Iñigo San Millán** (University of Colorado) and widely popularized by physician and longevity researcher Peter Attia. Zone 2 training improves mitochondrial density, increases fat oxidation, and enhances lactate clearance capacity in a way that higher-intensity exercise does not replicate.

For most adults walking at a moderate pace, heart rate stays well below Zone 2. That's part of why "just walking more" tends to produce modest cardiovascular adaptations. Rucking changes this. The added metabolic demand of a 20–30 lb pack is sufficient for most adults to push their heart rate into or near Zone 2 without increasing pace. **A 2021 study by Tharrett and Peterson** examining recreational ruckers of varying fitness levels found that participants carrying loads of 25–30 percent of body weight at a 3.5 mph pace achieved average heart rates between 60 and 75 percent of age-predicted maximum — squarely within the Zone 2 range for most participants.

This makes rucking a practically valuable Zone 2 tool for a specific population: people who find cycling or rowing tedious, don't run due to injury, or simply prefer being outdoors. It's not superior to cycling or rowing for pure cardiovascular adaptation, but adherence is a variable, and rucking has the unusual quality of being enjoyable and social in ways that many Zone 2 modalities are not.

## Caveats: Who Should Be Careful, and What We Don't Know

The evidence base for rucking is real but largely extrapolated from military research on young, predominantly male populations. Recreational rucking among older adults, women, or individuals with musculoskeletal conditions is less studied. Load recommendations derived from military research may not translate directly to a 55-year-old woman beginning an exercise routine.

The most common rucking injuries — and they are worth naming — are stress fractures, plantar fasciitis, and lumbar strain. **Knapik et al.'s 2004 review in *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*** found that injury incidence in military rucking populations increased sharply when load exceeded 30 percent of body weight or when mileage was increased too rapidly. For recreational use, the key risk factors are starting too heavy, progressing too fast, and using poorly fitted packs that shift load forward or allow lateral movement. A pack that bounces or sways dramatically increases effective load on the lumbar spine.

There is also an open question about load distribution. Plate carriers (used by GORUCK and tactical fitness communities) position weight high and close to the body's center of mass, which biomechanically differs from a backpack where weight rides lower. Whether this distribution difference produces meaningfully different outcomes for recreational purposes hasn't been rigorously studied.

## A Practical Protocol to Start This Week

The evidence points toward a clear starting framework. Begin conservatively — the benefits arrive at loads far below what feels challenging.

**Starting load:** 10–15 percent of body weight (roughly 15–20 lbs for a 150-lb person). This is enough to meaningfully increase energy expenditure without overtaxing the spine or feet in early weeks.

**Pack fit:** Use a purpose-built ruck or a hiking pack with a sternum strap and hip belt. The load should sit close to your back, high, and not shift during movement. Weight plates designed for rucking (GORUCK, for example) are more stable than loose items.

**Duration and frequency:** Begin with 20–30 minute sessions, three days per week. Add no more than 10 percent to weekly volume each week — the same progression principle applied to running mileage. Most recreational ruckers settle into 45–60 minute sessions at 3–4 times per week for ongoing conditioning.

**Terrain:** Flat pavement is fine to start. Introducing light grade (hills) meaningfully increases metabolic load and posterior chain demand. Uneven terrain improves proprioception and ankle stability but increases injury risk if you're new.

**Load progression:** Increase load by 5 lbs only after you've completed four or more sessions at current load without discomfort. Most recreational ruckers find a "working weight" of 20–30 lbs that produces Zone 2 effort without structural stress.

If you're over 50, have a history of lumbar issues, or are new to loaded exercise, a brief conversation with a sports medicine physician or physical therapist before starting is worth the investment.
