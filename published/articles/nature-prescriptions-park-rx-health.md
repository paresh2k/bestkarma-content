Your doctor hands you a prescription. You expect a drug name, a dosage, a pharmacy address. Instead, you get the name of a park, a recommended duration, and instructions to go outside. This is not a thought experiment — it is a clinical practice now operating across the NHS, Japan's national health system, New Zealand's GP networks, and more than 125 U.S. healthcare sites through **Park Rx America**. The intriguing part is not the prescription pad. It is that the science behind it is more rigorous than most people expect.

Nature prescriptions sit at the intersection of two bodies of evidence: psychophysiological research showing that natural environments measurably reduce stress hormones, blood pressure, and inflammatory markers, and epidemiological data showing that people who live near green space live longer, report better mental health, and use fewer primary care services. Taken together, these findings have persuaded physicians in at least a dozen countries that recommending a park is not soft medicine. It may be some of the highest-yield preventive medicine available.

---

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

The most cited entry point into this literature is a landmark **2019 study by White et al. in Scientific Reports**, which used data from 19,806 adults in England collected through the Monitor of Engagement with the Natural Environment survey. The finding was precise: people who spent at least 120 minutes in nature per week reported significantly higher wellbeing and better self-rated health than those who spent no time in natural settings. Critically, the effect was dose-dependent up to a point — those spending 120–179 minutes per week showed strong benefits, with no additional gain from dramatically higher doses. Below 120 minutes, no significant benefit emerged. That threshold has since become the most frequently cited number in nature prescription research: two hours, weekly, in nature.

The physiological mechanisms have been interrogated in tightly controlled settings. A **2010 study by Park et al. in Environmental Health and Preventive Medicine** conducted a series of field experiments across 24 Japanese forests, measuring salivary cortisol, heart rate variability, blood pressure, and pulse rate in 280 young male subjects. Forest environments produced a 12.4% reduction in cortisol, a 1.4% reduction in systolic blood pressure, and a 5.8% improvement in heart rate variability compared with urban controls — with subjects spending just 15 minutes seated in each environment. This was among the first rigorous physiological confirmations of what Japanese practitioners had called **shinrin-yoku** (forest bathing) for decades.

More recent meta-analyses have strengthened the aggregate picture. A **2021 systematic review and meta-analysis by Coventry et al. in SSM – Population Health**, drawing on 50 studies and 8,756 participants, found that nature-based interventions produced statistically significant reductions in depression, anxiety, and blood pressure, with moderate effect sizes (standardised mean differences around 0.5 for mental health outcomes). Importantly, effects held across diverse populations including clinical and non-clinical groups, and across a range of activities — walking, gardening, conservation volunteering — suggesting the natural environment itself, not merely the physical activity, was doing meaningful work.

## The Mechanisms: Why Green Spaces Change Your Biology

Two theoretical frameworks have dominated the mechanistic conversation, and they are not mutually exclusive.

**Stress Recovery Theory (SRT)**, developed by Roger Ulrich at Texas A&M in the 1980s, proposes that humans have evolved rapid psychophysiological responses to environments that historically signalled safety — water, open savannah, moderate vegetation — and that exposure to these environments triggers a fast shift from sympathetic to parasympathetic dominance. You do not choose to relax in a green space; your autonomic nervous system does it before your conscious mind catches up. Ulrich's original **1984 study in *Science*** found that post-surgical patients with a window view of trees recovered faster, used fewer analgesics, and received better nursing evaluations than patients with a view of a brick wall. The study had only 46 patients — tiny by modern standards — but its elegant design and dramatic effect sizes have made it one of the most replicated findings in environmental psychology.

**Attention Restoration Theory (ART)**, developed by Rachel and Stephen Kaplan at the University of Michigan, offers a complementary account. The Kaplans proposed that modern environments — open-plan offices, traffic, screens, notifications — demand **directed attention**, a cognitively effortful, top-down mode that depletes over time. Natural environments, by contrast, engage **involuntary attention**: the soft fascination of moving water, wind in leaves, or a horizon. This form of attention is effortless and restorative. It allows the prefrontal cortex, which governs directed attention and executive function, to recover. A **2008 study by Berman, Jonides, and Kaplan in Psychological Science** tested this directly: participants who walked in an arboretum versus downtown Ann Arbor showed significantly better performance on backwards digit-span tasks — a measure of working memory and directed attention — after the nature walk. The urban walk produced no such improvement.

At the molecular level, time in natural environments has been associated with increases in **natural killer (NK) cell activity**, a component of innate immunity. A **2008 study by Li et al. in the International Journal of Immunology** found that three-day forest visits in Japan produced increases in NK cell number and activity that persisted for more than 30 days, with investigators attributing the effect partly to phytoncides — volatile organic compounds emitted by trees, particularly conifers. Phytoncides have been shown to directly increase NK cell activity in controlled laboratory conditions. Whether these immunological effects have meaningful clinical consequences in healthy adults remains an open question.

## From Theory to Prescription: How Healthcare Systems Are Acting

**Park Rx America**, a non-profit founded in Washington D.C. in 2017, has built a platform allowing clinicians to write formal park prescriptions — specifying park name, duration, and activity — which are tracked as part of the clinical encounter. By 2023, the programme operated at over 125 sites across 40 U.S. states, with a growing evidence base from implementation studies showing improvements in physical activity, reduced screen time in children, and patient-reported wellbeing.

In the UK, NHS England's **Green Social Prescribing pilot**, launched in 2020 across seven test sites, referred patients with mild to moderate mental health needs to nature-based activities through social prescribing link workers — non-clinical navigators embedded in GP practices. The programme's 2022 evaluation, published by the University of Exeter and Natural England, found that participants showed significant reductions in anxiety and depression scores, with two-thirds of participants reporting improved mental health. Notably, the intervention was most effective in socioeconomically deprived areas — populations with the least existing access to quality green space, who also tend to be underserved by conventional mental health services.

Japan's formalization of shinrin-yoku began in 1982 under the direction of the national Forest Agency and has since developed into a structured therapy with accredited forest therapy trails and certified guides. Patients may be referred specifically for stress-related conditions, hypertension management, or burnout recovery. Similar programmes exist in South Korea, Finland, and New Zealand, where GP referral to outdoor walking programmes has been embedded in some district health board pathways.

## What the Evidence Doesn't Settle

The honest caveat here is significant: most nature prescription research relies on **self-reported outcomes**, short follow-up periods, and non-randomised designs. Blinding is essentially impossible — you cannot give someone a placebo forest. Effect sizes in mental health outcomes, while statistically significant, are modest by the standards of pharmacological interventions. The Coventry et al. meta-analysis noted substantial heterogeneity across studies, meaning results varied considerably depending on population, intervention design, and outcome measure.

There is also a confounding question the field has not fully resolved: how much of the benefit comes from the nature itself versus the physical activity, social contact, or simply time away from a screen? Studies using seated forest exposure (as in the Park et al. cortisol work) suggest the environment alone has some physiological effect. But most real-world programmes involve walking, often in groups — making it difficult to isolate the active ingredient.

## A Practical Protocol

The evidence points toward a specific, achievable target rather than a vague aspiration. Based on the White et al. dose-response data, the following approach is defensible:

- **Minimum effective dose:** 120 minutes per week in a natural setting — this can be achieved in two 60-minute sessions or spread across the week
- **Setting:** Any green or blue-green space counts (parks, woodland, coastline, rivers) — results do not appear to require wilderness
- **Intensity:** Walking is sufficient; vigorous activity is not required for the stress recovery or attentional benefits, though it adds cardiovascular gains
- **Consistency:** Benefits appear cumulative; irregular, high-dose exposure is likely less effective than regular shorter exposures
- **For clinical populations:** GP referral through a social prescribing pathway (where available) can provide accountability, group support, and guided programming — the Green Social Prescribing pilot data suggests this structure meaningfully improves uptake and adherence

The prescription is straightforward. The friction is cultural: we have built lives that make going outside the exception rather than the default. The evidence increasingly suggests that reversing that default is not a luxury but a clinical priority.
