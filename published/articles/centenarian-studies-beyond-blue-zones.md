The people who live past 100 don't follow the rules we wrote for them. Many centenarians smoke. Some drink. A surprising number are overweight by midlife standards. When researchers from the **New England Centenarian Study (NECS)** systematically profiled the lifestyle histories of over 1,500 people who'd reached 100 or beyond, they found that the usual suspects — pristine diet, lifelong exercise, low stress — explained far less than expected. What explained far more was something you can't buy, engineer, or find on a wellness retreat: their DNA.

That inconvenient finding doesn't mean lifestyle is irrelevant. It means the Blue Zones framework — however useful as a population-level heuristic — may have given us the wrong map for the territory of *exceptional* longevity. The centenarian studies are drawing a different map.

---

## What the Data Actually Shows

Blue Zones research, popularized by Dan Buettner and grounded in observational fieldwork in Sardinia, Okinawa, and Loma Linda, identified nine lifestyle characteristics shared by unusually long-lived populations: plant-heavy diets, regular low-intensity movement, strong social connection, and a sense of purpose, among others. It's compelling. It's also methodologically limited — retrospective, self-reported, difficult to separate from survivorship bias, and unable to isolate cause from correlation.

The centenarian cohort studies operate differently. The **NECS**, led by Thomas Perls at Boston University, has followed more than 1,600 centenarians and their family members since 1995, combining clinical phenotyping with genomic analysis. A landmark 2012 paper by **Sebastiani et al.** in *PLOS ONE* identified a genetic signature of 281 SNPs (single-nucleotide polymorphisms) that could classify centenarians with roughly 60% accuracy — far above chance. Crucially, many centenarians carried significant disease-risk alleles. They just appeared buffered against their effects.

The **90+ Study**, run by Claudia Kawas at UC Irvine and one of the largest studies of the oldest-old in the world, has followed over 1,700 people aged 90 and older since 2003. Its findings have been quietly disruptive. A 2018 analysis published in *JAMA* found that moderate alcohol consumption (up to two drinks per day) was associated with reduced mortality in this cohort — a finding that contradicts the increasingly zero-tolerance messaging around alcohol and longevity. The same study found that being *overweight* (BMI 25–30) at age 70 was associated with greater longevity than being normal weight. Neither finding fits cleanly into a Blue Zones narrative.

## The Genetics of Getting to 100

Two gene variants recur through centenarian literature with unusual consistency.

**APOE2** — one of three variants of the apolipoprotein E gene — appears at roughly twice the frequency in centenarians compared to the general population. In the general population, APOE4 is the risk allele: carrying one copy roughly triples Alzheimer's risk; carrying two copies increases it by as much as 12-fold. APOE2 appears to be functionally protective — associated with better lipid profiles, lower neuroinflammation, and reduced amyloid accumulation. A 2019 analysis by **Sebastiani and Perls** in *Frontiers in Genetics* found APOE2 enrichment across multiple independent centenarian cohorts, strengthening the case that this isn't noise.

**FOXO3A** is arguably even more interesting. A member of the forkhead transcription factor family, FOXO3A regulates an array of processes relevant to longevity: insulin signaling, oxidative stress response, apoptosis, and autophagy — the cellular recycling process increasingly associated with healthy aging. The initial association between FOXO3A variants and exceptional longevity was published by **Willcox et al.** in *PNAS* in 2008, based on the **Honolulu Heart Program cohort** of 5,765 American men of Japanese ancestry. Subsequent replication across European, German, Italian, and Chinese cohorts has made FOXO3A one of the most robustly replicated longevity loci in the literature.

What's notable about FOXO3A is that its effects appear to be *pathway-based*, not just variant-specific. The long-lived variants seem to enhance the cell's ability to respond to stress — activating repair mechanisms under conditions (caloric restriction, exercise, hypoxia) that would damage cells in people with less favorable variants. This is why FOXO3A research connects directly to interventions like fasting and exercise: you may be able to activate the same downstream pathways through behavior, even without the favorable genotype.

## What Genetics Doesn't Explain — And What Caloric Restriction Might

Even in centenarian studies, genetics is not destiny. **Perls and colleagues** have estimated, based on twin studies and NECS data, that genetic factors account for approximately 20–30% of variation in lifespan at the population level — and perhaps more at the extremes (supercentenarians, those over 110). But that still leaves 70–80% attributable to non-genetic factors, including environment, behavior, and — critically — stochastic biological noise (random cellular events that accumulate over decades and are invisible to any study).

This is where the **CALERIE trial** (Comprehensive Assessment of Long-term Effects of Reducing Intake of Energy) becomes relevant. CALERIE is the only long-term randomized controlled trial of caloric restriction in healthy, non-obese humans. Participants in the intervention arm aimed for 25% caloric reduction over two years. A key analysis by **Ravussin et al.** in *Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology* (2015) found significant improvements in cardiometabolic risk factors — blood pressure, insulin sensitivity, LDL cholesterol, inflammatory markers. A 2023 follow-up published in *Nature Aging* by **Waziry et al.** used DNA methylation clocks (epigenetic biological age measures) and found that two years of modest caloric restriction slowed biological aging by approximately 2–3% — a small but measurable effect in a two-year trial.

Importantly, CALERIE participants were achieving roughly 12% caloric restriction in practice (not the 25% target), which suggests the effect may be achievable without extreme dietary deprivation. The mechanism almost certainly involves FOXO3A-adjacent pathways: mTOR inhibition, AMPK activation, and upregulation of autophagy — the same cellular maintenance processes that the longevity-associated FOXO3A variants appear to enhance.

## What Centenarian Research Can't Tell Us Yet

Centenarian studies have real limitations worth naming. Survival bias is inherent and inescapable: you can only study the people who made it, which makes it difficult to know whether centenarians' lifestyles *caused* their longevity or are simply compatible with the underlying biology that did. Self-reported lifestyle data (diet, exercise, alcohol) in people who are 100+ is subject to obvious recall limitations. And most centenarian cohorts are heavily skewed toward white European populations, limiting generalizability — though the Honolulu Heart Program and ongoing work in Okinawan cohorts provide some cross-ethnic replication.

The genomic picture is also more complicated than individual variants suggest. The 2012 Sebastiani et al. signature identified 281 SNPs, not one or two — and none of those variants is deterministic. They shift probabilities, they don't determine fates. Someone with every favorable variant still needs decades of functional biology, social support, and a fair amount of luck.

## What You Can Actually Do With This

The gap between "this is what centenarians have" and "here is what you should do" is real, but not unbridgeable. The evidence suggests several priorities:

**Preserve the FOXO3A-adjacent pathways through behavior.** Even without favorable genotype, behaviors that activate AMPK and suppress mTOR — regular vigorous exercise, periodic caloric restriction or time-restricted eating, adequate sleep — appear to recapitulate some of the cellular maintenance benefits associated with longevity genetics. The 90+ Study found that exercising 15–45 minutes *daily* was the sweet spot for longevity in the oldest-old; less showed diminishing benefit, more didn't add much.

**Don't optimize for a single metric.** Centenarian data repeatedly shows that people who survive to extreme old age aren't maximizing any one variable — they're maintaining *adequate* function across multiple systems simultaneously. Perls describes this as "compression of morbidity": centenarians don't just live longer, they're sick for a shorter proportion of their lives, often experiencing major illness only in the final 1–2 years.

**Track biological age, not chronological age.** DNA methylation clocks (GrimAge, DunedinPACE) are increasingly available through consumer testing and offer a better read on your aging trajectory than birth year does. The CALERIE data suggests these clocks are sensitive enough to detect meaningful change in two years — making them useful for evaluating whether your interventions are working.

**Don't mistake Blue Zones heuristics for mechanisms.** Plant-rich diets, social connection, and purpose are probably genuinely good for healthspan. But they are downstream of biology, not substitutes for understanding it. Centenarian genomics is showing us what the biological targets are. The interventions that hit those targets — metabolic flexibility, cellular maintenance, inflammation suppression — are where the evidence is heading.
