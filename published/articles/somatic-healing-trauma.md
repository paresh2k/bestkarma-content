Traditional trauma therapy works primarily with the mind: it invites people to talk about what happened, understand it, reframe it, and find meaning in it. For many people, this works. For others — particularly those with severe, prolonged, or developmental trauma — talk alone misses something important. They understand intellectually that the war is over, or that the abuser is gone, or that they are safe now. The body doesn't seem to have gotten the message.

This gap between cognitive understanding and physiological response is what the somatic therapy frameworks have been built to address. The premise, increasingly supported by neuroscience, is that trauma isn't only stored as narrative memory — it is stored in the body as unresolved survival responses, as altered nervous system basity, as patterns of tension, collapse, hypervigilance, and freeze that persist long after the original threat has passed.

Treating trauma, on this view, requires working at that level.

## The Body-Based Model of Trauma

Peter Levine, the developer of Somatic Experiencing, arrived at his framework through an unexpected observation: animals in the wild routinely complete their stress responses and return to homeostasis. A gazelle chased by a predator, if it escapes, will often shake and tremble after the fact — a visible discharge of the accumulated activation energy from the threat response. It then returns to grazing.

Humans, with our more developed prefrontal cortex and the cultural conditioning that teaches us to manage and suppress physiological reactions, often don't complete this cycle. The biological survival response — which may involve a powerful impulse to run, fight, or freeze — is interrupted and overridden by cognitive control. The activation energy that was mobilized doesn't fully discharge. It remains in the nervous system as a kind of frozen state: the system is primed for a threat that never technically resolved.

Bessel van der Kolk, the psychiatrist whose work *The Body Keeps the Score* brought these ideas to mainstream attention, adds the neurological dimension. Trauma is stored not primarily as coherent narrative memory but as sensory fragments — physical sensations, sounds, smells, postural patterns, visceral states — processed through subcortical brain structures (amygdala, brainstem) that operate faster than conscious thought and below the reach of language.

This is why trauma can be triggered by sensory cues with no apparent logical connection to the original event, why hypervigilance and startle responses persist in safe environments, and why people can know intellectually that they are safe and still not feel safe. The knowing is cortical; the not-feeling-safe is subcortical and speaks a different language.

## Somatic Experiencing: Completing the Response

Somatic Experiencing (SE), developed by Levine over decades of clinical work, aims to do what the interrupted stress cycle left undone: allow the physiological survival response to complete.

The clinical approach works through two central principles. **Pendulation** involves moving attention back and forth between sensations of distress and sensations of relative safety or neutrality within the body — building the client's capacity to experience difficult material without being overwhelmed by it. **Titration** involves approaching traumatic material in tiny doses, rather than the intensive reliving sometimes associated with other approaches — small exposures that can be metabolized without retraumatization.

The goal is not primarily to revisit the story of what happened but to notice what is happening in the body when activated material surfaces, and to allow the biological response that was interrupted — trembling, shaking, impulses to move — to complete. In successful SE work, clients often experience spontaneous physical movements, tingling, or temperature changes as activation discharges.

Clinical evidence for SE has been developing. A 2025 study published in PLOS One found that a 12-week SE intervention produced significant reductions in PTSD symptoms in combat veterans — comparable to more established first-line treatments. A literature scoping review found consistent positive outcomes across small-to-medium studies, with safety profiles favorable to populations for whom intensive trauma exposure is contraindicated. The evidence base is smaller than for EMDR or Cognitive Processing Therapy, but is growing.

## EMDR: Bilateral Stimulation During Processing

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR), developed by Francine Shapiro in the late 1980s, is the most evidence-based of the somatic-influenced trauma therapies and currently holds WHO endorsement as a first-line treatment for PTSD.

The protocol involves recalling traumatic material while following bilateral stimulation — typically horizontal eye movements, alternating taps, or alternating audio tones. What happens neurologically during bilateral stimulation is still under active investigation, but leading hypotheses suggest it resembles the REM sleep state, during which the brain appears to process and consolidate emotional memories. The bilateral stimulation may facilitate the same kind of memory reconsolidation and desensitization that normally occurs during dreaming but is disrupted in PTSD.

What is clear from the evidence: EMDR produces significant and lasting reductions in PTSD symptoms, typically within 6-12 sessions, with effect sizes comparable to prolonged exposure therapy but often better tolerated (because it does not require as sustained contact with traumatic material). Multiple meta-analyses and direct comparisons support EMDR as equivalent to or marginally superior to Cognitive Processing Therapy and Prolonged Exposure on most outcomes, with lower dropout rates.

## Trauma-Sensitive Yoga: Safety Through the Body

Trauma-sensitive yoga adapts traditional yoga practice for populations with trauma histories — modifying the standard yoga framework to prioritize choice, body ownership, and safe sensory exploration over performance, instruction-following, and spiritual framing.

A randomized controlled trial by Bessel van der Kolk's group compared 20 weeks of trauma-sensitive yoga to 20 weeks of supportive women's health education in women with complex PTSD (chronic, repeated developmental trauma). The yoga group showed significantly greater reduction in PTSD symptoms — and, notably, a subgroup of participants who had failed to respond to prior pharmacological and psychotherapy treatments showed meaningful response to the yoga intervention.

The mechanism operates through interoception — the perception of internal body states. Many trauma survivors have learned, as a protective strategy, to disconnect from body sensations; their interoceptive awareness is diminished or disrupted, which leaves them unable to access somatic signals of safety or distress. Trauma-sensitive yoga rebuilds the capacity to notice body sensations in a context of safety and choice. This restored interoceptive access then supports other therapeutic work.

## Tension and Trauma Releasing Exercises (TRE)

TRE, developed by David Berceli, takes Levine's concept of natural discharge most literally: it uses a sequence of physical positions to deliberately induce neurogenic tremoring — the body's natural trembling response that is normally suppressed. The intentional activation of tremoring is proposed to release muscular tension patterns and stored activation energy from the nervous system.

The RCT evidence for TRE is less developed than for EMDR or SE, but it has been used extensively in humanitarian and disaster contexts (Berceli developed it working in Lebanon and Africa after conflict situations) and is now being studied in military populations. A 2025 veteran study found reduced PTSD symptoms with TRE as an adjunct to standard care. The method is one of the few body-based approaches that can be taught as a self-practice — practitioners eventually learn to induce and manage the tremoring independently.

## How to Think About Somatic Approaches

The somatic therapies are not alternatives to proven treatments — they are, in several cases, proven treatments themselves (EMDR holds that status unambiguously), and in other cases, emerging evidence-based adjuncts that address aspects of trauma the cognitive approaches do not fully reach.

For people with clear PTSD and access to an EMDR-trained therapist, the evidence strongly supports that approach as a first-line intervention. For people with complex or developmental trauma who have not responded well to talk-based therapy, SE with a trained practitioner represents a well-grounded alternative worth exploring. For people who want body-based practices as adjuncts to whatever professional care they're receiving, trauma-sensitive yoga offers the strongest RCT support.

The self-practices — breathwork, TRE, body-scan meditation, yoga — can serve as meaningful complements to professional trauma care but are not replacements for it in cases of clinical PTSD. They support the general capacity for nervous system regulation and interoceptive awareness that makes trauma therapy more effective. They are also, for many people living with subclinical stress, dysregulation, and the ordinary physical holding patterns that contemporary life produces, genuinely useful practices in their own right.

The central insight of the somatic framework — that the body holds what the mind has survived — is not a metaphor. It is a neurobiological description of what trauma is and where it lives. How we work with it follows from that.
