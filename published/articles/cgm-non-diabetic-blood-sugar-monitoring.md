If you strap a continuous glucose monitor to your arm and eat a slice of white bread, you will almost certainly watch your blood sugar spike. The app will flash. Your score will drop. You'll feel like you've done something wrong. But here's what that alert doesn't tell you: in a healthy person with a functioning pancreas, that spike is almost certainly going to resolve within 90 minutes, and the long-term health consequences may be close to zero. The question worth asking — the one the $89-per-sensor marketing rarely raises — is whether watching that number go up and then come down is actually doing anything useful for you.

CGMs have become one of the fastest-growing segments in consumer health technology, migrating from hospital wards and endocrinology clinics into the wellness routines of people who have never had a blood sugar problem in their lives. The pitch is compelling: real-time metabolic feedback, personalized nutrition insights, early warning for pre-diabetes. Some of it holds up. A lot of it doesn't.

## What CGMs Actually Measure — and How Accurately

A continuous glucose monitor works by threading a small filament sensor into the interstitial fluid just beneath the skin, where it reads glucose concentrations every few minutes and transmits the data to an app. It does not directly measure blood glucose — it measures **interstitial glucose**, which lags behind blood glucose by roughly 5–15 minutes and can diverge meaningfully during periods of rapid change, like after exercise or a large meal.

This distinction matters more than it might seem. The sensors currently approved for diabetic management — including Dexcom G6, Abbott FreeStyle Libre, and the Stelo (Abbott's recently FDA-cleared over-the-counter device) — are calibrated and validated primarily against the glucose ranges seen in diabetic patients, typically 70–400 mg/dL. In non-diabetic adults, where blood glucose typically fluctuates between 70–140 mg/dL in tightly regulated patterns, the measurement error becomes a larger proportion of the actual signal. A 2019 review by **Rodbard in Diabetes Technology & Therapeutics** noted that CGM accuracy in euglycemic ranges (normal glucose levels) is systematically lower than in hyperglycemic ranges, with mean absolute relative differences that can exceed 15% in the low-normal band. That means a reading of 85 mg/dL might reflect an actual value of 73 or 98 — a range that encompasses clinically distinct physiological states.

This isn't a reason to dismiss CGMs entirely. But it is a reason to be skeptical of treating every data point as signal rather than noise.

## What Does "Normal" Glucose Variability Look Like?

Before you can interpret your CGM data, you need a reference point — and the data on healthy people is more reassuring, and more variable, than the wellness space acknowledges.

A foundational study for this question is the **2018 Hall et al. study published in PLOS Biology**, which used CGMs in 57 adults without diabetes and found substantial glycemic variability even among metabolically healthy individuals. Post-meal glucose regularly exceeded 140 mg/dL — the threshold sometimes used to flag "prediabetes" in clinical screening — in people with perfectly normal HbA1c values and fasting glucose. Roughly 15% of participants spent more than 15 minutes above 140 mg/dL on a typical day, without any apparent metabolic consequence. The takeaway isn't alarming; it's clarifying: transient spikes are a normal feature of normal physiology.

The landmark **PREDICT study**, a collaboration between King's College London and Massachusetts General Hospital led by **Tim Spector and colleagues (2020, Nature Medicine)**, enrolled over 1,000 adults and paired CGM data with detailed dietary logs, gut microbiome analysis, sleep tracking, and blood biomarkers. It confirmed that postprandial glucose responses to identical foods vary enormously between individuals — twin studies within the dataset suggested that genetics explained only about 33% of glycemic response variability, with the rest attributable to gut microbiome composition, meal timing, sleep, and prior exercise. This is genuinely important science, and it forms the intellectual foundation for personalized nutrition. But it does not, on its own, establish that wearing a CGM improves health outcomes in healthy people.

## The Mechanism: Why Glucose Spikes Happen and Why Your Body Handles Them

The pancreas is remarkably good at its job. When blood glucose rises after a meal, beta cells in the islets of Langerhans release insulin in a biphasic pattern — a rapid first-phase release within 2–5 minutes of glucose entry into the bloodstream, followed by a sustained second-phase release over the next 30–120 minutes. In a healthy metabolic system, this response brings glucose back toward baseline efficiently, typically within 60–120 minutes of a meal.

The spikes that alarm CGM users are, in most cases, the system working as designed. Glucose rises to provide fuel to cells throughout the body, insulin facilitates uptake, and the signal clears. The concern in metabolic disease is not that glucose spikes, but that the insulin response is blunted (Type 2 diabetes), absent (Type 1), or chronically overwhelmed — leading to prolonged hyperglycemia and its downstream effects: glycation of proteins, endothelial damage, and inflammation. In a healthy person with intact insulin signaling, the spike is the stimulus; the clearance is the response. Pathologizing the spike in isolation misunderstands the physiology.

There is reasonable evidence that **chronic** glycemic variability — measured over months and years — is associated with adverse cardiovascular outcomes even in non-diabetic ranges. A 2019 meta-analysis by **Ceriello et al. in Diabetes Care** found that higher intra-individual glucose variability was associated with increased cardiovascular risk in both diabetic and non-diabetic cohorts. But "associated with" in observational data is not the same as "caused by," and none of this literature establishes that CGM-guided behavior change in healthy individuals reduces those risks.

## The Psychological Risk Nobody Is Talking About

Here is where the honest conversation gets uncomfortable. A 2020 pilot study by **Daubenmier et al. published in Nutrients** explored CGM use in adults without diabetes as part of a mindful eating intervention. While the researchers found modest improvements in dietary awareness and some participants reported useful behavioral feedback, the study also flagged something more troubling: a subset of participants developed heightened food anxiety, became preoccupied with glucose numbers, and reported increased stress around eating. The sample was small (n=23), and the study wasn't designed to measure psychological outcomes systematically, but the finding is consistent with a broader literature on health monitoring and anxiety.

There is a well-documented phenomenon in health psychology sometimes called **"cyberchondria by quantification"** — where continuous self-monitoring, particularly of biomarkers that fluctuate normally, amplifies health anxiety rather than reducing it. For individuals with a history of disordered eating, health anxiety, or orthorexic tendencies, a device that scores every meal in real time is not a neutral tool. The absence of formal screening guidance for who should and shouldn't use consumer CGMs is a genuine gap in how these devices are currently being sold.

## Nuance and Caveats

The PREDICT findings are real and interesting — individual variation in glycemic response is large and actionable in principle. But the critical limitation of virtually all CGM-in-healthy-people research is the absence of **randomized controlled trials with hard clinical endpoints**. We have mechanistic data. We have observational associations. We do not yet have a well-powered RCT showing that CGM use in non-diabetic adults reduces the incidence of Type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular events, or all-cause mortality. That study has not been done.

The population where the evidence is clearest for CGM utility outside of diagnosed diabetes is **prediabetes** — people with fasting glucose between 100–125 mg/dL or HbA1c between 5.7–6.4%. For this group, real-time feedback on how specific foods and behaviors affect glucose may be genuinely motivating and clinically useful. The same case can be made for individuals with insulin resistance, polycystic ovary syndrome, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, or a strong family history of Type 2 diabetes. For a metabolically healthy 38-year-old with normal HbA1c, normal fasting glucose, and no risk factors, the evidence is thinner.

## Who Should Consider a CGM — and How to Use One Wisely

If you fall into one of the elevated-risk categories above, or if you're an athlete trying to understand how training and recovery affect fuel utilization, a CGM trial of 2–4 weeks is a reasonable experiment. Here's how to do it in a way that generates signal rather than noise:

- **Don't optimize for a flat line.** Some variability is normal and healthy. The goal is pattern recognition, not glucose suppression.
- **Use it for 2–4 weeks, not indefinitely.** The utility is in learning your personal responses to foods, timing, sleep, and stress — not in perpetual surveillance.
- **Pair it with context.** Log meals, exercise, sleep, and stress alongside glucose data. A spike after a 10km run means something different than a spike after a sedentary afternoon.
- **Set a threshold that reflects actual risk, not aesthetics.** For non-diabetic adults, time-above-140 mg/dL is a more meaningful signal than any individual spike.
- **If monitoring is increasing your anxiety about food, stop.** The tool is meant to reduce uncertainty, not manufacture it.

For most metabolically healthy adults, an HbA1c test every year or two and a fasting insulin level provide most of the actionable information a CGM would generate — at a fraction of the cost and none of the ambient stress.
