Most people optimizing their protein intake are asking only half the right question. They're tracking grams — hitting 1.6g per kilogram of bodyweight, distributing carefully across the day — and feeling good about it. What they're often missing is that the *same* total protein, eaten at the *same* times, can produce meaningfully different amounts of muscle protein synthesis depending on whether each individual meal crosses a critical biological threshold. You can hit your daily target and still be leaving significant anabolic stimulus on the table.

The threshold in question is for **leucine** — the branched-chain amino acid that acts less like a building block and more like a molecular ignition key for muscle growth. Get enough of it at each sitting, and you trigger the machinery. Fall short, and the machinery largely stays dormant, regardless of how much protein arrives later.

---

## The Leucine Threshold: A Switch, Not a Dial

Leucine doesn't simply contribute to muscle protein synthesis (MPS) in proportion to how much of it you consume. It behaves more like a light switch than a dimmer. Below a certain plasma concentration, the anabolic signaling cascade — centered on the **mTORC1 pathway** — remains largely inactive. Once leucine crosses that threshold, mTORC1 activates, initiating the translation of new contractile proteins. More leucine beyond that point produces diminishing returns; the switch is already on.

The practical threshold, established across multiple studies from Douglas Paddon-Jones at the University of Texas Medical Branch and replicated by subsequent researchers, sits at approximately **2.5–3g of leucine per meal**. For most whole-food protein sources, this corresponds to roughly **30–40g of total protein per sitting** — a serving of chicken breast, a large container of Greek yogurt, four eggs plus a side of cottage cheese. Importantly, this threshold is not fixed: it rises with age, body mass, and the leucine content of the specific protein source.

---

## The Evidence for Distribution Over Total Intake

The landmark challenge to "total daily protein" thinking came from a 2009 study by **Paddon-Jones and Rasmussen** published in *Current Opinion in Clinical Nutrition and Metabolic Care*. Their controlled feeding experiments demonstrated that distributing protein evenly across three meals — roughly 30g per meal — produced greater 24-hour MPS than the same total protein concentrated in one or two larger feedings. The mechanism wasn't mysterious: even distribution meant the leucine threshold was crossed three times daily rather than once or twice.

This was extended into more granular territory by **Areta et al. (2013)** in the *Journal of Physiology*, which is arguably the cleanest experimental test of protein distribution to date. In a post-exercise context, 80g of whey protein was administered to young men across a 12-hour recovery window in one of three conditions: eight small doses of 10g, four moderate doses of 20g, or two large doses of 40g. The **4 × 20g protocol produced the greatest myofibrillar protein synthesis** — roughly 31% higher than the bolus condition and 19% higher than the pulse condition. The 10g doses likely failed to consistently clear the leucine threshold; the 40g doses crossed it but offered no additional benefit from the excess protein.

**Trommelen and van Loon (2016)** in *Nutrients* later reinforced the translational picture, examining protein intake in the context of a full day's eating rather than controlled lab windows. Their analysis of protein distribution data across habitual diets found that the average Western eating pattern — which tends to concentrate most protein in the evening meal — is suboptimal for maximizing MPS across the day. A morning meal delivering 40g of protein and an evening meal delivering 60g produces fewer threshold-crossing events than three meals each delivering 33g, even if total intake is identical.

## Why Older Adults Should Pay Particular Attention

The leucine threshold concept matters for everyone, but it takes on urgent significance for adults over 55. **Anabolic resistance** — the blunted MPS response to a given dose of amino acids — is one of the defining physiological features of aging muscle. Put simply: the older you are, the more leucine it takes to flip the same switch.

**Churchward-Venne et al. (2012)** in the *Journal of Physiology* showed that in older adults, co-ingestion of leucine alongside a sub-optimal protein dose could rescue the anabolic signaling deficit — essentially compensating for anabolic resistance by spiking leucine above the threshold artificially. Their work helped establish that the leucine threshold in older adults may sit closer to **3–4g per meal**, implying a need for 35–45g of high-quality protein per sitting or deliberate leucine supplementation.

This matters enormously in the context of **sarcopenia**, the age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass that accelerates after 60 and predicts falls, fractures, metabolic decline, and all-cause mortality. Most older adults are not only eating too little total protein; they're distributing what little they eat in patterns that fail to trigger MPS at any meal. A typical older adult breakfast — toast and tea, or yogurt and fruit — may deliver 10–15g of protein, well below the threshold. The anabolic opportunity of that meal is largely wasted.

Paddon-Jones has been particularly vocal on this point, noting in multiple publications that the protein distribution problem in older adults is at least as significant as the total intake problem — and potentially more tractable, since it can be addressed through meal restructuring without requiring a dramatic increase in total calories.

## The Pre-Sleep Protein Evidence

One specific distribution strategy has accumulated enough evidence to deserve its own mention: **pre-sleep protein ingestion**. The work of **Luc van Loon's group at Maastricht University**, most comprehensively reviewed by Trommelen and van Loon in *Nutrients (2016)*, established that overnight represents a substantial window during which MPS rates are low — not because the muscle machinery is switched off, but because most people are fasted for 7–9 hours and circulating amino acids fall well below the leucine threshold.

A 2012 study from the same group (**Res et al., Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise**) found that 40g of casein protein consumed 30 minutes before sleep increased overnight MPS by approximately 22% compared to placebo in young men who had trained that evening. Casein's slow digestion rate — it clots in the stomach and releases amino acids gradually over 5–7 hours — makes it particularly well-suited to the overnight window. Subsequent work confirmed that this effect is not confined to post-exercise contexts: even without prior resistance training, pre-sleep protein elevates overnight MPS.

## What We Don't Know

The distribution literature, while compelling, has real limits worth naming. Most controlled feeding studies have been conducted in young men or in supervised lab settings, making direct extrapolation to free-living populations — especially women and older adults — imperfect. Individual variation in gastric emptying, protein digestion rate, and mTORC1 sensitivity means the leucine threshold is a population average, not a personal prescription.

It's also worth noting that the studies comparing distribution patterns have largely used whey or casein — fast and slow animal proteins. Whether the same threshold dynamics apply equally to plant proteins, which have lower leucine density and less complete amino acid profiles, is less thoroughly characterized. Plant-based eaters likely need to increase per-meal protein targets further to achieve equivalent leucine delivery.

---

## Practical Protocol

Getting this right doesn't require a spreadsheet. It requires shifting one question: not "did I hit my daily protein goal?" but "did I cross the leucine threshold at each meal?"

**For adults under 55:**
- Target **30–40g of high-quality protein per meal**, spread across 3–4 meals
- Prioritize **leucine-rich sources**: whey protein (~2.5g leucine per 25g protein), chicken breast, eggs, Greek yogurt, salmon, beef
- Post-exercise: aim for at least 20–40g within 2 hours of training — the muscle is more sensitive, but the threshold still applies

**For adults over 55:**
- Raise the per-meal target to **35–45g of protein** to compensate for anabolic resistance
- Consider **2–3g of additional leucine** alongside meals that fall short (leucine powder is inexpensive and tasteless)
- Audit breakfast specifically — it's the meal most likely to miss the threshold
- Consider **30–40g of casein** (cottage cheese, casein powder) 30–60 minutes before sleep

|---|---|

| Chicken breast | ~2.4g |
| Eggs | ~2.3g |
| Greek yogurt | ~2.0g |
| Tofu | ~1.5g |
| Lentils | ~1.3g |

If you're plant-based, these numbers suggest that hitting the leucine threshold requires either larger serving sizes or deliberate leucine supplementation — especially at breakfast, and especially after 55.
