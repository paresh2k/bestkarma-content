Most people who start Ozempic or Wegovy are thinking about the number on the scale. Few are thinking about what's happening to the tissue underneath. That's a problem — because the clinical trial data tells a story that the prescription pad often doesn't: a meaningful portion of the weight lost on GLP-1 receptor agonists isn't fat. It's muscle.

This isn't a reason to avoid these medications. For the right person, semaglutide and tirzepatide are genuinely transformative tools. But they work by suppressing appetite so effectively that many users enter a sustained caloric deficit deep enough to trigger the same metabolic cascade seen in crash dieting — accelerated lean mass loss, reduced strength, and a metabolic rate that may take years to recover. The good news is that this is largely preventable, if you know what to do.

---

## The Body Composition Problem Hidden in the Trial Data

The **STEP-1 trial** (Wilding et al., *New England Journal of Medicine*, 2021), which established semaglutide's efficacy in 1,961 adults with obesity, reported a mean body weight reduction of 14.9% over 68 weeks. The headlines focused on that number. What received less attention was that the trial didn't fully characterize the lean-to-fat ratio of what was lost.

That data came later. **SURMOUNT-1** (Jastreboff et al., *NEJM*, 2022), the landmark trial for tirzepatide — the dual GIP/GLP-1 agonist marketed as Mounjaro — tracked 2,539 adults over 72 weeks and found mean weight loss of up to 22.5% at the highest dose. Body composition analysis in a subset of participants revealed that roughly **40% of the weight lost was lean mass**, including muscle. Investigators noted this was consistent with what is observed in other models of aggressive caloric restriction — the body doesn't selectively spare muscle when it's losing weight this fast.

The **STEP-HFpEF trial** (Kosiborod et al., *NEJM*, 2023) — focused on heart failure patients with preserved ejection fraction — raised an even sharper concern. Semaglutide produced an average weight loss of 13.3% in a population where muscle mass is already compromised. The researchers flagged that in this context, loss of lean mass could partially offset the cardiovascular and functional benefits of the medication. The trial highlighted that what the scale says and what's happening to body composition are two very different stories.

The **SUSTAIN-6 trial** (Marso et al., *NEJM*, 2016), while primarily a cardiovascular outcomes trial, similarly didn't optimize for lean mass preservation — because the field, at the time, wasn't asking that question. It's only now, as GLP-1 use has become population-scale, that researchers and clinicians are catching up.

## Why Fast Weight Loss Hits Muscle Harder

The mechanism here matters, and it's not complicated once you see it.

When you're in a caloric deficit, your body draws on stored energy — ideally fat. But protein is also a macronutrient your body can oxidize for fuel, and muscle is a large, metabolically expensive tissue. Under conditions of sufficient protein intake and mechanical stress (i.e., resistance training), the body gets a signal to preserve that tissue. Without those signals, lean mass becomes fair game.

The critical threshold, supported by decades of energy balance research, is approximately **0.5–1% of body weight per week**. Lose faster than that — as many GLP-1 users do, particularly in the first few months — and the proportion of lean mass in total weight loss rises significantly. A 2021 analysis by **Cava et al. in *Obesity Reviews*** found that in studies of very low-calorie diets (below 800 kcal/day), lean mass comprised up to 30–40% of total weight lost. GLP-1-induced appetite suppression can easily push users into this territory without them realizing it.

The hormonal picture compounds the problem. Semaglutide and tirzepatide reduce IGF-1 signaling indirectly through caloric restriction, and IGF-1 is one of the primary drivers of muscle protein synthesis. Meanwhile, cortisol — the body's stress and gluconeogenesis hormone — rises in states of caloric deficit. That combination creates a catabolic environment that, without countermeasures, preferentially erodes muscle over time.

## What the Evidence Says About Protein on GLP-1s

Here's where the practical intervention begins. The single most evidence-supported lever for preserving lean mass during weight loss is **protein intake** — and the targets appropriate for GLP-1 users are substantially higher than standard dietary guidelines suggest.

The **Recommended Dietary Allowance for protein is 0.8g per kilogram of body weight** — a figure designed to prevent deficiency in sedentary adults, not to preserve muscle during active weight loss. A landmark 2015 meta-analysis by **Morton et al. in** ***AJCN*** of 49 randomized controlled trials found that increasing protein intake during a caloric deficit significantly attenuated lean mass loss, with benefits plateauing around **1.6g/kg/day** for most adults. For older adults and those in aggressive deficits, the protective ceiling is higher.

**Stokes et al. (*Nutrients*, 2018)** reviewed the evidence for protein requirements during weight loss specifically and concluded that intakes of **1.6–2.4g/kg of body weight per day** were optimal for preserving lean mass, with the upper range most relevant during hypocaloric conditions. GLP-1 users suppressing 500–1,000+ calories per day likely fall into that higher-need category.

The practical challenge: GLP-1 medications suppress appetite non-selectively. Users often find that protein-dense foods — meat, fish, eggs, legumes — feel particularly filling and are the first things they stop eating. This makes hitting protein targets harder, not easier. Several clinicians working with GLP-1 patients, including **Dr. Erin Avery and colleagues at Stanford Medicine** (reporting clinically, 2023), have noted that protein intake often falls to 60–70g per day in users who aren't actively monitoring — roughly half the recommended target for a 75kg adult.

The solution is front-loading protein intentionally: prioritize it at every meal before appetite suppression ends the eating window. Protein shakes aren't glamorous, but in this context they are clinically useful.

## Resistance Training Is Non-Negotiable

Protein is necessary but not sufficient. The signal that tells your body to rebuild or retain muscle is **mechanical loading** — picking up heavy things and putting them down. This is not wellness culture; it's basic myofibrillar biology.

**Resistance exercise activates the mTOR pathway**, which triggers muscle protein synthesis. Without that activation, dietary protein has less to do. A 2022 systematic review by **Lim et al. in *Sports Medicine*** of 58 trials found that combining higher protein intake with resistance training preserved lean mass during caloric restriction far more effectively than either intervention alone — an effect size roughly twice that of diet intervention without exercise.

The **STEP-1 and SURMOUNT-1** trials did not include structured resistance training protocols. This is almost certainly why lean mass losses were as high as they were. The medications were doing exactly what they were designed to do — reduce caloric intake — while the participants exercised at baseline levels that weren't sufficient to protect lean tissue.

For GLP-1 users, the evidence supports a minimum of **two to three resistance training sessions per week**, targeting all major muscle groups, with progressive overload — meaning you're adding weight or reps over time, not just going through the motions. Intensity matters: **Schoenfeld et al. (*Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*, 2017)** found that training within 1–3 reps of failure is the critical stimulus for hypertrophy and lean mass preservation regardless of load.

## Nuance: Who Is Most at Risk?

Two populations deserve particular attention. **Adults over 50** are already experiencing sarcopenia — the age-related loss of muscle mass that begins in the fourth decade of life and accelerates in the fifth and sixth. Losing an additional 30–40% of weight loss as lean mass on top of baseline sarcopenia is a compounding risk, particularly for functional independence and bone health. In this group, protein targets closer to **2.0–2.4g/kg** and supervised resistance training aren't optional — they're essential.

The second group is patients with obesity-related **heart failure** (as in STEP-HFpEF). Here, weight loss has cardiovascular benefits, but the loss of skeletal muscle — which plays a role in cardiac output and functional capacity — may mute those gains. This is an evolving research area, and clinicians should monitor functional metrics, not just body weight, in these patients.

It's also worth noting that the body composition data from GLP-1 trials is, in many cases, based on **DEXA scans in subgroups** rather than the full trial population. The numbers are real, but sample sizes for body composition endpoints are often smaller than for the primary weight-loss outcomes. The direction of the evidence is clear; the precise magnitudes should be held with some humility.

## Practical Protocol for GLP-1 Users

This is what the evidence supports, made specific:

**Protein targets:**
- Minimum: **1.6g per kilogram of current body weight per day**
- Optimal for users over 50 or in aggressive deficit: **2.0–2.2g/kg/day**
- Distribute across 3–4 meals, with at least **30–40g per sitting** to maximize muscle protein synthesis (based on Moore et al., *AJCN*, 2015)
- Prioritize complete proteins: animal sources (meat, fish, eggs, dairy), or combinations that cover all essential amino acids (soy, pea protein + rice)

**Resistance training:**
- Frequency: **3x per week minimum**
- Structure: Full-body or upper/lower split; all major muscle groups per week
- Intensity: Work within **2–4 reps of muscular failure** on primary movements
- Progressive overload: Increase load or reps every 1–2 weeks

**Monitoring:**
- Track protein intake for at least the first 4–6 weeks (most people are surprised by how far below target they fall)
- Consider baseline DEXA scan if accessible — it gives you a number to protect, not just body weight to reduce
- Discuss body composition, not just weight, with your prescribing clinician at every check-in

**Rate of loss:**
- If losing more than **1% of body weight per week** consistently, discuss with your clinician whether dose adjustment is appropriate — fast loss is not always better loss
