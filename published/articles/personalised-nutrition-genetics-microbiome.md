The most striking finding in modern nutrition research isn't about a superfood or a macronutrient ratio. It's about how wildly different two people's blood sugar can spike after eating the exact same meal. In a landmark 2015 study published in *Cell*, Eran Segal, Eran Elinav, and colleagues at the Weizmann Institute measured continuous glucose responses in 800 adults eating standardized meals. The variability was enormous — one person's blood sugar would barely budge after white rice while another's would spike as dramatically as if they'd eaten straight glucose. The average response to a given food told you almost nothing useful about any individual's response to that food. If the average is that misleading, the premise of universal dietary guidelines starts to look shaky.

This is the scientific foundation for personalised nutrition: the idea that the optimal diet isn't fixed, but varies person to person based on genetics, gut microbiome composition, metabolic history, and lifestyle. It's a compelling idea — and it's also now a marketing category, complete with consumer DNA tests, stool sample kits, and wearable glucose monitors promising to decode your unique biology. The science behind the concept is real. The consumer tools are, in most cases, significantly ahead of the evidence that would justify their claims.

---

## What the Research Actually Shows

The Weizmann study — formally **Zeevi et al., 2015, *Cell*** — remains one of the most influential papers in personalised nutrition. The researchers recruited 800 healthy and pre-diabetic adults, fitted them with continuous glucose monitors for a week, and tracked their responses to nearly 47,000 meals. They found that postprandial glycaemic response (the blood sugar rise after eating) was highly individualised, shaped by a combination of factors including gut microbiome composition, body metrics, physical activity, and sleep — but not reliably predicted by the food's glycaemic index alone. They then trained a machine learning algorithm on microbiome and other data to predict personalised glycaemic responses, and validated it in a small cohort of 26 additional subjects. The algorithm outperformed GI-based predictions.

The follow-up work — the **PREDICT programme** led by Tim Spector at King's College London, in collaboration with researchers at Harvard and Tufts — substantially expanded on this. The PREDICT 1 study, published in *Nature Medicine* in 2020 (Berry et al.), enrolled 1,002 twins and unrelated adults, again using CGM alongside detailed dietary tracking, gut microbiome profiling, and blood biomarkers. The results reinforced the individualisation story: even identical twins showed meaningfully different postprandial responses to the same foods, with gut microbiome composition and meal context (timing, prior sleep, exercise) accounting for a significant portion of variability. A key finding was that **postprandial triglyceride and insulin responses** were at least as important as glucose responses for understanding metabolic health — and were similarly individualised.

These are real, important findings. But it's worth being precise about what they established: that interpersonal variability exists, that the microbiome contributes to it, and that machine learning models *can* do better than population averages. They did not establish that the consumer products built on this science deliver clinically meaningful improvements to long-term health outcomes. That is a different question, and largely unanswered.

## What Your DNA Test Can and Cannot Tell You

Nutrigenomic tests — products like Nutrigenomix, GenoPalate, or DNAfit — typically sequence a panel of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and return reports suggesting you metabolise caffeine slowly, absorb vitamin D poorly, or respond better to a lower-carbohydrate diet. The appeal is obvious. The limitations are substantial.

The science of gene-diet interactions is real but immature. Variants in **CYP1A2** do genuinely affect caffeine metabolism, and a 2018 randomised trial by Boekholdt and colleagues in *BMJ* found that slow metabolisers had elevated cardiovascular risk with higher coffee intake. Variants in **FTO** and **PPARG** are associated with differential responses to dietary fat. **MTHFR** variants affect folate processing. These associations exist and are biologically plausible.

The problem is effect size and actionability. Most validated gene-diet interactions explain a small fraction of population-level variance, and they were identified in large epidemiological studies that measure associations, not in intervention trials showing that genotype-guided diets outperform standard advice. A systematic review by Celis-Morales et al., published in *PLOS Medicine* in 2017, examined eight randomised controlled trials of personalised dietary advice based on genetic information and found no significant improvement in dietary behaviour or biomarkers compared to general advice. The genes being tested in consumer panels are often chosen more for their marketability — clean, comprehensible, binary — than for the strength of their evidence base.

What DNA tests legitimately offer: clinically meaningful information for a small subset of variants. **APOE ε4 status** has well-established implications for saturated fat intake and Alzheimer's risk. **MTHFR C677T** homozygosity may warrant methylfolate supplementation. If a test surfaces one of these, it's worth discussing with a clinician. For the broader package of 30–70 "personalised" dietary recommendations, the evidence for acting on them is thin.

## The Microbiome: Real Biology, Uncertain Guidance

The gut microbiome is genuinely extraordinary — trillions of organisms metabolising food, modulating immune function, producing neurotransmitter precursors, and influencing systemic inflammation. Its composition varies substantially between individuals and does appear to shape how we process food. The Weizmann and PREDICT research is not wrong about this.

What commercial microbiome testing offers is a different matter. Companies like Viome, Thryve, or Atlas Biomed sequence your stool sample and return dietary recommendations based on your microbial profile. The core problem is that the microbiome science used to justify these recommendations relies heavily on cross-sectional association data — we know that people with certain microbial compositions have certain health profiles, but the causal direction is often unclear, the microbiome is highly dynamic (it can change substantially in days with dietary shifts), and the field lacks validated, reproducible reference ranges for "optimal" microbial composition.

A 2019 review by Dahl, Crost and colleagues in *Nutrients* noted that while the microbiome clearly influences host metabolism, translating microbiome signatures into reliable dietary prescriptions remains technically and scientifically premature. A specific example: many tests flag low *Akkermansia muciniphila* as a concern, citing its associations with metabolic health. But whether supplementing or eating to increase *Akkermansia* causes downstream health improvements in otherwise healthy adults — and what that intervention should look like — is still under investigation.

The most honest framing is this: microbiome testing can confirm that dietary diversity matters (higher diversity is consistently associated with better outcomes) and may identify meaningful dysbiosis in symptomatic individuals, but its role in fine-tuning the diet of a healthy person remains speculative.

## CGM: The Most Useful Tool — With Caveats

Of the three personalised nutrition tools, continuous glucose monitors have the strongest short-term feedback utility. Wearing a CGM for two to four weeks gives you real data on your actual glucose responses to your actual foods, eaten in your actual life. It can reveal that your "healthy" overnight oats spike your blood sugar, that a glass of wine with dinner flattens a carbohydrate response, or that your morning cortisol is already pushing glucose up before you eat anything.

But CGM data for non-diabetic individuals requires careful interpretation. In metabolically healthy adults, transient glucose spikes are not automatically pathological — the clinical significance of postprandial glucose variability in people without diabetes or insulin resistance is still debated. **Shah et al. (2019, *Diabetes Care*)** found that non-diabetic individuals regularly experience glucose excursions above 140 mg/dL with no apparent adverse consequences, which would be flagged as alarming by some consumer CGM platforms. Using a CGM without appropriate context risks optimising for a metric whose target range — in a healthy person — is not yet evidence-based.

Used well, CGM is a powerful learning tool: it helps you understand how sleep, stress, meal timing, and food composition interact in your specific body. Used poorly, it generates anxiety about normal physiology.

## A Practical Framework

The science of personalised nutrition is advancing faster than the products built on it. Here's how to engage with these tools without over-interpreting them:

- **CGM:** Consider a 2–4 week trial if you're metabolically curious or have a family history of type 2 diabetes. Track meals systematically. Look for patterns across multiple exposures to the same food, not single data points. Don't chase a flat glucose line as an end in itself.
- **Microbiome testing:** Most useful as a prompt to increase dietary fibre diversity — aim for 30+ different plant foods per week, which is strongly supported by PREDICT and broader epidemiological data, regardless of your specific microbial profile.
- **DNA testing:** Skip the broad nutrigenomic panel unless you have clinical reason for specific variants. Ask your physician about **APOE** or **MTHFR** if cardiovascular disease or cognitive risk is relevant to your family history.
- **The universal interventions still apply:** No personalised tool yet discovered a population for whom vegetables, resistance training, adequate sleep, and not smoking are bad ideas. The variance is at the margins.

Start with the fundamentals. Use personalised tools to refine, not to replace, evidence-based foundations.
