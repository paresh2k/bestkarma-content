You can eat a perfectly clean diet — no gluten, no dairy, no processed food — and still spend every afternoon looking six months pregnant. The bloating is real, the discomfort is real, and the standard reassurance ("it's probably just IBS") is, for a significant subset of sufferers, wrong. What's actually happening is a bacterial problem in a place bacteria aren't supposed to thrive in large numbers: the small intestine.

Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth — SIBO — is increasingly recognized as one of the most underdiagnosed conditions in gastroenterology. A 2020 meta-analysis by **Ghoshal et al.** in the *Journal of Gastroenterology and Hepatology*, pooling data from 11 studies and over 1,200 patients with IBS, found that roughly **36% of IBS patients tested positive for SIBO** using breath testing — compared to 10% of healthy controls. That gap is striking. It suggests that for millions of people cycling through elimination diets and digestive enzymes, the root cause hasn't been addressed at all.

---

## What Is SIBO, and Why Does It Happen?

The small intestine is not supposed to be a microbial metropolis. The colon hosts trillions of bacteria; the small intestine, by design, keeps populations sparse. Several mechanisms enforce this: stomach acid creates an inhospitable entry point, the ileocecal valve acts as a one-way gate between the small and large intestine, and — critically — a sweeping muscular contraction called the **migrating motor complex (MMC)** runs along the gut every 90 minutes during fasting, clearing debris and bacterial stragglers like a slow housekeeper.

When any of these fail, bacteria accumulate where they shouldn't. **Low stomach acid** (hypochlorhydria), which becomes more common with age and with proton pump inhibitor use, removes the acid barrier. Anatomical issues — surgical adhesions, diverticulosis, strictures — can create stagnant pockets where bacteria pool. Diabetes, hypothyroidism, and scleroderma all impair gut motility, disrupting the MMC. A 2003 study by **Pimentel et al.** in *Digestive Diseases and Sciences* demonstrated that patients with IBS had significantly abnormal MMC activity compared to controls — a finding that reframed IBS, at least in part, as a motility disorder with bacterial consequences.

There are also post-infectious cases. Gastroenteritis from *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, or *E. coli* can trigger an autoimmune response against the cells that coordinate the MMC, a mechanism detailed by **Pimentel and colleagues** in a landmark 2015 paper in *JAMA*. This helps explain why some people develop chronic bloating after a single bout of food poisoning and never quite recover.

## Recognizing the Pattern

SIBO doesn't announce itself with a single dramatic symptom. It tends to accumulate quietly: bloating that worsens through the day and peaks after meals (especially carbohydrate-heavy ones), gas, abdominal distension, alternating constipation and diarrhea, and sometimes belching. What distinguishes SIBO from ordinary digestive sensitivity is the consistency of the postprandial pattern and, in chronic or severe cases, downstream consequences.

Because bacteria in the small intestine feed on nutrients *before* the host can absorb them, prolonged SIBO can cause deficiencies in **fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)**, **vitamin B12**, and **iron**. Bacterial fermentation of bile acids impairs fat digestion, sometimes leading to steatorrhea. A subset of patients report significant cognitive symptoms — the "brain fog" that has become a hallmark complaint, though the mechanism here is less fully characterized. One hypothesis involves elevated levels of D-lactic acid produced by bacterial fermentation, which can be neuroactive in high concentrations.

Importantly, SIBO is not one condition but at least two. **Hydrogen-dominant SIBO** (caused by bacteria that produce hydrogen gas) is associated more with diarrhea. **Methane-dominant SIBO** — sometimes reclassified as intestinal methanogen overgrowth (IMO) because the methane producers are archaea, not bacteria — is strongly associated with constipation. The **2020 North American Consensus** on breath testing, published in *The American Journal of Gastroenterology* by **Rezaie et al.**, formalized these distinctions and updated diagnostic thresholds accordingly.

### The Breath Test

The **lactulose or glucose hydrogen/methane breath test** is the primary non-invasive diagnostic tool. The patient ingests a sugar substrate; bacteria in the small intestine ferment it and produce hydrogen or methane, which is absorbed into the bloodstream, exhaled in the breath, and measured at intervals over 2–3 hours. Elevated hydrogen (≥20 ppm rise from baseline within 90 minutes) or methane (≥10 ppm at any point) suggests overgrowth.

The test is useful but imperfect. False positives occur with rapid intestinal transit; false negatives occur when the bacterial population isn't fermenting the specific substrate. Sensitivity and specificity vary significantly by protocol — a persistent frustration in the field. Some clinicians prefer empiric treatment when the clinical picture is compelling and testing is unavailable or equivocal.

## What the Evidence Says About Treatment

### Rifaximin: The First-Line Antibiotic

**Rifaximin**, a non-absorbable antibiotic that acts locally in the gut, is the most well-studied pharmacological treatment for SIBO. The landmark **TARGET 1 and TARGET 2 trials** (2011, *New England Journal of Medicine*, **Pimentel et al.**) enrolled 1,260 IBS-D patients and found that a 14-day course of rifaximin (550 mg three times daily) produced significantly greater relief of bloating and overall IBS symptoms compared to placebo — with response rates around 40% versus 23%, sustained at 10 weeks. While these trials were in IBS-D rather than SIBO specifically, the mechanism aligns: reducing small intestinal bacterial load.

For methane-dominant cases, rifaximin is typically combined with **neomycin**, because rifaximin alone has limited efficacy against methanogens. A randomized trial by **Low et al.** (2010, *Digestive Diseases and Sciences*) found that the rifaximin-neomycin combination was significantly more effective at normalizing methane breath test results than either antibiotic alone. More recently, **lovastatin lactone** has emerged as an investigational agent against methanogens, though clinical evidence remains preliminary.

### Elemental Diets

An elemental diet — pre-digested nutrients absorbed entirely in the proximal small intestine, leaving nothing for bacteria downstream to ferment — is a powerful but demanding intervention. A 2004 study by **Pimentel et al.** in *Digestive Diseases and Sciences* found that two weeks of exclusive elemental formula normalized breath test results in **80% of patients** who hadn't responded to antibiotics. The mechanism is elegant: starve the bacteria without killing the host. The practical challenge is significant — elemental formulas taste poor and cost is substantial — but for refractory cases, the evidence is compelling.

### Low-FODMAP as Adjunct

The **low-FODMAP diet** (restricting fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, and polyols) doesn't treat SIBO directly, but it reduces the fermentable substrate available to bacteria, providing symptomatic relief. A 2014 RCT by **Halmos et al.** in *Gastroenterology* of 30 IBS patients found that a low-FODMAP diet significantly reduced GI symptoms compared to a typical Australian diet. Most gastroenterologists now use it as a bridge — symptom management during and after antibiotic treatment — rather than a standalone cure.

## The Recurrence Problem

Here is the honest reckoning: SIBO comes back. Recurrence rates of **44% within 9 months** have been reported (**Lauritano et al., 2008, *American Journal of Gastroenterology***), and without addressing the underlying cause, repeat antibiotic courses become the norm. This is where **prokinetics** — agents that stimulate gut motility and restore MMC function — become important.

Low-dose **naltrexone** (LDN), **prucalopride**, and the herbal prokinetic **Iberogast** have all been used for MMC support with varying evidence. The most studied pharmaceutical option is **low-dose erythromycin** (a macrolide antibiotic that also acts as a motilin agonist at low doses), though long-term antibiotic use for prokinesis raises its own concerns. **5-HTP**, a serotonin precursor, has theoretical appeal given serotonin's role in gut motility, but clinical evidence in SIBO-specific populations is sparse.

The pragmatic approach recommended in current clinical practice is to identify and treat the *underlying cause* — address hypochlorhydria, optimize thyroid function, review PPI use — and use prokinetics between antibiotic courses or after treatment to reduce the MMC dysfunction that allows bacterial repopulation.

---

## A Practical Starting Point

If the symptom pattern above sounds familiar, the logical next step is a breath test through your gastroenterologist or, where available, a telehealth GI service. Treatment decisions should follow testing results. A general framework:

- **Hydrogen-dominant SIBO:** Rifaximin 550 mg three times daily for 14 days (prescription required); consider low-FODMAP during treatment to reduce symptoms
- **Methane-dominant (IMO):** Rifaximin + neomycin combination; discuss with a gastroenterologist
- **Refractory or antibiotic-intolerant cases:** Elemental diet for 14–21 days — high burden, high efficacy
- **Maintenance:** Discuss a prokinetic agent with your provider; address root causes (PPI reduction if appropriate, thyroid optimization, motility support)
- **Diet post-treatment:** Gradual FODMAP reintroduction — avoidance alone won't prevent recurrence

This is one of the few areas of gut health where a specific, testable, treatable cause underlies what often gets labeled "functional." That framing matters — not because the diagnosis is a magic fix, but because it points toward mechanisms rather than permanent dietary restriction.
