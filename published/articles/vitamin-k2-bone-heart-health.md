Most people taking vitamin D supplements don't realize they may be solving half the problem. Vitamin D tells your body to absorb calcium. But without vitamin K2, that calcium has nowhere useful to go — and may end up exactly where you don't want it: hardening your arteries while leaving your bones brittle. The irony is that the supplement aisle has spent two decades fixated on vitamin D while K2, its essential partner, sits quietly on the bottom shelf. The Rotterdam Study, which followed 4,807 Dutch adults over ten years, found that people with the highest dietary K2 intake had a **52% lower risk of severe aortic calcification** and a 57% lower risk of dying from cardiovascular disease compared to those with the lowest intake. K1 — the form found in leafy greens — showed no such association. That distinction matters enormously, and understanding it could change how you think about bone and heart health.

---

## K1 vs. K2: Not the Same Vitamin

Vitamin K is not a single compound. **Vitamin K1 (phylloquinone)** is the form found in spinach, kale, and broccoli. It plays a critical role in blood clotting and is efficiently used by the liver for that purpose — but it doesn't stick around in the blood long enough to serve the body's peripheral tissues, including bones and blood vessels.

**Vitamin K2 (menaquinone)** is a family of related compounds differentiated by the length of their side chains, labeled MK-4 through MK-13. The two forms most relevant to human health are:

| Form | Source | Half-life | Bioavailability |
|------|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **MK-4** | Meat, eggs, some dairy | ~1–2 hours | Short; requires multiple doses |
| **MK-7** | Natto, aged cheese, fermented foods | ~3 days | High; once-daily dosing effective |

MK-7 is the form most extensively studied for cardiovascular and bone outcomes, largely because its extended half-life allows it to reach and remain active in extrahepatic tissues — the bones, arterial walls, and soft tissues where K2's biological work actually happens.

---

## What the Evidence Shows

The **Rotterdam Study** (Geleijnse et al., 2004, *Journal of Nutrition*) remains the most cited population study on dietary K2 and cardiovascular risk. In 4,807 participants followed for 10 years, each 10-microgram increment in dietary K2 was associated with a 9% reduction in coronary heart disease mortality. K1 intake was not associated with cardiovascular outcomes. This was an observational study — it can't prove causation — but the dose-response relationship and the specificity of the K2 effect were striking.

The **Prospect-EPIC cohort** (Gast et al., 2009, *Nutrition, Metabolism and Cardiovascular Diseases*) replicated and extended these findings in 16,057 women followed for eight years. Each 10-microgram increment of dietary K2 intake was associated with a **9% reduction in coronary heart disease incidence**, again with no significant effect seen for K1.

For bone health, a 2019 meta-analysis by **Mott et al.** in *Osteoporosis International* pooled data from 19 randomized controlled trials and found that MK-7 supplementation significantly improved bone mineral density at the lumbar spine and femoral neck — the two sites most predictive of fracture risk. The effect was modest but consistent across studies, and notably, it was additive to calcium and vitamin D supplementation rather than redundant with it.

The most compelling bone study is arguably the **MK-7 and osteoporosis trial** by Knapen et al. (2013, *Osteoporosis International*), a three-year randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of 244 healthy postmenopausal women. Those receiving 180 mcg/day of MK-7 showed significantly less decline in bone mineral density and bone strength at the lumbar spine and femoral neck compared to placebo. They also showed improved activation of osteocalcin, the K2-dependent protein central to bone mineralization.

---

## The Mechanism: Where Calcium Goes Matters

To understand why K2 is so consequential, you need to know about two proteins: **osteocalcin** and **matrix Gla protein (MGP)**.

**Osteocalcin** is produced by osteoblasts — the cells that build bone. In its inactive form, it cannot bind calcium. Vitamin K2 activates osteocalcin through a process called **carboxylation**: K2 acts as a cofactor for an enzyme that adds a carboxyl group to glutamate residues on osteocalcin, enabling the protein to grab calcium ions and anchor them into the bone matrix. Without sufficient K2, osteocalcin remains undercarboxylated and ineffective, even when calcium is abundant.

**Matrix Gla protein** is produced by vascular smooth muscle cells and chondrocytes (cartilage cells). Its job is the opposite: it acts as a potent inhibitor of soft-tissue calcification. Like osteocalcin, MGP only works when carboxylated by vitamin K2. Undercarboxylated MGP cannot bind and neutralize calcium, allowing it to precipitate in arterial walls — a process that directly contributes to arterial stiffness and cardiovascular risk. A landmark study by **Schurgers et al.** (2005, *Arteriosclerosis, Thrombosis, and Vascular Biology*) found that undercarboxylated MGP levels predicted arterial calcification progression in adults, and that MK-7 supplementation effectively reduced circulating uncarboxylated MGP.

Think of it this way: vitamin D opens the gate for calcium to enter the body. Vitamin K2 is the traffic director — routing calcium to bones and away from arteries. Running one without the other leaves the system poorly coordinated.

---

## The Vitamin D Connection — and What We Don't Know

The synergy between vitamin D and K2 is mechanistically logical and has become a significant focus in research, but it's worth being precise about the evidence here. Vitamin D increases the expression of both osteocalcin and MGP, which means it actually increases the *demand* for K2 as a carboxylating cofactor. Supplementing with high-dose vitamin D without adequate K2 theoretically accelerates the production of inactive, undercarboxylated proteins — potentially promoting soft-tissue calcification rather than preventing it. This hypothesis has been explored by **Masterjohn** (2007, *Journal of the American College of Nutrition*) and others, and it provides a compelling rationale for combined supplementation.

However, we don't yet have large randomized controlled trials directly testing the combination of vitamin D and K2 supplementation on hard cardiovascular outcomes like heart attack or stroke. The VITAL trial (Manson et al., 2019, *NEJM*), which tested vitamin D3 at 2,000 IU/day, did not include a K2 arm. What we have is mechanistic plausibility, population data, and smaller intervention trials — enough to take seriously, not enough to call definitive. Epistemic honesty requires acknowledging that gap while recognizing the favorable benefit-to-risk profile of K2 supplementation.

## Food Sources and Supplementation

### Getting K2 from Food

The richest dietary source of K2, by a wide margin, is **natto** — a Japanese fermented soybean dish that contains roughly 1,000 mcg of MK-7 per 100g serving. A single small serving (about 40g) would dwarf the intake levels associated with benefit in the Rotterdam Study. The barrier for most Western adults is purely palatability; natto has a pungent, sticky texture that inspires strong opinions.

More accessible sources include:

- **Aged hard cheeses** (Gouda, Brie, Edam): 10–80 mcg MK-4/MK-7 per 100g
- **Egg yolks** (pasture-raised): ~15–30 mcg MK-4 per yolk
- **Chicken liver**: moderate MK-4 content
- **Fermented foods** (some sauerkraut, kefir): variable MK content depending on bacterial strains

Most Western diets deliver somewhere between 10–30 mcg/day of K2 — well below the levels studied in intervention trials.

### Supplementation Protocol

Based on the available intervention trials — particularly the Knapen 2013 trial and the systematic review by Mott et al. — the following represents a defensible evidence-based approach:

- **Form:** MK-7 (preferred over MK-4 for once-daily dosing and tissue retention)
- **Dose:** 100–200 mcg/day (Knapen used 180 mcg; most supplement products offer 100–200 mcg)
- **Timing:** Take with a fat-containing meal — K2 is fat-soluble and requires dietary fat for absorption
- **Pairing:** If supplementing vitamin D3, K2 co-supplementation is mechanistically rational; common pairings are 1,000–2,000 IU D3 with 100–200 mcg K2 MK-7
- **Anticoagulant caution:** Individuals taking warfarin (Coumadin) should consult their physician before adding any form of vitamin K, as it interacts with the drug's mechanism of action

The safety profile of MK-7 at these doses is reassuring — no adverse effects have been observed in trials at 180–360 mcg/day.
