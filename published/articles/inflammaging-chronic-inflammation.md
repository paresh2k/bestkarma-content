In 2000, Italian gerontologist Claudio Franceschi published a paper in the *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* that introduced a word that would reshape how geroscientists — researchers who study the biology of aging — think about disease.[^1]

**Inflammaging**: a portmanteau of inflammation and aging. The concept: chronic, low-grade, systemic inflammation is not merely a consequence of aging, but one of its primary *drivers* — and the common upstream cause of most major age-related diseases.

Two decades of subsequent research have validated this framework comprehensively. Chronic inflammation is now understood to be the engine running underneath cardiovascular disease, Alzheimer's, Type 2 diabetes, cancer, osteoarthritis, and sarcopenia simultaneously.

## Acute vs. Chronic Inflammation: The Critical Distinction

Inflammation is not inherently damaging — it is essential. Acute inflammation is the body's first-response repair system: when you cut your finger, bruise your knee, or catch an infection, the inflammatory cascade floods the area with cytokines, neutrophils, and macrophages to clear debris and initiate healing. This is biological excellence.

The problem is chronic, low-grade systemic inflammation: a state where inflammatory signals are persistently elevated throughout the body without a specific threat to resolve. Unlike acute inflammation — which is intense, localized, and short-lived — chronic inflammation is:

- **System-wide**, not localized
- **Low-level**, flying below the threshold of obvious symptoms
- **Persistent**, measured in years to decades
- **Self-reinforcing**, generating cellular damage that drives further inflammation

The primary markers of chronic inflammation are measurable in standard blood tests: **high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP)**, **interleukin-6 (IL-6)**, **tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α)**, and **fibrinogen**. Most people have no idea what their baseline levels are. Increasingly, longevity physicians regard these markers as essential screening tools.

## The Mechanisms Connecting Inflammation to Disease

**Cardiovascular disease**: Paul Ridker at Harvard Medical School spent years building the case that inflammation — not just cholesterol — is a primary driver of atherosclerosis. His 2002 landmark study in *Circulation* documented that hsCRP predicted future cardiovascular events independently of LDL cholesterol.[^2] His subsequent JUPITER trial (2008, *New England Journal of Medicine*) demonstrated that statin treatment in patients with elevated hsCRP (but normal LDL) reduced cardiovascular events by 44% — suggesting that the anti-inflammatory effect of statins, not just their cholesterol reduction, was clinically meaningful.[^3]

**Alzheimer's and neurodegeneration**: The "inflammatory hypothesis of Alzheimer's" has gained significant traction over the past decade. Microglia — the brain's immune cells — when persistently activated by amyloid beta and tau protein accumulation, drive a neuroinflammatory state that damages neurons beyond what the protein accumulation alone would cause. A 2020 review in *Nature Neuroscience* documented that chronic systemic inflammation accelerates neurodegeneration through multiple pathways, including IL-6-mediated blood-brain barrier disruption and microglial hyperactivation.[^4]

**Cancer**: Chronic inflammation creates a pro-tumorigenic microenvironment. NF-κB (nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells) — one of the master regulators of inflammation — when chronically activated, promotes cell survival, proliferation, invasion, and angiogenesis. It also suppresses tumor-suppressor genes. H. pylori-driven gastric cancer and hepatitis-driven liver cancer are extreme examples, but chronic low-grade systemic inflammation appears to modestly but meaningfully increase cancer risk across multiple sites.

**Type 2 Diabetes**: Fat tissue — particularly visceral (abdominal) fat — is now understood to be an endocrine and immune organ, not just a storage depot. Adipose tissue in excess releases pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β) and adipokines that impair insulin signaling. The resulting inflammation is both a consequence of metabolic dysfunction and a driver of further insulin resistance, creating a reinforcing cycle.

## What Drives Inflammaging

The sources of chronic low-grade inflammation in modern life are well-characterized:

**1. The Microbiome Disruption**
A dysbiotic gut — characterized by low microbial diversity and compromised intestinal barrier integrity — is one of the strongest drivers of systemic inflammation. Bacterial endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide) from gram-negative bacteria translocating across a "leaky" gut epithelium into the bloodstream triggers a persistent LPS-mediated inflammatory state. Ultra-processed food, low dietary fiber, antibiotics, and chronic stress all drive dysbiosis and intestinal permeability.

**2. Visceral Adiposity**
Excess abdominal fat is independently inflammatory. Adipose tissue contains immune cells (macrophages, T cells) that, in overweight conditions, shift toward a pro-inflammatory phenotype. Even modest reductions in visceral fat (through diet, exercise, or both) produce significant reductions in circulating inflammatory markers.

**3. Chronic Psychological Stress**
The HPA axis drives cortisol production under stress. While acute cortisol is anti-inflammatory, chronic cortisol excess paradoxically promotes inflammation through glucocorticoid receptor resistance — cells become less sensitive to cortisol's anti-inflammatory signaling while the HPA axis remains chronically active.

**4. Sleep Deprivation**
Even one night of partial sleep deprivation produces measurable increases in IL-6, TNF-α, and CRP. Chronic poor sleep (less than 7 hours) is associated with persistently elevated inflammatory markers. The proposed mechanism involves disruption of the glymphatic clearance system and increased sympathetic nervous system activation.

**5. Sedentary Behavior and Lack of Exercise**
Regular aerobic exercise has potent anti-inflammatory effects — primarily through myokines (cytokines released by contracting muscle). IL-6 released by working muscle during exercise paradoxically has anti-inflammatory systemic effects (distinct from macrophage-derived IL-6, which is pro-inflammatory). Regular exercise also reduces visceral fat — removing one of the primary inflammatory drivers.

**6. Dietary Pro-Inflammatory Patterns**
Ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates, industrial seed oils high in omega-6 fatty acids, and advanced glycation end products (AGEs, formed during high-heat cooking of certain foods) all independently activate inflammatory pathways. By contrast, a Mediterranean dietary pattern — characterized by abundant vegetables, olive oil, legumes, fish, nuts, and whole grains — consistently reduces circulating inflammatory markers across dozens of trials.

## Measuring Your Inflammation

The two most clinically accessible biomarkers:

**High-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP)**:
- < 1.0 mg/L: low cardiovascular risk
- 1.0–3.0 mg/L: moderate risk
- > 3.0 mg/L: high risk (in the absence of acute infection)

**Interleukin-6 (IL-6)**: A more sensitive marker than CRP, as CRP is produced downstream of IL-6. Normal range varies by lab, but > 3.5 pg/mL at rest is associated with elevated disease risk.

These are standard blood tests. Most physicians do not order them routinely — asking your doctor to include them in your annual labs is a reasonable request supported by substantial evidence.

## The Anti-Inflammatory Lifestyle: Ranked by Evidence

**1. Mediterranean dietary pattern** — the most consistently replicated dietary intervention for reducing CRP, IL-6, and cardiovascular events. Key components: extra virgin olive oil (oleocanthal has direct COX-inhibiting anti-inflammatory activity), fatty fish (EPA and DHA reduce arachidonic acid-derived pro-inflammatory eicosanoids), abundant vegetables and legumes (fiber feeding anti-inflammatory gut bacteria), polyphenols from colorful plants.

**2. Regular moderate exercise** — 150+ minutes per week of moderate aerobic activity produces a 35–50% reduction in CRP in randomized trials. The effect is independent of weight loss.

**3. Sleep optimization** — 7–9 hours, consistent schedule, dark and cool room. (See our article on sleep architecture.)

**4. Stress management** — MBSR, meditation, and HRV biofeedback all produce measurable reductions in inflammatory markers across RCTs. The pathway is HPA axis modulation.

**5. Gut microbiome support** — dietary fiber (target 30g+ daily), fermented foods (yogurt, kimchi, kefir), limiting antibiotics to medically necessary use.

**6. Omega-3 supplementation** — EPA and DHA from fish oil at 2–4g/day has been shown in meta-analyses to significantly reduce CRP and IL-6. The 2019 VITAL trial (NEJM) found omega-3 supplementation reduced cardiovascular events in people without prior cardiovascular disease.

The research on inflammaging offers one of the clearest convergence points in all of medicine: the same lifestyle interventions that reduce chronic inflammation also reduce the risk of virtually every major cause of death and disability. This is not coincidence. Inflammation is the shared upstream mechanism.

Your inflammation level is not destiny. It responds to what you do.
