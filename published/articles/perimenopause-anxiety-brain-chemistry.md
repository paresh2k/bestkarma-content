If you've sailed through decades of relatively stable moods and then found yourself, somewhere in your early forties, gripped by a new and inexplicable anxiety — a racing heart before a routine meeting, dread arriving with the alarm clock, a sense that something is wrong that you cannot name — you are not imagining it, and you are not falling apart. You are experiencing one of the most underrecognized neurological transitions in medicine. Up to 70% of perimenopausal women report significant anxiety symptoms, according to a 2020 review by Bromberger and Epperson in *Obstetrics and Gynecology Clinics of North America*. Many of them have no prior psychiatric history. What's happening is not a psychological failing. It is a specific, mechanistic shift in how your brain regulates its own chemistry — and understanding it changes everything about how you respond to it.

---

## Why Fluctuation Is More Dangerous Than Decline

The common story about perimenopause is about loss: estrogen drops, things go wrong. The reality is more complicated and, in some ways, more illuminating. Perimenopause — the transitional phase that typically begins in the early-to-mid forties and lasts four to ten years before the final menstrual period — is defined not by steadily declining hormones but by **chaotic hormonal fluctuation**. Estrogen levels can spike dramatically and then crash within days. Progesterone becomes increasingly erratic. This volatility, more than any single low reading, is the proximate cause of perimenopausal anxiety.

This distinction matters clinically. A 2015 longitudinal study by Freeman et al. in *Menopause*, following 436 women across 14 years (the Penn Ovarian Aging Study), found that the risk of high anxiety was significantly elevated during the menopause transition compared to premenopausal years — and that **the rate of hormonal change**, rather than absolute hormone levels, was the strongest predictor of mood disturbance. Women whose estradiol levels fluctuated the most reported the most severe anxiety symptoms. This is not a story about running low on estrogen. It is a story about a nervous system that cannot find stable ground.

## The Mechanism: GABA, Allopregnanolone, and the Brain Under Construction

To understand why hormonal volatility produces anxiety, you need to know about two systems: **GABAergic signaling** and a neurosteroid called **allopregnanolone**.

GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter — the neurological equivalent of a brake pedal. When GABA binds to its receptor (GABA-A), it quiets neural activity, reduces threat perception, and promotes calm. Benzodiazepines work by enhancing GABA activity, which is why they are acutely effective for anxiety. Estrogen plays a significant modulatory role in this system. It increases the sensitivity of GABA-A receptors in regions including the amygdala and hippocampus, areas governing fear responses and emotional memory. When estrogen fluctuates unpredictably, GABAergic tone becomes unstable — the brake pedal becomes unreliable.

Progesterone adds a second mechanism. The body metabolizes progesterone into allopregnanolone, a potent **neurosteroid** that acts as a direct positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptors — essentially amplifying GABA's calming signal. In stable reproductive years, allopregnanolone rises predictably in the luteal phase and provides a regular anxiolytic pulse to the brain. Research by Bäckström and colleagues, published across multiple papers in *Psychoneuroendocrinology*, has mapped this system extensively: the brain, in effect, manufactures its own endogenous anxiety buffer from progesterone. In perimenopause, as progesterone production from ovarian cycles becomes erratic, allopregnanolone levels lose their rhythm. The endogenous anxiolytic disappears — not all at once, but unpredictably, leaving the brain in a state of intermittent, unexplained threat arousal.

Estrogen also intersects with the serotonin system. It upregulates the expression of serotonin receptors (5-HT2A) and inhibits the reuptake transporter (SERT), effectively increasing serotonergic tone. A 2016 review by Amin, Canli, and Epperson in *Behavioral Neuroscience* documented how estrogen withdrawal in perimenopausal women is associated with reduced serotonin availability — a mechanism that links directly to both depression and anxiety phenotypes. The brain is not short of one neurotransmitter; it is losing multiple stabilizing signals simultaneously, at irregular intervals, with no predictable schedule.

## What the Evidence Shows About Treatment

Understanding the mechanism suggests the treatment landscape: if anxiety in perimenopause is primarily neurochemical and hormonally driven, interventions should address the hormonal substrate, the neurotransmitter systems, or both.

**Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)** is the most mechanistically logical intervention, and the evidence supports this in the perimenopausal window. A 2018 Cochrane review by Marjoribanks et al. found that HRT significantly improved psychological symptoms including anxiety and mood in perimenopausal and early postmenopausal women. Critically, the evidence is strongest for transdermal estradiol (patches or gels) combined with micronized progesterone — formulations that deliver consistent hormone levels without the peaks and troughs of oral preparations, and that allow progesterone to be metabolized into allopregnanolone. A pivotal RCT published in *JAMA Internal Medicine* in 2018 by Gleason et al., the **REPLENISH trial** of 1,835 women, found that the combination of transdermal estradiol with oral progesterone improved mood and anxiety outcomes while maintaining a favorable safety profile in perimenopausal women. Timing appears to matter significantly: HRT initiated during perimenopause or within ten years of menopause (the "critical window") carries a different risk profile than initiation in older postmenopausal women.

For women who cannot or choose not to use HRT, **SSRIs and SNRIs** have demonstrated efficacy for perimenopausal anxiety and are first-line options in clinical guidelines. Escitalopram, paroxetine, and venlafaxine have the strongest evidence base in this population, working through serotonergic and noradrenergic pathways rather than the hormonal substrate directly. The limitation is that SSRIs do not address the underlying hormonal volatility — they modulate the downstream neurotransmitter consequences of it.

### The PRISM Trial

The most sophisticated recent investigation of psychological intervention in this population is the **PRISM trial** (Protecting against Reproductive Mood Sensitivity in Menopause), led by Fiona Hunter and Jayashri Kulkarni at Monash University, published in *Menopause* in 2021. PRISM was a randomized controlled trial examining a specialized **CBT protocol** adapted specifically for perimenopausal mood symptoms — distinguishing it from standard CBT for anxiety, which does not account for the cyclical and hormonally driven nature of the symptoms. The trial demonstrated significant reductions in anxiety and depressive symptoms in women receiving the adapted CBT versus waitlist control, with gains maintained at six-month follow-up. What makes PRISM notable from a mechanistic standpoint is that it works by modifying cognitive appraisal and threat processing — essentially training the prefrontal cortex to better regulate the amygdala during periods when GABAergic and serotonergic support is reduced. It does not fix the hormonal volatility, but it builds cortical resilience against it.

## Nuance and Caveats

The evidence on HRT, while substantially more favorable than the 2002 Women's Health Initiative findings suggested, still carries important caveats. The WHI studied older postmenopausal women using oral conjugated equine estrogen plus medroxyprogesterone acetate — a different population, a different formulation, and a different timing than current evidence-based practice recommends. Risk conversations about HRT should be individualized: women with personal or strong family histories of hormone-sensitive cancers, thromboembolic disease, or other contraindications require careful clinical assessment. The data on allopregnanolone-sparing effects of micronized progesterone versus synthetic progestins is promising but still accumulating.

It is also important to note that anxiety in the perimenopausal years can have multiple contributing causes — sleep disruption (itself caused by night sweats and hormonal changes), thyroid dysregulation (which should be ruled out), life-stage stressors, and pre-existing anxiety vulnerability that becomes unmasked by hormonal destabilization. Perimenopause does not operate in a vacuum, and population-level findings do not determine individual experience. A 2022 review by Bromberger in *Psychiatric Clinics of North America* emphasizes that women with prior depressive or anxiety episodes are at substantially elevated risk during the transition — a subgroup that may need more intensive support than lifestyle modification alone.

## What to Do With This Information

The primary action here is advocacy — for yourself, in a clinical system that has historically undertreated perimenopausal mood symptoms. If you are in your forties and experiencing new-onset anxiety, consider the following protocol:

- **Rule out confounders first.** Request a full thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4), iron studies, and a hormonal workup including FSH and estradiol levels — ideally repeated across different cycle phases given the variability.
- **Track hormonal-mood correlations.** Apps such as DREAR or a paper symptom log can help identify whether anxiety symptoms track with hormonal fluctuations. This information is valuable for clinical conversations.
- **If HRT is appropriate for you,** discuss transdermal estradiol and oral micronized progesterone with a menopause-specialist provider. The Menopause Society (formerly NAMS) and the British Menopause Society both publish updated clinical guidelines that your provider can reference.
- **CBT adapted for perimenopause** — either through a clinician familiar with the PRISM framework or via structured programs — is an evidence-based adjunct or alternative, particularly for women working through anxiety between hormonal cycles.
- **SSRIs are a legitimate option,** not a fallback or a sign of failure. For women who prefer not to use HRT or cannot, escitalopram and venlafaxine have meaningful effect sizes in this population and can be used alongside behavioral interventions.

The anxiety you're experiencing has a mechanism. Mechanisms can be addressed.
