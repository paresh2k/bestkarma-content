Three decades after the War on Drugs effectively ended psychedelic research, psychiatry finds itself in an unusual position: some of the most promising results in treatment-resistant mental illness are coming from compounds that remain federally illegal, administered in carefully monitored clinical sessions lasting six to eight hours, with therapists sitting quietly in the room while patients wear eye shades and listen to curated playlists. The science is real. The results, in some cases, are striking. But the path from compelling trial data to something you can actually access is bumpier than the headlines suggest — and the evidence has meaningful limits that are easy to miss.

Here is an honest account of where things stand.

---

## Psilocybin for Depression: Genuine Signal, Genuine Uncertainty

The study that put psilocybin on psychiatry's radar for major depressive disorder came from **Robin Carhart-Harris and colleagues at Johns Hopkins**, published in *JAMA Psychiatry* in 2021. Fifty-nine adults with moderate-to-severe MDD — not treatment-resistant, just unresponsive to current care or unwilling to pursue it — received two psilocybin sessions two weeks apart, each preceded and followed by preparatory and integration therapy sessions. At the four-week follow-up, **71% of participants showed a significant response** (≥50% reduction in GRID-HAMD scores), and **54% were in remission**. These are numbers that would be remarkable for any antidepressant.

More recently, **COMPASS Pathways** completed what is, to date, the largest randomized controlled trial of psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression: the Phase 2b **COMP360** trial, published in *The New England Journal of Medicine* in 2022 by **Goodwin and colleagues**. This was a rigorous trial — 233 participants across 22 sites in 10 countries, randomized to receive a single dose of 1mg, 10mg, or 25mg of synthetic psilocybin (COMP360). The 25mg group showed a statistically significant reduction in depression scores at three weeks compared to the 1mg control group. The effect size was clinically meaningful. But by 12 weeks, the advantage was less clear, and the **relapse rate was notable**: roughly a third of initial responders had relapsed. The 10mg group showed no significant benefit over control.

What does this tell us? Psilocybin can produce rapid, profound antidepressant effects — but durability is a legitimate open question. A single session may not be sufficient for sustained remission. The COMPASS data has prompted serious discussion about maintenance dosing, booster sessions, and how to structure the therapeutic container around the pharmacological experience. The FDA granted psilocybin **Breakthrough Therapy designation** for treatment-resistant depression, which accelerates review — but a Phase 3 trial has not yet been completed, and approval is not imminent.

## MDMA for PTSD: A More Complicated Picture

MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD had an extraordinary run of Phase 2 results. The **Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS)** published a Phase 2 pooled analysis in *Nature Medicine* (Mitchell et al., 2021) showing that **67% of participants who received MDMA-assisted therapy no longer met diagnostic criteria for PTSD** at follow-up, compared to 32% in the placebo-plus-therapy group. These were people with severe, chronic PTSD — military veterans, sexual assault survivors, first responders — who had not responded to conventional treatment.

The Phase 3 trials — **MAPP1** and **MAPP2** — produced more mixed results, and the path to FDA approval turned significantly rougher. In June 2024, the FDA issued a **Complete Response Letter rejecting the MDMA therapy application**, citing concerns about functional unblinding (patients generally know if they've received MDMA), the difficulty of interpreting self-reported outcomes under drug influence, and questions about trial conduct at some sites. The agency also requested an additional Phase 3 trial.

This is not evidence that MDMA therapy doesn't work. It is evidence that **proving it to regulatory standards is harder than the earlier data suggested**. The functional unblinding problem is real and not unique to psychedelics — it affects any trial of a drug with perceptible effects — but regulators are right to ask hard questions about what the signal actually means when patients can almost certainly tell what they received. The field is actively developing better trial designs. The story is not over, but the timeline to approval is now years longer than it appeared in 2022.

## Ketamine and Esketamine: The One That's Already Approved

While psilocybin and MDMA navigate trials, **ketamine has been available as an off-label treatment for depression since the early 2000s**, and its close chemical relative **esketamine (Spravato)** received FDA approval in 2019 for treatment-resistant depression and, later, major depressive disorder with acute suicidal ideation.

The esketamine evidence base is substantial. The pivotal **TRANSFORM trials** (Popova et al., 2019, *JAMA Psychiatry*) were randomized, double-blind, active-controlled studies showing that intranasal esketamine plus a newly initiated oral antidepressant produced significantly greater reduction in depression symptoms at 28 days compared to placebo plus antidepressant. The effect was meaningful, though the long-term maintenance data is more nuanced — relapse rates after discontinuation are significant.

Ketamine's mechanism is distinct from classical psychedelics: it works primarily as an **NMDA receptor antagonist**, blocking glutamate activity in a way that rapidly triggers downstream neuroplasticity. The effects are often described as dissociative rather than psychedelic, and the experience is shorter (45–60 minutes) and less dependent on therapeutic set and setting than psilocybin or MDMA. IV ketamine clinics have proliferated across the US, operating legally and offering infusion series for depression, anxiety, PTSD, and chronic pain — with widely varying protocols and quality.

## What These Compounds Share: The Neuroplasticity Hypothesis

Despite different chemical structures and receptor profiles, psilocybin, MDMA, and ketamine may share a common thread worth understanding.

**Psilocybin** acts on serotonin 5-HT2A receptors, predominantly in the prefrontal cortex. One of its most consistent neuroimaging findings is **quieting of the default mode network (DMN)** — the brain's self-referential "background noise" associated with rumination, narrative self, and the kind of rigid negative thought patterns characteristic of depression and PTSD. Research by **Carhart-Harris and colleagues** (*Proceedings of the Royal Society B*, 2014) proposed that this DMN disruption creates a window of **increased neural flexibility**, where entrenched patterns become temporarily malleable. More recent work has shown that psilocybin promotes **dendritic spine growth** in rodent prefrontal cortex within hours of administration (Shao et al., *Neuron*, 2021) — a structural marker of neuroplasticity.

**Ketamine** achieves something related through a different route. By blocking NMDA receptors, it produces a **rapid burst of glutamate activity**, which activates AMPA receptors and triggers BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) release. BDNF is essentially fertilizer for synaptic connections. This is why ketamine's antidepressant effects can appear within hours — far faster than SSRIs, which require weeks. The working hypothesis: both psilocybin and ketamine are opening a temporary **critical period** of neuroplasticity, during which therapeutic work and new learning can take hold more readily.

**MDMA** operates differently again — it floods the synapse with serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine and appears to reduce amygdala reactivity to threat cues while increasing feelings of social trust and openness. The therapeutic model for PTSD is that this pharmacological state allows patients to process traumatic memories with reduced fear response, making the memories available for reconsolidation and integration.

## What the Evidence Doesn't Yet Support

A few things worth stating plainly:

The evidence does not support **recreational or unsupervised use as a mental health intervention**. Every positive trial result has occurred in a structured therapeutic context with trained facilitators, careful screening, preparation, and integration. The compound alone is not the treatment.

The evidence does not yet tell us **who is most likely to benefit or be harmed**. Participants with personal or family history of psychosis, bipolar I disorder, or certain cardiac conditions have been systematically excluded from trials. The safety profile in those populations is genuinely unknown.

The evidence does not support **microdosing as a validated therapy**. The clinical enthusiasm around sub-perceptual doses of psilocybin is running well ahead of the data. A 2022 randomized controlled trial by **Szigeti and colleagues** (*eLife*) using a self-blinding design found no significant benefit of microdosing over placebo on primary measures.

## Current Access Pathways

| Compound | Legal Status (US) | Access Route |
|---|---|---|
| Esketamine (Spravato) | FDA-approved | Certified prescriber, in-office administration |
| IV Ketamine | Legal off-label | Ketamine clinics, psychiatrists |
| Psilocybin | Schedule I (federal); legal in OR, CO | Oregon licensed facilitators; clinical trials |
| MDMA | Schedule I | Clinical trials only (ClinicalTrials.gov) |

If you or someone you know is considering any of these treatments, **ClinicalTrials.gov** lists all active studies recruiting participants. For psilocybin, the **Oregon Psilocybin Services program** began licensing facilitators in 2023 and provides a legal, regulated access pathway for adults without a medical diagnosis requirement. Colorado's program is in development. Ketamine clinics vary enormously in quality — look for board-certified psychiatrists who integrate psychotherapy, not just infusion centers focused on volume.

The science here is young, fast-moving, and genuinely exciting. It also requires more intellectual honesty than it usually gets.
