Something remarkable happened in early 2024 that barely made it past the health news cycle: a meta-analysis so large it should have changed how every psychiatrist and GP thinks about first-line treatment for depression. It didn't get nearly enough attention. Here's what it found — and what you can do with it.

---

## The Study That Should Have Made Headlines

In February 2024, **Michael Noetel** and colleagues published a meta-analysis in *The BMJ* that pooled data from **218 randomized controlled trials** covering **14,170 participants** with depression and anxiety. The finding was blunt: exercise works about as well as antidepressants, and in some comparisons, it works better. Not "exercise is good for mood." Not "exercise may help as an adjunct." As well as medication. For major depression.

The effect sizes weren't marginal. Compared to active controls, **walking and jogging produced a standardized mean difference (SMD) of –0.62** for depression — a medium-to-large effect that eclipsed yoga (–0.55), strength training (–0.49), and mixed aerobic exercise (–0.43). To put those numbers in context: the typical SMD for SSRIs versus placebo in well-matched trials hovers around –0.30 to –0.40. Exercise, particularly vigorous aerobic exercise, appears to outperform the drugs we've organized entire treatment systems around.

This isn't the first study to make this case. A **2023 meta-analysis by Recchia and colleagues in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine*** examined 41 RCTs and found that exercise reduced depressive symptoms with an effect size comparable to pharmacotherapy, with the strongest effects in clinical populations — people with diagnosed major depressive disorder, not just subclinical low mood. And a landmark **2016 trial by Blumenthal et al.** (a follow-up to his original 1999 JAMA paper) tracked 202 adults with MDD randomized to supervised aerobic exercise, home-based exercise, sertraline, or placebo — and found that after 16 weeks, the exercise and medication groups were statistically indistinguishable.

## Why Exercise Works: The Biology

Understanding *why* exercise functions as an antidepressant — not just that it does — is what separates a useful intervention from a vague lifestyle recommendation.

### The BDNF Connection

The most well-characterized mechanism runs through **brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF)**. BDNF is essentially fertilizer for neurons — it promotes neuronal survival, growth, and the formation of new synaptic connections. Depression is associated with reduced BDNF levels, particularly in the hippocampus, the brain region most implicated in memory, emotion regulation, and stress response. One of the most consistent findings in biological psychiatry is that antidepressants — SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs — all increase BDNF expression over weeks of use. So does exercise, and possibly faster.

A **2013 review by Szuhany, Bugatti, and Otto in the *Journal of Psychiatric Research*** pooled 29 studies and found that exercise produced a significant and robust increase in circulating BDNF, with the largest effects seen after aerobic exercise at moderate-to-high intensity. The elevated BDNF then promotes **hippocampal neurogenesis** — the growth of new neurons — in a brain region that physically shrinks in chronic depression. A **2021 study by Kandola et al. in *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*** tied this neurogenic effect specifically to the antidepressant benefit, suggesting that the mood effects of exercise and the structural brain changes are not coincidental but causally linked.

### Regulating Stress at the Root

Exercise also acts on the **HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis** — the system that governs your cortisol stress response. In people with depression, this axis is frequently dysregulated: chronically elevated cortisol suppresses BDNF, disrupts sleep architecture, and feeds a self-reinforcing loop of anxiety and low mood. Regular aerobic exercise has been shown to normalize HPA reactivity — not by suppressing cortisol acutely (intense exercise actually spikes it briefly), but by training the system to mount an appropriate response and recover efficiently. Think of it as recalibrating the thermostat rather than turning off the heat.

### The Inflammation Piece

The third major pathway involves **neuroinflammation**. A robust subset of depressive illness — estimates range from 30 to 50% of cases — involves elevated inflammatory markers, particularly IL-6, TNF-α, and CRP. These cytokines cross the blood-brain barrier and disrupt serotonin synthesis, reduce BDNF expression, and alter reward circuitry. Exercise has measurable anti-inflammatory effects: a **2019 review by Gleeson et al. in *Nature Reviews Immunology*** found that regular moderate-intensity exercise reduces circulating inflammatory cytokines, with the effect scaling with training consistency rather than any single session.

## Dose, Type, and Timing

If exercise is medicine, what's the prescription?

The Noetel 2024 analysis offers the clearest answer to date on **type and dose**. The ranking by effect size for depression:

| Exercise Type | Effect Size (SMD) vs. Active Control |
|---|---|
| Walking / Jogging | –0.62 |
| Yoga | –0.55 |
| Strength Training | –0.49 |
| Mixed Aerobic | –0.43 |
| Tai Chi / Qigong | –0.42 |

Higher intensity consistently outperformed lower intensity for depression specifically. **Walking worked, but jogging worked better.** This matters for recommendations: the wellness-world tendency to suggest "just go for gentle walks" undersells what the data supports.

On **dose**, the evidence points to a sweet spot of **3–5 sessions per week, 30–45 minutes per session, at moderate-to-vigorous intensity** (roughly 60–80% of maximum heart rate, or an effort level where you can speak but not comfortably hold a full conversation). Below this threshold, effects are detectable but weaker. Above it — particularly with very high-volume training — some studies observe a J-curve, with excessive training associated with increased anxiety and HPA dysregulation in vulnerable individuals.

**Timing** is less studied but worth noting. A **2023 study by Svensson et al. in *Translational Psychiatry*** found that morning exercise produced greater improvements in depressive symptoms than evening exercise, potentially due to its interaction with cortisol's natural diurnal rhythm — exercise in the morning amplifies the cortisol awakening response in a way that may sharpen circadian entrainment. That said, the best time to exercise is almost certainly whenever you'll actually do it consistently.

## Exercise vs. Medication: What the Evidence Actually Says

The Noetel analysis directly compared exercise to antidepressants and found **no statistically significant difference** in effect size when head-to-head comparisons were pooled. Blumenthal's earlier trials found the same. This is not a claim that exercise should replace medication — it's a claim that exercise deserves to be treated as a first-line option, not an afterthought.

More interesting is the **combination effect**. The Blumenthal 1999 and 2016 data suggest that supervised exercise plus medication doesn't clearly outperform either alone — but the exercise group had significantly lower relapse rates at 10-month follow-up. Patients who exercised were less likely to re-enter a depressive episode. The proposed mechanism: exercise builds durable biological changes (hippocampal volume, HPA calibration, BDNF baseline) rather than merely managing symptoms acutely.

---

## Caveats: Who May Not Respond, and What We Still Don't Know

Epistemic honesty requires acknowledging the limits. The Noetel analysis, like most in this space, is heterogeneous — different populations, different exercise protocols, different outcome measures, different control conditions. Effect sizes shrink when only the highest-quality trials are analyzed, and several landmark trials have used supervised exercise (which improves adherence dramatically compared to real-world conditions).

**Exercise is not effective for everyone.** People with severe, treatment-resistant depression, bipolar disorder with active episodes, or psychotic features were largely excluded from these trials. Anhedonia — the inability to experience pleasure — can make initiating exercise feel impossible, creating a cruel paradox: the people who most need the intervention are often least able to access it. **Starting very small (5–10 minutes of walking) and working with behavioral activation therapy frameworks may help bridge this gap.** Medication or therapy may need to come first before exercise becomes feasible.

There is also meaningful individual variation in the BDNF response to exercise, partially explained by a common genetic variant in the **BDNF gene (Val66Met polymorphism)** that blunts the exercise-induced BDNF increase in roughly 30% of the population. This doesn't mean exercise won't help them — the HPA and anti-inflammatory pathways still operate — but it may explain why some people respond more robustly than others.

## The Practical Protocol

If you want to use exercise therapeutically for depression — whether as a standalone intervention, an adjunct to medication, or a protective factor against relapse — the evidence supports the following:

- **Type:** Prioritize aerobic exercise. Walking is the minimum viable dose; jogging, cycling, swimming, or rowing at moderate-to-vigorous intensity produce larger effects. Strength training and yoga are meaningful complements but should not replace aerobic work if your primary goal is mood.
- **Frequency:** 3–5 days per week. Fewer than 3 sessions weekly shows attenuated effects in the meta-analytic data.
- **Duration:** 30–45 minutes per session. The dose-response curve flattens beyond 60 minutes.
- **Intensity:** Aim for 60–80% of max heart rate. A simple proxy: you can hold a short conversation but couldn't sustain it comfortably.
- **Consistency over heroics:** Six months of three moderate runs per week will outperform two months of daily intense training followed by burnout. The BDNF and neurogenic effects require sustained stimulus.
- **Reduce friction:** Supervised exercise outperforms home-based in RCTs. A running group, a coach, or a committed training partner dramatically improves adherence — and the social component may carry its own therapeutic benefit.

If you are currently taking antidepressants, do not alter your medication without consulting your prescriber. Exercise as an adjunct is supported by evidence; unilateral medication changes are not.
