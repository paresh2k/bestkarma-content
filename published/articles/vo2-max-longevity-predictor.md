Your doctor measures your blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar. These are useful numbers. They tell you about cardiovascular risk, metabolic risk, the likelihood of certain diseases.

But there's a number your doctor almost certainly hasn't measured — and the research suggests it may be more predictive of how long you'll live than any of those standard markers.

It's your VO2 max. And if you've never heard of it, that's worth fixing today.

## What VO2 Max Actually Measures

VO2 max is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during intense exercise. It's measured in milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (mL/kg/min). The name is shorthand: V stands for volume, O2 for oxygen, max for maximum.

The number reflects something fundamental about how your cardiovascular and muscular systems work together. A high VO2 max means your heart can pump large volumes of blood efficiently, your muscles can extract oxygen from that blood readily, and your entire aerobic energy system is well-developed. A low VO2 max means the opposite — your body has less capacity to sustain physical effort, and every physical demand costs more.

Think of it as your engine size. You can drive a small engine carefully for years. But a larger engine handles load with less strain, runs cooler under stress, and tends to last longer.

## The Longevity Data

The research here is unusually consistent and unusually powerful.

A landmark study following more than 120,000 adults found that low cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with a mortality risk comparable to smoking — a comparison that tends to stop people cold. We've spent decades treating smoking as the paradigmatic lifestyle threat; the data on aerobic fitness puts it in the same conversation.

More precisely: a 2018 study in the Journal of the American College of Cardiology tracked participants over 46 years and found that each one-MET increment in cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with an 11.6% reduction in all-cause mortality, a 16.1% reduction in cardiovascular mortality, and a 14% reduction in cancer mortality. These effects are dose-dependent — more fitness, more protection — with no upper ceiling found in the data.

Every one mL/kg/min increase in VO2 max is estimated to correspond to roughly 45 additional days of life expectancy. That figure is worth sitting with. A 10-point improvement — achievable in 6-12 months with the right training — corresponds to more than a year of additional life expectancy.

A 2025 study in the *Journals of Gerontology* added a new dimension: people with VO2 max in the 70th percentile or higher had measurably longer telomeres across multiple cell types than unfit individuals. Telomere length is one of the key markers of cellular aging. The aerobic fitness link to cellular aging biology means this isn't just about heart disease — it's about the rate at which your cells age.

The most striking finding, replicated across multiple datasets: moving from the "low" fitness category to merely "below average" is associated with a 50%+ reduction in mortality risk. You don't need to become an elite athlete. You need to not be sedentary.

## What Your Number Means

VO2 max declines at roughly 1% per year after age 25 without training. By the time most people reach 60, they've lost 30-40% of their peak aerobic capacity — not because of age, but because of progressive deconditioning compounding over decades.

These are the reference ranges used in most clinical and research contexts:

| Fitness Category | Men (mL/kg/min) | Women (mL/kg/min) |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | 60+ | 50+ |
| Excellent | 52–60 | 45–50 |
| Good | 43–51 | 38–44 |
| Fair | 34–42 | 31–37 |
| Poor | <34 | <31 |

Where do you fall? Probably somewhere in the middle — which is where the biggest return on investment lies. Peter Attia, the longevity physician who has done more than anyone to bring VO2 max into the mainstream conversation, argues that his goal for patients is the 75th percentile for their age group by the time they reach 65. Not elite. Not optimal. Just: well above the dangerous low end.

## How to Measure Yours

The gold standard is a VO2 max test conducted with a metabolic cart — a mask attached to gas analysis equipment while you run on a treadmill or cycle on a bike at increasing intensity until you can't continue. These tests are available at university exercise physiology labs, some hospitals, and certain high-end fitness facilities. They typically cost $150-300 and take 15-20 minutes.

Most people don't need this level of precision. Several practical alternatives give you a reasonable estimate:

**Smartwatch estimate:** Apple Watch, Garmin, Polar, and similar devices estimate VO2 max from heart rate data during outdoor runs or structured exercise. Accuracy is within 10-15% for most people — good enough for tracking your trend over months.

**Cooper 12-minute test:** Run as far as you can in 12 minutes, then plug the distance into a standard table. Simple, free, and reasonably accurate. (Look up the Cooper Test VO2 max calculator — they're widely available.)

**Rockport 1-mile walk test:** Walk one mile as fast as you can, measure your heart rate at the end, and enter your data into the Rockport formula. More accessible for people who aren't ready to run.

Whichever method you use, measure it now — then again in three months, six months, a year. The trend matters more than the absolute number.

## Why Aerobic Fitness Protects You

The mortality data is strong enough to act on without fully understanding the mechanisms. But the mechanisms are worth knowing, because they reinforce that this isn't one of those statistical correlations where causality is unclear.

**Cardiac efficiency.** Trained aerobic systems produce structural changes in the heart: increased stroke volume (more blood per beat), lower resting heart rate, and greater cardiac output under stress. Your heart does more work with less effort.

**Glucose metabolism.** Aerobic exercise increases insulin sensitivity in muscle tissue — a primary driver of type 2 diabetes protection. VO2 max is one of the best predictors of insulin sensitivity. This is why high cardiorespiratory fitness is so strongly associated with lower metabolic disease risk.

**Inflammation.** Chronic low-grade inflammation underlies cardiovascular disease, cancer, and neurodegeneration. Regular aerobic exercise measurably reduces CRP, IL-6, and other inflammatory markers — even after adjusting for body composition.

**Brain health.** Exercise is the most potent stimulus for BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) — the protein responsible for neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity. High VO2 max correlates with larger hippocampal volume, better executive function, and lower dementia risk.

**Telomere preservation.** As noted in the 2025 *Journals of Gerontology* study: the cellular aging effect is real and measurable. Aerobic fitness appears to slow biological aging at the cellular level, not just the symptomatic level.

## How to Improve It

VO2 max responds to training. The right stimulus is working your aerobic system at or near its current limits. Three protocols have the strongest research support:

**Zone 2 training (the aerobic base):** This is steady, sustainable aerobic work at 60-70% of your maximum heart rate — a pace where you can hold a conversation but wouldn't want to sing. The key is duration: 45-60 minutes per session, 3-4 times per week. Zone 2 builds the mitochondrial density and cardiac efficiency that underpin your VO2 max capacity. It's not glamorous. It's foundational.

**HIIT (the direct stimulus):** High-intensity interval training at 80-95% of maximum heart rate produces the largest short-term VO2 max gains. The mechanism is straightforward — you're repeatedly pushing the aerobic system to or near its ceiling, which triggers adaptation. One to two HIIT sessions per week is effective; more than that is counterproductive for most people.

**The Norwegian 4×4 protocol:** This specific HIIT format is the most studied for VO2 max improvement. Structure: four minutes at approximately 90% of maximum heart rate, followed by three minutes of active recovery, repeated four times. This session takes about 40 minutes including warm-up and cool-down. Multiple randomized trials have shown 15-20% VO2 max improvements in sedentary and moderately fit individuals over 8-12 weeks.

Most people's programs should combine both: Zone 2 for 80% of cardio volume, HIIT for the remaining 20%.

## What Six Months Actually Looks Like

A sedentary 45-year-old with a VO2 max of 32 mL/kg/min (low category for men) commits to three Zone 2 sessions and one HIIT session per week. What happens?

Weeks 1-4: Significant discomfort. The training sessions feel hard. This is normal. The aerobic system is adapting.

Weeks 5-12: Measurable progress. Pace at the same heart rate improves. The 4×4 intervals start feeling manageable.

Months 4-6: A 6-month consistent effort in this person typically yields 15-25% improvement in VO2 max — moving from 32 to roughly 38-40, crossing from "low" into "fair" territory. That crossing corresponds to a significant mortality risk reduction.

Not elite. Not optimal. But meaningful — in a way that extends a life, not just a workout.

## Start Here

Get a baseline measurement this week. Use your watch's estimate, run the Cooper test, or book a proper assessment. Write the number down. Then train consistently for 90 days and measure again.

VO2 max is the one fitness metric that tells you not how fit you feel, but how long you'll live. The body keeps its own score. It's worth knowing yours.
