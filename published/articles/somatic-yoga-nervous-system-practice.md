There's a common assumption in yoga culture that doing the pose correctly — hips square, spine neutral, gaze fixed — is where the value lives. Somatic yoga inverts this entirely. In a somatic practice, the external shape of the movement is almost irrelevant. What matters is what you can feel from the inside. And this shift in attention, from performance to perception, turns out to have surprisingly deep roots in neuroscience — and growing relevance for people dealing with chronic pain, nervous system dysregulation, and trauma.

If that sounds like a subtle distinction, consider this: most people living with chronic lower back pain have structurally normal spines on imaging. The pain is real, but its origin is neurological, not architectural. Somatic movement practices — which train the brain's ability to sense and voluntarily control muscle tissue — may address this in ways that shape-based exercise simply doesn't.

## What Makes Somatic Movement Different

Conventional yoga, as typically taught in Western studios, is largely **exteroceptive** in orientation. You're guided toward an external form — a shape the teacher demonstrates, a position the body approximates. Feedback is visual and corrective: "straighten your arms," "tuck your tailbone." The implicit goal is alignment with an ideal.

Somatic yoga, by contrast, is **interoceptive and proprioceptive** in orientation. You're guided into slow, exploratory movement and asked to notice — what do you actually feel happening in your body right now? The implicit goal is not a shape but an experience of increased sensation clarity and voluntary control.

This isn't just philosophical. The distinction maps onto different neural pathways. Proprioception — the body's sense of its own position and movement — is processed through muscle spindle fibers and Golgi tendon organs, which report to the cerebellum and sensorimotor cortex. **Interoception**, the sense of the body's internal state, runs primarily through the vagus nerve and insular cortex. Both systems are trainable. Both are implicated in chronic pain, stress regulation, and trauma. Standard yoga practice engages proprioception to some degree, but somatic practices explicitly target both systems through slow, attentive movement rather than postural achievement.

### The Thomas Hanna Tradition

The intellectual foundation of somatic movement most relevant here comes from **Thomas Hanna**, an American philosopher and movement educator who founded the field of Hanna Somatic Education in the 1970s and 80s. Hanna's key insight was that habitual stress responses — what he called **Sensory Motor Amnesia (SMA)** — cause certain muscle groups to remain in chronic, involuntary contraction. The nervous system learns to hold the body in defensive or protective postures, and eventually loses the ability to release them voluntarily. The muscles aren't injured; the brain has simply forgotten how to let them go.

Hanna's antidote was **pandiculation**: a technique borrowed from the natural waking movement of animals (think of a cat stretching after sleep — that full-body, deliberate, yawning contraction and slow release). In pandiculation, you consciously contract the tight muscle group further, then very slowly release, giving the motor cortex an active signal that resets muscle length. This is neurologically distinct from passive stretching, which Hanna argued could temporarily lengthen tissue without reprogramming the nervous system's baseline tone.

## The Evidence Base — and Its Honest Limits

The research on somatic yoga specifically is still early-stage. This is worth saying plainly: we don't yet have the large, long-term RCTs that exist for, say, cardiovascular exercise or cognitive behavioral therapy. What we have is a mixture of somatic movement trials, interoception research, and trauma-body connection studies that collectively build a plausible case.

On chronic pain, the data are most developed. A **2017 randomized controlled trial by Mehling et al., published in JAMA Internal Medicine**, followed 342 adults with chronic low back pain across 12 weeks. Participants assigned to a mindful body awareness program — grounded in interoceptive attention during movement — showed clinically significant reductions in pain and functional disability compared to usual care. The mechanism proposed was improved cortical representation of the affected area, essentially the brain re-learning where the body is.

A closely related finding comes from **Moseley and colleagues**, whose work on the **neuroscience of pain** (consolidated in Moseley & Butler's *Explain Pain* framework and multiple publications in journals including *The Journal of Pain* and *Pain*) has consistently demonstrated that chronic pain is a brain output, not simply a tissue signal. Somatic practices that increase body awareness and reduce threat-perception may down-regulate pain by altering the predictive models the brain uses to interpret sensation.

On the trauma side, the theoretical scaffolding is strong, even if RCT evidence lags. **Bessel van der Kolk's work**, particularly his landmark 2014 book *The Body Keeps the Score* and his earlier research published in the *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, established that trauma is stored somatically — in patterns of autonomic nervous system activation, altered interoception, and disrupted body image. Van der Kolk's own studies, including a **2015 pilot RCT in the Journal of Clinical Psychology** with 64 women with treatment-resistant PTSD, found that trauma-sensitive yoga produced significant reductions in PTSD symptoms compared to a support group control. The effect size was large (Cohen's d = 1.1).

Interoceptive training itself has been studied independently of yoga. **Garfinkel and colleagues' 2015 work in Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B** demonstrated that interoceptive accuracy — how well you can sense your own heartbeat, for instance — is associated with emotional regulation and reduced anxiety. This is relevant because somatic yoga explicitly trains this capacity.

### What the Evidence Doesn't Settle

It would be dishonest to claim the research fully supports somatic yoga as a defined practice. Most trials use heterogeneous protocols. Sample sizes are often small. Few studies isolate what's doing the work: is it the slow movement? The interoceptive attention? The non-judgmental framing? The vagal activation from breathwork that typically accompanies somatic practice? We don't yet know. And the practitioner landscape is unregulated — "somatic yoga" can mean very different things depending on who's teaching it.

## Why the Nervous System Framing Matters

One of the reasons somatic yoga has gained traction in trauma-informed clinical spaces is its relationship to **polyvagal theory**, developed by neuroscientist **Stephen Porges** and published in journals including *Psychophysiology* and consolidated in his 2011 book *The Polyvagal Theory*. Porges' framework describes three hierarchical states of the autonomic nervous system: ventral vagal (social engagement, safety), sympathetic (mobilization, fight-or-flight), and dorsal vagal (immobilization, shutdown).

Chronic stress and trauma tend to dysregulate this system — people get stuck in sympathetic activation or dorsal vagal collapse. Somatic practices, by combining slow movement, breath attention, and non-threatening physical sensation, are thought to stimulate the ventral vagal system — the state associated with felt safety and social connection. This is why somatic yoga is often described as **nervous system regulation** rather than exercise: the goal is to shift the body's baseline state, not to build strength or flexibility.

The biological mechanism here involves the **myelinated vagus nerve**, which innervates the heart, lungs, and digestive organs, and responds to slow, rhythmic movement and gentle pressure. Activating this pathway slows heart rate, reduces cortisol output, and shifts the autonomic nervous system toward the parasympathetic state. Breathwork — specifically extended exhalation — enhances this effect, which is why somatic yoga practices typically emphasize breath over form.

## Practical Entry Points

You don't need to find a certified Hanna Somatic Education practitioner to begin exploring this territory (though if you're working with chronic pain or trauma, a qualified guide is worthwhile). Here's a starting protocol grounded in the principles above:

- **Start with one pandiculation daily.** Lie on your back. Slowly contract your lower back (arch it gently), hold for three seconds, then take 20–30 seconds to very slowly release. The slowness is the point — it's what gives the motor cortex time to re-pattern. This is Thomas Hanna's foundational exercise.
- **Practice somatic scanning before movement.** Before any exercise, spend two minutes lying still and noticing sensations without trying to change them. This activates the interoceptive network before you move.
- **Choose slow over deep.** In any yoga posture, move at half your instinctive speed. Notice the moment before you feel a stretch. Explore the edge rather than pushing through it.
- **Extended exhalation as a closing practice.** End each session with five minutes of breathing where your exhale is twice as long as your inhale (e.g., inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 8). This directly activates the myelinated vagus and supports transition into parasympathetic tone.
- **Frequency:** three to four sessions per week, 20–40 minutes each, is consistent with protocols used in the clinical literature. Shorter daily practice is also well-supported — even 10 minutes of attentive, slow movement appears to shift nervous system state.

The research on somatic yoga is young. But the neuroscience of interoception, chronic pain, and autonomic regulation gives it a credible theoretical foundation. And the practice itself asks almost nothing of you in terms of fitness, flexibility, or performance — which, for people whose relationship with their body has been difficult, may be the most important quality of all.
