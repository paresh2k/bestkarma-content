Until 1993, women were routinely excluded from major cardiovascular clinical trials. Not as an oversight — as policy. The assumption was that women were essentially smaller men with hormonal noise that complicated the data. That assumption has cost lives. Today, heart disease kills more women than all cancers combined, yet women are still less likely to be referred for cardiac testing, less likely to receive evidence-based medications after a heart attack, and more likely to be sent home from the emergency department with a diagnosis of anxiety. The problem isn't just historical. It's happening right now, in hospitals with excellent reputations, to patients with real arterial disease.

The gap between how heart disease presents in women and how medicine has been trained to recognize it is one of the most consequential mismatches in modern healthcare. Closing it starts with understanding what the evidence actually says — and knowing which tests to ask for.

---

## The Research Gap That Became a Clinical Gap

The NIH Revitalization Act of 1993 finally required the inclusion of women and minorities in federally funded trials, but decades of male-dominated research had already shaped clinical guidelines, risk calculators, and physician training in ways that persist today. A landmark analysis by **Mosca et al. (2011, Circulation)** of 2,400 women found that only 55% of primary care physicians and 70% of cardiologists felt well-prepared to assess cardiovascular risk in women — this despite heart disease being the leading cause of female mortality in the United States for every year since 1984.

The downstream effects are measurable. A 2018 study by **Mehta et al. in the European Heart Journal**, analyzing data from more than 1 million acute coronary syndrome patients, found that women under 60 were **twice as likely as men** to be misdiagnosed following a heart attack. Women were less likely to receive coronary angiography, beta-blockers, or statins during hospitalization, and their in-hospital mortality was significantly higher. This wasn't explained by disease severity. It was explained by differential treatment.

Part of the problem is symptom pattern. The "Hollywood heart attack" — crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm — is predominantly a male presentation. Women are more likely to present with nausea, jaw pain, back pain, fatigue, and shortness of breath. A 2019 study by **Lichtman et al. in the Journal of the American Heart Association** of 2,009 women who had experienced acute myocardial infarction found that 78% reported prodromal symptoms in the weeks prior — most commonly unusual fatigue and sleep disturbance — but fewer than 30% associated those symptoms with their heart. Their physicians often didn't either.

## Why Standard Risk Calculators Underestimate Women

The **Pooled Cohort Equations (PCE)**, the standard tool used by American clinicians to estimate 10-year cardiovascular risk, were derived primarily from studies conducted in predominantly male and white populations. When applied to women, they systematically misclassify risk — particularly for younger women and women of color.

A 2018 analysis by **DeFilippis et al. in Annals of Internal Medicine** found that the PCE underestimated risk in women with subclinical atherosclerosis and overestimated risk in some lower-risk groups, producing a pattern of misclassification that led to undertreating the women who needed intervention most. The calculator doesn't account for several risk factors that disproportionately affect women: autoimmune conditions like lupus and rheumatoid arthritis, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), premature menopause (before age 40), and — critically — adverse pregnancy outcomes.

The 2019 **ACC/AHA Cardiovascular Risk Guidelines** acknowledged this gap by formally designating certain female-specific risk enhancers as factors clinicians should incorporate into risk discussions. But acknowledgment in a guideline and implementation in clinical practice are two different things.

---

## Pregnancy as a Cardiac Stress Test

Pregnancy is, physiologically, one of the most intense cardiovascular challenges a human body can face. Cardiac output increases by 30–50%, blood volume expands, and the placental circulation imposes sustained demands on vascular function. When that system fails — producing preeclampsia, gestational hypertension, gestational diabetes, or preterm birth — it leaves a biological signature that doesn't disappear when the pregnancy ends.

**A meta-analysis by Bellamy et al. (2007, BMJ)** of 3.5 million women found that a history of preeclampsia was associated with a **fourfold increase in the risk of hypertension** and a **twofold increase in the risk of cardiovascular disease** over the following decade. More recent data from the **HUNT study (Magnussen et al., 2009, Circulation)**, following 3,494 Norwegian women for up to 30 years, confirmed that pregnancy complications — not just preeclampsia, but also gestational hypertension and fetal growth restriction — were independently associated with cardiovascular mortality later in life, even after controlling for traditional risk factors.

The mechanism appears to involve shared underlying vulnerabilities: women who develop preeclampsia may have pre-existing endothelial dysfunction or metabolic dysregulation that the metabolic stress of pregnancy unmasks. The pregnancy complication is not just a risk marker — it may be a window into vascular biology that was always there, waiting.

Despite this evidence, a survey by **Mehta et al. (2020, Journal of the American College of Cardiology)** found that fewer than 50% of women with a history of preeclampsia reported ever being told by a physician that this increased their future cardiac risk. This is a straightforward, well-evidenced clinical conversation that is not happening.

## The Case for Coronary Artery Calcium Scoring in Women

The **coronary artery calcium (CAC) score** is a low-radiation CT scan that quantifies calcified plaque in the coronary arteries. A score of zero means essentially no detectable plaque; higher scores indicate increasing atherosclerotic burden. In men, its utility as a risk reclassification tool is well established. In women, the evidence is arguably even more compelling — precisely because standard risk models do such a poor job.

The **Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA)**, which has followed over 6,800 adults since 2000, has generated extensive sex-specific data on CAC. Research by **McClelland et al. and Detrano et al.** from MESA found that CAC scoring significantly improved 10-year cardiovascular risk prediction beyond the Framingham Risk Score in both sexes, but the **reclassification benefit was particularly pronounced in women** who had been categorized as intermediate or lower risk by standard calculators. Women who scored zero had a very low event rate — reassuring and potentially allowing them to avoid unnecessary statin therapy. Women with elevated scores despite "low" traditional risk were identified as needing earlier intervention.

A 2020 analysis by **Dzaye et al. in JACC: Cardiovascular Imaging**, specifically examining CAC in women, found that women tend to develop calcium deposits approximately **7–10 years later than men** — meaning that age thresholds and reference ranges derived from male populations can miss meaningful disease in women in their 40s and 50s. Using male-derived norms can produce false reassurance.

The current 2019 ACC/AHA guidelines recommend CAC scoring as a reasonable option for adults in whom the decision to initiate statin therapy is uncertain — a category that includes many women in the 45–65 range whose risk calculators return borderline results.

## Nuance and What We Don't Yet Know

It's worth being precise about what this evidence does and doesn't establish. The sex differences in cardiovascular presentation and risk are real and clinically meaningful — but they exist on a spectrum, not a binary. Many women do present with classic chest pain; many men present atypically. The goal is not to replace one oversimplification with another, but to expand the clinical picture.

The data on female-specific risk enhancers — PCOS, autoimmune disease, depression, pregnancy complications — is compelling but not yet fully integrated into validated risk prediction tools. These factors are recognized as important, but the precise magnitude of their contribution varies across populations and studies. This is an active area of research, and the field is moving. What we can say with confidence is that ignoring these factors, as current standard practice often does, systematically underestimates risk in a large subset of women.

## What to Do: Tests and Conversations to Initiate

This is not about catastrophizing your risk — it's about getting an accurate picture. Here is what the evidence supports:

**Discuss your full risk history.** At your next preventive cardiology or primary care visit, make sure your clinician knows about any history of preeclampsia, gestational hypertension, gestational diabetes, PCOS, premature menopause, or autoimmune conditions. These are formal risk enhancers per current ACC/AHA guidelines and should factor into your risk assessment.

**Ask whether your 10-year risk calculation accounts for female-specific factors.** If your clinician runs a standard PCE score without discussing the above, ask specifically whether those factors change the picture. If they don't know, that's useful information about whether you need a cardiovascular specialist.

**Consider requesting a CAC score if you are 45–75 and have borderline or uncertain risk.** The scan takes minutes, costs $100–$400 (often not insurance-covered but worth the out-of-pocket cost), and delivers a concrete number that substantially improves risk prediction. A score of zero is genuinely reassuring; a score above 100 in a woman whose PCE suggested low risk is clinically actionable.

**Don't let atypical symptoms be dismissed.** Unexplained fatigue, exertional shortness of breath, jaw or back pain, or episodic nausea — especially in the context of exertion or stress — deserve cardiac evaluation, not reassurance-by-default. You are entitled to ask: "Could this be cardiac?"

**Know your numbers.** Blood pressure, fasting glucose, lipid panel with ApoB if available, and hsCRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein) are the foundation. ApoB in particular is a superior marker to LDL-C for cardiovascular risk in women, per a 2021 analysis by **Sniderman et al. in JAMA Cardiology**.

The good news is that cardiovascular disease is largely preventable, and the interventions that work — blood pressure control, lipid management, aerobic fitness, not smoking — work in women as well as they do in men. The problem has never been a lack of solutions. It's been a failure to accurately identify who needs them.
