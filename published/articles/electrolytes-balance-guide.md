Most people think electrolyte balance is a sports nutrition concern — something for triathletes and football players sweating through August two-a-days. But a 2021 analysis of NHANES data published in *Nutrients* found that approximately 60% of American adults consume less than the adequate intake for potassium, and roughly 45% fall short on magnesium. These aren't athletes with unusual demands. These are ordinary people eating ordinary diets, running low on minerals that govern nerve firing, blood pressure regulation, muscle contraction, and sleep quality. The electrolyte supplement market, now worth over $3 billion annually, exists partly because it spotted a real gap — and partly because it learned to market effectively into anxiety. Knowing the difference requires understanding the underlying physiology.

---

## What Electrolytes Actually Do

The term "electrolyte" refers to minerals that carry an electrical charge when dissolved in fluid. Sodium, potassium, magnesium, chloride, calcium, and phosphate are all electrolytes, but sodium, potassium, and magnesium are the three most relevant to everyday health and the ones most likely to be out of balance in the general population.

**Sodium** is the dominant extracellular cation — the primary charged particle outside your cells. Its concentration gradient across cell membranes is what makes electrical signaling possible. Every nerve impulse, every heartbeat, every muscle contraction depends on sodium ions rushing through ion channels. Beyond excitability, sodium is your body's primary fluid-volume regulator. When serum sodium rises, the kidneys retain water and trigger thirst. When it drops, the kidneys excrete water. The whole system is exquisitely calibrated: a healthy body maintains serum sodium within a narrow range of 135–145 mEq/L, and deviations of even 10% in either direction constitute medical emergencies.

**Potassium** works in opposition to sodium — it is the dominant *intracellular* cation, and the ratio of intracellular potassium to extracellular sodium is what sets the resting membrane potential of every cell in your body. Think of this ratio as the cell's "idle voltage." When it shifts, cells become either too excitable (cardiac arrhythmias, muscle cramps) or too quiet (weakness, fatigue, constipation). Potassium also acts directly on arterial walls, promoting vasodilation and blunting the blood-pressure-raising effects of sodium.

**Magnesium** is biochemically distinct from the other two — it functions primarily as a cofactor rather than a charge-carrier. Over 300 enzymatic reactions require magnesium, including the synthesis of ATP (your cells' energy currency), DNA repair, and the regulation of calcium channels. In muscle and nerve physiology, magnesium plays a gating role: it blocks NMDA receptors and calcium channels at rest, preventing cells from firing without a proper signal. This is part of why low magnesium is associated with hyperexcitability symptoms — cramps, twitching, poor sleep, anxiety.

## The Evidence on Deficiency — and Its Consequences

The NHANES data on potassium and magnesium intake is sobering, but intake numbers alone understate the problem. A 2016 analysis by Rosanoff, Dai, and Shapses in *Nutrition Reviews* examined both dietary intake and functional biomarkers, arguing that serum magnesium — the standard clinical test — is a poor proxy for whole-body magnesium status because less than 1% of the body's magnesium circulates in serum. The remainder is stored in bone and soft tissue. This means a patient can have "normal" serum magnesium while functionally depleted at the cellular level.

The consequences of chronic sub-optimal potassium intake are well-documented. A **2021 meta-analysis by Filippini and colleagues** in the *Journal of Hypertension*, pooling data from 32 randomized controlled trials involving over 1,900 participants, found that potassium supplementation reduced systolic blood pressure by an average of 4.7 mmHg in people with hypertension. That's clinically meaningful — comparable in magnitude to some antihypertensive medications at low doses. The mechanism is dual: direct vasodilation of arterial smooth muscle and increased urinary sodium excretion (potassium stimulates the sodium-potassium pump in kidney tubule cells, which pulls potassium into cells in exchange for sodium excreted into urine).

Magnesium deficiency has a similarly strong relationship with cardiovascular and metabolic outcomes. A **2016 prospective cohort study by Del Gobbo and colleagues** in *BMC Medicine*, following 223,000 adults across nine studies with up to 28 years of follow-up, found that each 200 mg/day increment in dietary magnesium intake was associated with a 22% lower risk of ischemic heart disease. While observational data cannot establish causation, the mechanistic plausibility is high: magnesium deficiency promotes endothelial dysfunction, increases vascular smooth muscle contractility, and is associated with insulin resistance — a key driver of cardiovascular risk.

## Exercise, Sweat, and Who Actually Needs More

Sweat is not just water. A **2007 review by Shirreffs and Maughan** in the *Journal of Sports Sciences* documented sodium concentration in sweat ranging from 20 to 80 mmol/L, with a wide individual spread driven largely by genetics and heat acclimatization. A 70 kg person exercising at moderate intensity in warm conditions might lose 1–2 liters of sweat per hour, putting total sodium losses in the range of 500–2,000 mg per hour. That's substantial — and it's why sports scientists have long recognized that replacing fluid without replacing sodium can actually worsen hyponatremia, the dangerous condition of low blood sodium, in endurance athletes.

**Potassium losses in sweat are considerably smaller** — approximately 150–500 mg per liter compared to sodium's 460–1,150 mg per liter — and are largely replaced by typical dietary intake in the hours following exercise. Magnesium losses in sweat are even lower, though magnesium is also lost in urine, and intense training increases urinary magnesium excretion. A **2017 review by Nielsen and Lukaski** in *Magnesium Research* concluded that athletes engaged in heavy training likely have elevated magnesium requirements and may benefit from supplementation, though the evidence base for performance benefits of supplementing *in already-replete individuals* is weak.

The practical upshot: **most sedentary adults in air-conditioned environments don't lose meaningful electrolytes through sweat**. Their electrolyte concerns are primarily dietary — too much sodium from processed food, and not enough potassium and magnesium from whole food. The picture changes for people exercising more than 60–90 minutes, particularly in heat, and for people on certain medications (diuretics deplete both potassium and magnesium; proton pump inhibitors are associated with hypomagnesemia).

## What the Product Evidence Actually Shows

The consumer electrolyte market — LMNT, Liquid IV, Nuun, Precision Hydration — has positioned itself cleverly at the intersection of legitimate sports nutrition science and lifestyle marketing. Some of it is well-formulated. Some of it is expensive sugar water with traces of minerals.

**LMNT** contains 1,000 mg sodium, 200 mg potassium, and 60 mg magnesium per serving — a high-sodium formulation that tracks closely with actual sweat replacement needs for endurance athletes. For someone running 90 minutes in the heat, this is defensible. For someone sitting at a desk, the sodium load may be unnecessary and potentially counterproductive if they're already consuming 3,000–4,000 mg of dietary sodium daily (the average American consumes approximately 3,400 mg/day, per CDC data). **Liquid IV** uses a glucose-electrolyte formulation based on the science of oral rehydration therapy — co-transporting sodium and glucose to drive faster fluid absorption in the gut — which is well-supported by *WHO oral rehydration solution* research, but comes with 11 grams of added sugar per serving, which is irrelevant to most non-athletes and adds up if consumed daily.

For non-exercising adults whose primary gap is potassium and magnesium, the more efficient intervention is dietary: **dark leafy greens, legumes, avocado, nuts, and seeds** are among the richest whole-food sources of both minerals. A cup of cooked spinach provides roughly 840 mg of potassium; a quarter cup of pumpkin seeds provides approximately 190 mg of magnesium. These foods also carry fiber, phytonutrients, and other compounds absent from any supplement sachet.

## Practical Protocol

The right approach depends on who you are and what you're doing.

**For most adults (low-to-moderate activity):**
- Prioritize dietary potassium: aim for the WHO-recommended 3,500 mg/day through vegetables, legumes, and fruit — not supplements
- Dietary magnesium target: 400 mg/day for men, 310–320 mg/day for women; if dietary intake is consistently below this, a **magnesium glycinate or magnesium malate supplement at 200–400 mg** in the evening is well-tolerated and has the most favorable absorption data (avoid magnesium oxide — bioavailability is poor)
- Monitor sodium: for most desk-bound adults, sodium *restriction* (reducing processed food) is more relevant than supplementation

**For active adults (60+ minutes of vigorous exercise, especially in heat):**
- Target 500–1,000 mg of sodium replacement per hour of intense exercise in warm conditions
- An electrolyte product with 500–1,000 mg sodium, 150–200 mg potassium, and 40–60 mg magnesium per serving is a reasonable intra- or post-workout formulation
- Plain water is adequate for exercise sessions under 60 minutes at moderate intensity

| Electrolyte | Recommended Daily Intake | Common Dietary Sources | Supplementation Need |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sodium | 1,500–2,300 mg | Processed foods, salt | Primarily for athletes/heat exposure |
| Potassium | 3,500–4,700 mg | Leafy greens, legumes, fruit | When diet is consistently low |
| Magnesium | 310–420 mg | Nuts, seeds, dark chocolate, legumes | Glycinate or malate form if diet is insufficient |

---

The electrolyte supplement boom is not entirely manufactured hype. The underlying physiology is real, the dietary gaps are real, and for people exercising hard in heat, targeted supplementation has genuine utility. But for the majority of people buying LMNT sachets and Liquid IV sticks for daily hydration support, the honest answer is that a plate of legumes and leafy greens would cover the actual deficit more effectively, at a fraction of the cost, with meaningfully better overall nutritional value. Understanding the mechanism — rather than the marketing — is what allows you to make that call for yourself.
