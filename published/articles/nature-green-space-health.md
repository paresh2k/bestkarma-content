In 2019, a paper published in *Scientific Reports* by Matthew White and colleagues analyzed data from 19,806 UK adults and asked a question with a straightforward structure: does time spent in natural environments predict better health and wellbeing? And if so, is there a dose-response relationship?

The answer to both questions was yes. People who spent at least 120 minutes per week in nature — parks, countryside, forests, coastline — reported significantly better health and higher wellbeing than those who spent no time in natural environments. Below 120 minutes, the benefit was not reliably detected. Above it, the benefit was consistent and did not appear to increase substantially with more time. Two hours per week emerged as the evidence-based threshold: the minimum effective dose of nature for measurable wellbeing benefit.

This is an unusually specific and actionable finding for population health research. It gives a target — achievable by almost anyone, in any urban environment — rather than a vague directive to "spend more time outdoors."

## The Neuroscience: What Nature Does to the Brain

The most direct evidence for nature's effects on the nervous system comes from neuroimaging studies comparing brain activity during nature walks versus urban walks.

In a study of 63 adults who underwent fMRI before and after walking for 90 minutes in either a natural environment (grasslands, trees) or an urban one (a busy road), the nature group showed significantly lower activity in the subgenual prefrontal cortex — a region associated with rumination, self-referential negative thought, and vulnerability to depression. The urban walkers showed no change. The same nature walk participants also reported lower scores on measures of rumination after the walk compared to before.

A 2025 study published in Frontiers in Environmental Health examined a more minimal intervention: a small indoor green space (essentially a clinic biotope — potted plants, natural materials, water features) installed in an outpatient psychiatric clinic. Patients with depression who spent time near the installation showed measurably increased parasympathetic nervous activity and decreased sympathetic nervous activity compared to patients in the standard clinical environment. The effect was physiologically detectable from a modest amount of indoor greenery — not a forest, not a park, but a thoughtfully arranged assemblage of plants in a room.

These findings illustrate both the robustness of the effect and its accessibility. The nervous system responds to natural elements — not just to grand landscapes.

## Why It Works: The Theoretical Frameworks

Two complementary theories have dominated academic explanation of the nature-health connection.

**Attention Restoration Theory (Kaplan & Kaplan)** proposes that natural environments restore depleted directed attentional capacity. Directed attention — the effortful focus required for work, navigation, decision-making, and resisting distraction — has a limited daily budget. Urban environments make continuous demands on directed attention: traffic, noise, signage, social monitoring, rapid decisions. Natural environments, by contrast, engage what the Kaplans call "soft fascination" — the gentle, involuntary attention drawn to clouds, water, birdsong, trees — which is non-depleting. Recovery from directed attention fatigue occurs naturally when voluntary attention is not required.

**Stress Recovery Theory (Ulrich)** proposes a more primal mechanism: humans evolved in natural environments and carry an evolved physiological response to them that promotes stress recovery. Natural visual patterns (fractal patterns in leaves and branches, horizon lines, water surfaces) and soundscapes (birdsong, water, wind) trigger parasympathetic activation through pathways that operate faster than conscious appraisal. This theory is supported by studies showing that even brief nature exposure — including images of nature and nature sounds — reduces physiological stress markers within minutes.

Both theories are likely partially correct, and they operate through different mechanisms that may be additive. The attentional restoration operates over longer timescales (30-90 minutes of nature exposure); the physiological stress recovery begins more rapidly and may account for the acute cortisol and heart rate reductions documented in many studies.

## The Evidence Across Populations

Large-scale cross-national research confirms that the nature-wellbeing relationship is not culturally specific. A study covering adults across 18 countries found consistent associations between outdoor nature exposure and positive wellbeing, reduced mental distress, and better self-rated health — across countries with dramatically different climates, urbanization rates, and cultural norms about outdoor time.

The benefits appear strongest in the populations with the most to gain:

**Children.** A strong body of research links regular outdoor green space time to reduced ADHD symptoms, better emotional regulation, higher creativity, and lower stress reactivity. The developmental period during which the nervous system is being calibrated appears to be particularly important for nature exposure; some researchers have proposed that the epidemic increase in childhood anxiety and attention disorders is partly attributable to reduced access to unstructured outdoor time.

**People with depression and anxiety.** Forest bathing (shinrin-yoku, the Japanese practice of slow, deliberate time in forested environments) and "green exercise" (physical activity in natural environments) consistently show effect sizes for depression and anxiety symptoms comparable to mild-to-moderate psychological interventions. Cortisol reduction from forest walking is measurable in blood and saliva within 30 minutes.

**Older adults.** Access to green space is associated with slower cognitive decline, lower loneliness, and greater social engagement in older populations. Community gardens, where older adults have regular reasons to be outdoors in contact with others, show particularly strong loneliness and wellbeing effects — combining nature exposure with the social engagement that independently supports cognitive health.

## The Access Equity Problem

Nature access is not evenly distributed. Research across US and European cities consistently documents that low-income and minority communities have two to three times less access to quality green space than wealthier, predominantly white neighborhoods. This disparity is a health equity issue: the populations already at higher risk of the conditions that nature exposure helps address — cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety, cognitive decline — are disproportionately the same populations with least access to natural environments.

For individuals in urban environments with limited green space access, the evidence offers some mitigation: indoor plants, views of trees or sky through windows, nature soundscapes, and even brief access to urban pocket parks produce some of the cortisol-reducing and parasympathetic-activating effects documented in formal nature studies. The effect size is smaller than sustained green space exposure, but measurable.

## The Practice

The two-hour weekly target is more flexible than it sounds. White's 2019 study found that the threshold was met whether people accumulated it in one or two longer visits or across multiple shorter ones. A 30-minute lunch walk in a park three times per week, supplemented by a 30-minute morning walk on the weekend, meets the evidence-based threshold.

What improves the benefit: attention. Looking at the specific textures of bark, listening for distinct bird calls, noticing changes in light — the directed but soft fascination that nature enables — produces more restoration than the same time spent in nature while staring at a phone. The natural environment is the instrument; attention is the practice that plays it.

The additional synergy: combining nature with physical movement. "Green exercise" — any physical activity performed outdoors in natural environments — produces additive mood and stress-reduction benefits compared to the same activity performed indoors. The two interventions are independently beneficial and mutually reinforcing.
