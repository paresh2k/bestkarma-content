An estimated 30% of adults have insomnia symptoms. About 10% meet criteria for chronic insomnia disorder — difficulty sleeping at least three nights per week for at least three months, producing real daytime impairment. It is one of the most common health complaints in primary care.

The treatment that works best for it — better than sleeping pills, with longer-lasting effects, without dependency or rebound — has been first-line recommended by every major sleep medicine body for more than a decade. Most people with insomnia have never been offered it.

It's called Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia, or CBT-I. And the gap between its evidence and its availability represents one of the more frustrating failures of healthcare delivery.

## Why Sleeping Pills Don't Solve Insomnia

Sleeping pills — broadly, sedative-hypnotics including benzodiazepines (temazepam, triazolam), Z-drugs (zolpidem, zopiclone, eszopiclone), and antihistamines (diphenhydramine) — produce one measurable benefit: faster sleep onset. On average, they reduce sleep latency by 20-30 minutes.

What they don't do is produce normal sleep. Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs artificially suppress brain activity broadly, reducing the proportion of slow-wave sleep (the physically restorative stage) and REM sleep (the cognitively and emotionally restorative stage). The total time in bed increases; the quality and architecture of that time does not. Many users report feeling more rested on waking — this is at least partly tolerance developing to the drug's amnestic properties rather than genuine sleep quality improvement.

The longer-term picture is worse. Tolerance to sleeping pills develops within days to weeks, requiring higher doses for the same effect. Dependence follows. And stopping creates rebound insomnia — a withdrawal-induced worsening of sleep that is often more severe than the original insomnia. This rebound traps people in cycles of pill use that can persist for years or decades.

Long-term sleeping pill use is associated with elevated risk of dementia — a finding now sufficiently replicated across several large cohort studies that it has entered clinical prescribing discussions, particularly for benzodiazepines and Z-drugs.

The drugs are not addressing insomnia. They are temporarily managing a symptom while the underlying perpetuating factors grow stronger.

## What CBT-I Actually Addresses

CBT-I is structured around a central insight from sleep medicine research: chronic insomnia is maintained not by the factors that originally caused it but by behavioral and cognitive patterns that developed in response to it.

You sleep badly, so you go to bed earlier to give yourself more time. You lie in bed awake for hours, which teaches your brain that bed is a place for wakefulness. You sleep in on weekends to recover, which shifts your circadian anchor. You develop anxiety about sleep — monitoring your sleepiness, dreading bedtime, catastrophizing about tomorrow if you don't sleep tonight. The anxiety itself generates arousal that prevents sleep. The behaviors designed to protect sleep become the mechanisms perpetuating it.

CBT-I breaks each of these maintaining factors systematically.

## The Five Components

**1. Sleep restriction therapy** — the most powerful and most counterintuitive component.

A person with insomnia who spends 9 hours in bed and sleeps for 5 of them has 44% sleep efficiency. Sleep restriction consolidates this by matching time in bed to actual sleep time. In the first week, a patient sleeping 5 hours is allowed only 5-5.5 hours in bed. The sleep drive — the homeostatic pressure to sleep — builds over the week. Sleep becomes deeper and more consolidated. Sleep efficiency rises toward 85-90%. Time in bed then expands in 15-minute increments each week as efficiency is sustained.

This is genuinely uncomfortable for the first 7-10 days. Daytime sleepiness increases. Most patients want to quit. The ones who persist through the first two weeks reliably see significant improvement in weeks 3-4.

Sleep restriction addresses the core problem: too much time in bed relative to sleep ability, producing fragmented, inefficient sleep and associating bed with wakefulness.

**2. Stimulus control** — rebuilding the bed-sleep association.

The behavioral component: the bedroom is for sleep and sex only. No phones, no reading, no television, no work, no worrying. If you're awake for more than 20 minutes in bed, get up. Do something calm — reading, gentle stretching, dim light — until genuinely sleepy, then return. Repeat as needed.

This feels wrong. It is the opposite of what most insomniacs do (lie in bed willing themselves to sleep). But the classical conditioning principle is sound: if the bed has been associated with hours of frustrated wakefulness, it has become a cue for arousal, not sleep. Stimulus control reverses this conditioning.

**3. Sleep hygiene** — the behavioral infrastructure.

Consistent wake time, daily. This single instruction — rise at the same time regardless of how much you slept — is the most important anchor of the circadian system and the most evidence-supported sleep behavioral recommendation. Cool bedroom (65-68°F / 18-20°C). No caffeine after noon. No alcohol within 3 hours of bed. Dim lights in the evening. Exercise regularly, not within 2 hours of sleep.

Sleep hygiene alone does not cure insomnia, but it is the necessary substrate on which the other components work.

**4. Cognitive restructuring** — addressing the thoughts.

Chronic insomniacs develop characteristic beliefs about sleep: "I must get 8 hours or tomorrow is ruined." "Every bad night damages my health." "I've lost the ability to sleep normally." These beliefs are catastrophic, inaccurate, and directly generate the pre-sleep arousal that prevents sleep.

A CBT-I therapist works through these thoughts using the same techniques as general CBT: identifying cognitive distortions, testing them against evidence, and replacing them with more accurate and less anxiety-provoking alternatives. "Last night I slept 5 hours and functioned adequately" is a more accurate and more useful thought than "if I sleep less than 7 hours I'll be incapacitated."

**5. Relaxation techniques** — reducing physiological arousal.

Progressive muscle relaxation (systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups), diaphragmatic breathing, and guided imagery target the physiological hyperarousal that characterizes insomnia. These are used both at bedtime and during nighttime awakenings.

## What the Evidence Shows

A 2025 meta-analysis of 130 randomized controlled trials found CBT-I superior to pharmacotherapy across all primary outcome measures for chronic insomnia, with effects that persisted at 6 and 12-month follow-up. Sleeping pills show effect-size advantage in short-term sleep onset latency reduction; CBT-I shows advantage in sleep efficiency, wake after sleep onset, sleep quality ratings, and long-term outcomes.

Most significantly: CBT-I effects improve after treatment ends. Sleep continues to get better in the months following a completed CBT-I program, as the behavioral and cognitive patterns consolidate and rebuild. Sleeping pill effects, when the drug is stopped, immediately worsen (rebound insomnia).

CBT-I works for 75-80% of people with chronic insomnia. The failure cases are typically people who do not complete the full program — particularly who abandon sleep restriction in week one when it's most uncomfortable.

## How to Access It

The barrier to CBT-I has historically been therapist availability. Trained behavioral sleep medicine specialists are rare in most markets.

Digital CBT-I (dCBT-I) has substantially changed the access equation. A 2025 randomized trial published in *npj Digital Medicine* found that digital CBT-I produced outcomes comparable to face-to-face therapy for most patients.

**Options:**
- **Sleepio:** Evidence-based digital program; free via NHS in the UK; available through many US employers and health plans
- **Somryst:** FDA-authorized prescription digital therapeutic for insomnia; covered by some US insurers
- **Stellar Sleep:** US-based digital CBT-I app with therapist support
- **Self-guided books:** *Say Good Night to Insomnia* (Gregg Jacobs, PhD) and *End the Insomnia Struggle* (Colleen Carney, PhD) deliver the full CBT-I protocol in readable format

The AASM (American Academy of Sleep Medicine), the European Sleep Research Society, the NHS, APA, and UpToDate all recommend CBT-I as first-line treatment for chronic insomnia. It should be what's offered before a sleeping pill prescription. If it hasn't been offered to you, ask for it specifically.

## A Note on Medication

This article is not an argument that sleeping pills are never appropriate. For short-term, situational insomnia — jet lag, pre-procedure anxiety, acute crisis — short-term pharmaceutical support has a role. The concern is the chronic use that develops when short-term prescriptions are never paired with behavioral treatment.

For anyone taking sleeping pills long-term, tapering off in conjunction with CBT-I is the evidence-based approach. This should be done under physician supervision, with CBT-I beginning 2-4 weeks before or alongside the taper.

The tool that works is available. That it remains under-used while sleeping pill prescriptions continue at scale is a healthcare system failure worth naming.
