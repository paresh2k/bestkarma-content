Most women are never told that their testosterone levels peak in their early twenties and then spend the next four decades declining — quietly, continuously, and with real consequences. By the time a woman reaches menopause, her testosterone is roughly half what it was at its peak. By her late fifties, it may be a quarter. And yet testosterone barely registers in routine women's healthcare. It isn't screened. It isn't discussed. When women bring up fatigue, low libido, or difficulty building muscle, the conversation rarely goes there. That silence has a cost.

The evidence is clear enough that in 2019, an international panel of endocrinologists, gynecologists, and sexual medicine specialists published the **Global Consensus Statement on the Use of Testosterone Therapy for Women** in four major journals simultaneously — *The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*, *Climacteric*, *Maturitas*, and the *Journal of Sexual Medicine*. The statement was unambiguous: testosterone therapy has a proven role in treating certain conditions in women, and the primary barrier to wider use is not evidence, but regulatory inertia and cultural discomfort.

---

## What Testosterone Actually Does in Women

Testosterone is not a male hormone that women happen to produce in small amounts. It is a primary androgen in women, synthesized in the ovaries and adrenal glands, and active throughout the body. Women produce it in smaller absolute quantities than men — roughly 5–10% of male levels — but their tissues are exquisitely sensitive to it. The androgen receptors in the brain, bone, muscle, skin, and genitals don't care about the sex of the person. They respond to the hormone.

The functions are wide-ranging. Testosterone drives **libido and sexual arousal** — this is perhaps its best-documented role in women, and the one with the most clinical trial data behind it. But it also supports **muscle protein synthesis**, meaning it contributes to strength, lean mass, and metabolic rate. In bone, it works alongside estrogen to maintain density. In the brain, androgen receptors are concentrated in regions associated with motivation, mood regulation, and cognition — including the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. Women with lower testosterone levels report higher rates of fatigue, low motivation, and difficulty concentrating, though causality here is harder to establish cleanly.

Energy and mood are the hardest to pin down mechanistically, because they're multifactorial. But the receptor biology is real, and dismissing these symptoms as "just menopause" or "just stress" without considering the hormonal substrate is, at minimum, an incomplete clinical picture.

## The Evidence: HSDD and Beyond

The most rigorous evidence in this area concerns **hypoactive sexual desire disorder (HSDD)** — defined as persistently low sexual desire that causes personal distress. HSDD affects an estimated 10–12% of women in the United States (Shifren et al., 2008, *Obstetrics & Gynecology*, n = 31,581), making it the most common female sexual dysfunction. And despite that prevalence, there is currently no FDA-approved testosterone product for women.

That is not because the therapy doesn't work. In a **2019 systematic review and meta-analysis published in *The Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology***, Davis and colleagues analyzed 36 randomized controlled trials involving 8,480 women. Testosterone therapy, compared to placebo or comparator treatments, produced significant improvements in sexual function — including desire, arousal, orgasm frequency, and satisfaction — with effect sizes that were clinically meaningful, not just statistically detectable. Women receiving testosterone also reported reduced sexual distress.

The FDA declined to approve Intrinsa, a testosterone patch designed for women, in 2004, citing concerns about long-term cardiovascular and breast cancer safety data. The concern was not unreasonable at the time. But the Global Consensus Statement authors — including Susan Davis (Monash University), Abdulmaged Traish (Boston University), and Irwin Goldstein (San Diego Sexual Medicine) — argued that the bar being applied to testosterone was inconsistent with how other hormonal therapies had been evaluated. The evidence of harm at physiological doses simply was not, and still is not, there.

On breast cancer specifically: the Davis et al. *Lancet* meta-analysis found no significant increase in breast cancer risk with testosterone therapy. A large observational study by Glaser and Dimitrakakis (2013, *Maturitas*, n = 1,100 over a decade) found that subcutaneous testosterone implants in women were associated with a *lower* incidence of breast cancer compared to the general population — a finding that remains intriguing but requires replication in controlled trials before drawing strong conclusions.

## Why Levels Decline — and When

Testosterone in women doesn't fall off a cliff at menopause the way estrogen does. Instead, it follows a slower, earlier, and more continuous trajectory. Peak levels occur in the **early-to-mid twenties**. After that, there is a gradual decline through the thirties and forties driven largely by decreasing ovarian production. By the time a woman enters perimenopause, her testosterone is already substantially lower than it was two decades earlier.

Menopause contributes an additional layer of complexity. The loss of estrogen triggers a compensatory increase in sex hormone–binding globulin (SHBG), the protein that binds testosterone in the bloodstream and renders it inactive. Even if total testosterone stays relatively stable, **free testosterone** — the bioavailable fraction — can drop sharply. This is why measuring total testosterone alone can be misleading; free or calculated free testosterone gives a more accurate picture of what's actually reaching the tissues.

Surgical menopause — bilateral oophorectomy — causes an abrupt, dramatic fall in testosterone because the ovaries, which supply roughly 50% of a woman's testosterone, are removed. Women who undergo this procedure without androgen replacement report significantly higher rates of sexual dysfunction and fatigue than those who go through natural menopause, a finding documented by Shifren et al. in a series of studies in *The New England Journal of Medicine* and *JAMA*.

---

## Nuance and Caveats

The evidence base for testosterone in women is real, but it is also narrower than for men. Most of the high-quality trial data focuses on **postmenopausal women with HSDD** — not premenopausal women, not women with other symptoms, not women without a diagnosable sexual dysfunction. The Global Consensus Statement explicitly notes that evidence is insufficient to support use of testosterone in premenopausal women for most indications, and recommends against use for cognitive symptoms alone, athletic performance enhancement, or general well-being in the absence of deficiency.

Testing also remains problematic. There is no validated, standardized method for measuring testosterone in women. Assays designed for male ranges often lack the sensitivity to detect the lower concentrations in women accurately. The Global Consensus Statement recommends against relying on serum testosterone levels either to diagnose androgen deficiency or to decide whether to initiate therapy — instead suggesting a trial-based approach in symptomatic women. This is a limitation worth naming honestly: we are working with imperfect tools.

## Practical Protocol

*This article is for informational purposes. Consult a qualified healthcare provider — ideally one with expertise in hormonal health or sexual medicine — before making changes to your health routine.*

If you are a postmenopausal woman experiencing low sexual desire that is causing you distress, and other causes (relationship factors, depression, medication side effects, thyroid dysfunction) have been reasonably excluded, the evidence supports a conversation with a clinician about testosterone therapy.

Because no FDA-approved female testosterone product exists in the United States, treatment typically involves **off-label use of male-formulated testosterone**, applied transdermally at doses that achieve female physiological levels. The Global Consensus Statement recommends targeting a **free testosterone level in the upper quartile of the normal female reference range** — not supraphysiologic male levels, which carry androgenic side effects including acne and unwanted hair growth. Compounded formulations exist but vary in quality; pharmaceutical-grade products (used at lower doses) are generally preferred.

### What to Ask Your Clinician

| Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Should we check free testosterone, not just total? | Total T misses SHBG-related changes |
| Have other causes of low libido been ruled out? | Depression, thyroid, SSRI effects overlap |
| What formulation and dose would you use? | Compounded vs. pharmaceutical-grade matters |
| How will we monitor for side effects? | Check every 3–6 months initially |
| How long will we trial this before reassessing? | 3–6 months is a reasonable window |

If you are premenopausal and experiencing symptoms — fatigue, low libido, mood changes — the path is less clear. Those symptoms warrant investigation, but the evidence for testosterone therapy in that population is insufficient to make a general recommendation. Work with a clinician to explore the full picture before narrowing to any single intervention.
