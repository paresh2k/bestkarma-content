Most people treat jet lag like a hangover — something to push through with coffee and willpower. That framing costs you days. Jet lag isn't tiredness; it's a biological timing disaster. Your circadian clock — the roughly 24-hour oscillator that governs sleep, hormone release, digestion, immune function, and cognitive performance — has been violently displaced from local time. The good news is that this clock is not fixed. It is exquisitely responsive to a small set of environmental signals, and if you know what those signals are and when to deploy them, you can compress a week of misery into 48 hours of manageable adjustment.

The difference between people who recover fast and people who don't is rarely willpower. It's protocol.

---

## Why Eastward Travel Hits Harder

Not all jet lag is equal. Crossing eight time zones flying west from New York to Tokyo feels categorically different from crossing the same eight zones flying east from Tokyo to New York. This asymmetry is not imaginary. The human circadian clock has a natural period of approximately **24.2 hours** — slightly longer than a solar day. That small bias means your clock finds it easier to delay (stay up later) than to advance (go to bed earlier).

Flying west asks your clock to delay, which is closer to its natural drift. Flying east demands the harder thing: advancing the clock, compressing the day. Research by **Eastman and Burgess (2009, Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine)** confirmed this asymmetry, estimating that circadian rhythms advance roughly one hour per day after westward travel but often struggle to exceed **1.5 hours per day** of phase advance after eastward travel under normal conditions — and with strategic light exposure, that rate can be pushed closer to **2 hours per day**.

This matters because your recovery timeline scales with direction. A five-zone westward trip might resolve in three days; the same five zones eastward could take five or six days without intervention. The protocol differs accordingly.

## The Evidence for Melatonin: Dose, Direction, and Timing

Melatonin is the most studied jet lag intervention, and the evidence is genuinely good — with important nuances about dose and timing that most travelers get wrong.

A **Cochrane systematic review by Herxheimer and Petrie (2002)** examined ten randomized controlled trials and found that melatonin taken at the destination bedtime was **effective in reducing jet lag symptoms in 8 of the 10 trials**, with faster self-reported recovery and better sleep quality across flights crossing five or more time zones. Effect sizes were clinically meaningful — not subtle.

The dose question matters more than most people realize. The standard over-the-counter dose in the U.S. is 5–10 mg. But **Lewy et al. (1992, Clinical Pharmacology & Therapeutics)** demonstrated that even **0.5 mg of melatonin raises blood levels to a physiologically meaningful range**, and that higher doses produce supraphysiological concentrations that may actually blunt the phase-shifting signal. A **2001 trial by Atkinson et al. in Ergonomics** found 5 mg outperformed 0.5 mg on some sleep quality measures after eastward flight, but the signal-to-noise ratio argues for starting at **0.5–1 mg for phase-shifting effects** and using higher doses (2–5 mg) primarily as a sleep aid in the destination's first few nights.

Timing is where most people err. Melatonin's phase-shifting effect depends on when in the circadian cycle you take it. **Lewy et al. (2006, PNAS)** modeled the "phase response curve" for melatonin: taken in the early evening (relative to your body clock), it advances the clock — helpful for eastward travel. Taken in the morning, it delays the clock — helpful for westward travel. Taking melatonin at the wrong time relative to your circadian phase can actually slow your recovery.

**Practical rule:** For eastward travel, take 0.5–1 mg melatonin at your *destination's* bedtime, starting the first night. For westward travel, use it primarily to support sleep at local bedtime rather than for phase-shifting, since the clock will drift in the right direction on its own.

## Light Exposure: The Master Lever

Melatonin is useful, but light is the primary zeitgeber — the dominant environmental signal that resets the circadian clock. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus receives direct input from intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) that are maximally sensitive to **short-wavelength blue light (~480 nm)**. Bright morning light advances the clock; bright evening light delays it. This is not subtle biology — it is the mechanism.

**Czeisler et al. (1989, Science)** showed that appropriately timed bright light exposure (over 7,000 lux) could shift circadian rhythms by up to **12 hours**, far exceeding what melatonin achieves alone. **Eastman et al. (1995, Sleep)** demonstrated that intermittent bright light exposure during the biological night could produce phase advances of **2–3 hours per day** — roughly double the unaided rate.

The application for travelers:

- **Eastward travel:** Seek bright outdoor light in the *morning* at your destination for the first 2–3 days. This advances your clock toward local time. Critically, **avoid bright light in the late evening** — it will push your clock in the wrong direction. The Harvard Medical School's Division of Sleep Medicine recommends using blue-light blocking glasses after 8 PM local time for the first two nights after eastward travel.
- **Westward travel:** Seek light in the late afternoon and evening at your destination. Allow yourself to stay up toward local bedtime. Avoid bright morning light for the first day or two, which could accidentally advance your clock and make the delay harder.

If outdoor light is impractical (winter travel, indoor schedules), a **10,000-lux light therapy lamp** for 20–30 minutes at the appropriate time is a validated substitute. The **Chronobiology International consensus guidelines (Sack et al., 2007)** explicitly include light therapy as a first-line intervention for jet lag management.

## Meal Timing and the Argonne Protocol

Your circadian system is not a single clock — it is a hierarchy. The SCN is the master clock, but peripheral clocks in the liver, gut, and metabolic tissues run semi-independently and are strongly entrained by **feeding timing**. This creates a second lever.

The **Argonne Anti-Jet-Lag Diet**, developed by **Ehret and Scanlon** at Argonne National Laboratory and later tested by **Hermos et al. (2002, Military Medicine)** in a study of 186 National Guard personnel deploying to Korea, found that a diet protocol alternating feast and fast days before travel — combined with strategic caffeine use — reduced jet lag symptoms by approximately **7.5 times** compared to controls. The methodology has been critiqued for self-report bias, but the biological rationale is solid: **fasting appears to temporarily subordinate the master SCN clock to peripheral feeding-driven clocks**, allowing meal timing at the destination to accelerate entrainment.

A simpler, evidence-consistent approach drawn from **Stothard et al. (2019, Current Biology)** and circadian feeding research: **avoid large meals during your origin-time nighttime hours** while in transit, and eat your first real meal at a normal mealtime for your destination. Eating breakfast at breakfast time, even if your body clock says midnight, sends a powerful timing signal to peripheral clocks.

## Caffeine as a Clock Tool

Caffeine is not just alertness management — it has direct circadian effects. **Burke et al. (2015, Science Translational Medicine)** found that **200 mg of caffeine (roughly two strong espressos) delayed the circadian clock by approximately 40 minutes** — a meaningful fraction of the typical adjustment window. This makes caffeine a double-edged tool: useful for staying awake at destination-appropriate times, but capable of sabotaging your clock if timed carelessly.

For eastward travel, limit caffeine to morning and early afternoon at your destination. Avoid it after 2 PM local time — it will delay the clock you are trying to advance. For westward travel, a moderate afternoon dose can help sustain wakefulness toward a later local bedtime.

## The Protocol: Day-by-Day Reset Plan

### Eastward Travel (e.g., New York → London, 5 time zones)

| Day | Light | Melatonin | Meals | Caffeine |
|-----|-------|-----------|-------|----------|
| Flight day | Avoid evening light on plane; use eye mask | 0.5–1 mg at destination bedtime | Eat at destination meal times | Morning only (origin time) |
| Day 1 | 20–30 min bright light before 10 AM local | 0.5–1 mg at 10 PM local | Normal local meal schedule | Before 2 PM local only |
| Day 2 | Same; extend outdoor morning time | 0.5–1 mg at 10 PM local | Normal local meals | Before 2 PM local only |
| Day 3 | Morning light; evening light blocking optional | Low dose or discontinue | Normal | Unrestricted if adjusted |

### Westward Travel (e.g., New York → Los Angeles, 3 time zones)

For shorter westward trips, focus on staying up to local bedtime with afternoon/evening light exposure, using melatonin only as a sleep aid if needed (2–5 mg at local bedtime), and avoiding early morning bright light on day one. Most people self-correct within 2–3 days with this approach alone.

## What We Don't Know Yet

The research on jet lag interventions — while robust in aggregate — has real limitations. Most studies are small, involve healthy young adults, and measure self-reported symptoms rather than objective circadian markers. Individual variation in circadian chronotype (owls vs. larks) significantly affects phase-shifting rates, and the optimal protocol likely differs between chronotypes. The interaction effects between melatonin, light, and meal timing are not fully characterized. And almost nothing has been published on jet lag interventions in people over 60, who may have attenuated circadian amplitude and different melatonin kinetics.

The honest summary: the tools above are the best we have, and they are genuinely effective. But they are not perfectly optimized for everyone, and some experimentation — particularly around melatonin dose — is warranted.
