Most active women have never heard of relative energy deficiency in sport. Many are living with it right now — and mistaking it for normal training fatigue.

Here's the problem: the original framework for understanding this condition, the **female athlete triad**, focused narrowly on three linked issues in competitive athletes: disordered eating, menstrual dysfunction, and low bone density. That framing, while useful, missed the full picture. A 2014 consensus statement by the International Olympic Committee introduced the term **RED-S** — relative energy deficiency in sport — and the shift was more than semantic. RED-S recognized that low energy availability doesn't just damage bones and disrupt periods. It suppresses immune function, impairs cognition, destabilizes mood, compromises cardiovascular health, and slows recovery. And crucially, it doesn't only affect elite athletes. It affects the recreational runner training for a half-marathon, the CrossFit regular who cleans up her diet before summer, the cyclist who extends her rides without eating more. The threshold for harm is lower than most people assume.

## The Evidence: What the Research Actually Shows

The foundational work on energy availability comes from **Mary Jane De Souza and colleagues**, whose research established that a threshold of roughly **30 kcal per kilogram of fat-free mass per day** is where things begin to go wrong. Below this level — compared to a functional threshold closer to 45 kcal/kg FFM/day — the body begins to suppress non-essential systems. A 2014 paper by De Souza et al. in *the British Journal of Sports Medicine* synthesized two decades of research and showed that menstrual disruption appears early in the energy-deficiency cascade, often before measurable bone loss occurs.

The bone data are sobering. A landmark study by **Nattiv et al. (2007)** in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, which formed the basis of the American College of Sports Medicine's female athlete triad position stand, found that exercising women with low energy availability had significantly lower bone mineral density at the spine and hip compared to eumenorrheic (regularly menstruating) controls — and that this deficit could persist even after recovery. More recent work has reinforced the durability of the damage: a 2019 study by **Heikura et al.** in the *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*, following elite track-and-field athletes over two competitive seasons, found that athletes with periods of low energy availability had roughly double the bone stress injury rate of those with adequate fueling.

What makes RED-S difficult to catch early is that many effects are subclinical — measurable in the lab before the athlete notices anything. A 2020 review by **Mountjoy et al.** in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, updating the original IOC consensus, documented impairments across ten physiological systems in energy-deficient athletes, including reduced insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), altered thyroid hormone profiles, blunted immune markers, and decreased muscle protein synthesis. Critically, Mountjoy's team noted that **male athletes are also affected** — but for this article, the focus is on the female-specific hormonal cascade, which is more immediately damaging.

## The Mechanism: Why Low Fuel Has Such Far-Reaching Effects

To understand RED-S, you have to understand the hypothalamus as a fuel gauge. The hypothalamus continuously monitors available energy, and when intake drops below what exercise demands, it starts making triage decisions. The first non-essential system it shuts down is reproduction.

The mechanism is called **hypothalamic suppression of the HPG axis** (hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal). When energy is chronically scarce, the hypothalamus reduces the pulsatile secretion of **gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH)**. Without GnRH pulses, the pituitary stops releasing LH and FSH in the patterns necessary to drive ovulation. Estrogen and progesterone fall. Periods become irregular, then absent — a condition called **functional hypothalamic amenorrhea (FHA)**.

This is where bone comes in. Estrogen is one of the primary inhibitors of osteoclasts — the cells that break down bone. When estrogen drops, the brake on bone resorption releases. Meanwhile, low energy availability also suppresses IGF-1 and growth hormone signaling, which reduces osteoblast activity — the cells that build bone. You get simultaneous suppression of bone formation and acceleration of bone loss. In a 20-something athlete at what should be her peak bone-building years, this is a serious long-term liability.

The immune and cognitive effects operate through different but related pathways. Chronic low energy availability elevates cortisol and suppresses T3 (active thyroid hormone), creating a low-grade catabolic state. Research by **Loucks and colleagues** — including a foundational series of controlled studies published in the *Journal of Applied Physiology* in the 1990s and early 2000s — showed that these hormonal disruptions are acute and dose-dependent, appearing within days of energy restriction, not months. The body does not adapt comfortably to chronic underfueling. It accommodates — and in doing so, it sacrifices.

## Nuance and Caveats: Who Is This Really About?

It's worth being precise about who is most at risk. RED-S exists on a spectrum. At the severe end are athletes with diagnosable eating disorders and significant bone loss. But the most common and most overlooked presentations are **subclinical** — athletes who don't restrict intentionally, who simply underestimate how much fuel high training loads demand. A recreational runner increasing her weekly mileage from 25 to 45 miles without increasing caloric intake may have no awareness that her energy availability is dropping. She's not dieting. She's just not compensating.

The **LEAF-Q** (Low Energy Availability in Females Questionnaire), developed by **Melin et al. (2014)** and validated in *the British Journal of Sports Medicine*, is a brief self-report tool designed to identify athletes at risk. It screens for menstrual history, gastrointestinal symptoms, injury history, and body perception — not just eating behaviors. A score ≥8 indicates elevated risk and warrants further assessment. It is not a diagnostic tool, and a low score does not rule out RED-S, but it is useful for flagging athletes who might otherwise go unscreened for years. The questionnaire is freely available online.

What the research does not fully address: most long-term bone data comes from competitive athletes, not recreational ones. Whether the same recovery trajectories apply to someone training 8 hours per week versus 20 is not well established. Similarly, most RED-S research has studied lean-sport athletes (runners, gymnasts, cyclists) — the risk profile for strength-sport athletes or those in higher-weight bodies is less characterized.

## Practical Protocol: What to Actually Do

### Calculate your energy availability

The formula: **EA = (Energy Intake − Exercise Energy Expenditure) ÷ Fat-Free Mass**

You need three values — daily caloric intake (a food tracking app for 3–5 days gives a reasonable estimate), the caloric cost of your exercise sessions (heart rate-based trackers are imperfect but adequate for screening), and your fat-free mass (a DEXA scan is the gold standard; bioelectrical impedance is an accessible proxy). A result under 45 kcal/kg FFM/day warrants attention. Under 30 kcal/kg FFM/day is the clinical threshold for significant risk.

### Watch the early signals

Menstrual irregularity — cycles longer than 35 days, or any cycle loss — is the most sensitive early indicator in premenopausal women. Other signals include persistent fatigue that doesn't resolve with rest, recurrent stress reactions or soft tissue injuries, slower recovery between sessions, low mood or concentration difficulties, and GI disruption during training.

### Rebuild with a caloric surplus, not just maintenance

Recovery from RED-S is not as simple as eating at maintenance. A 2021 paper by **Ackerman et al.** in *the British Journal of Sports Medicine* found that bone density improvements in energy-deficient athletes required a meaningful caloric surplus — not just restoring EA to 45 kcal/kg FFM/day but exceeding it during the recovery phase. Working with a registered sports dietitian is strongly recommended; the goal is to rebuild energy stores and restore hormonal function simultaneously, which may take 6–18 months of sustained effort.

### Reduce load if necessary — temporarily

Reducing training volume by 10–20% while increasing intake is often more effective than dietary change alone in the short term. This is not a permanent prescription, but it creates the energy surplus needed to restart hormonal signaling. Resumption of spontaneous menstruation is the most clinically meaningful marker of recovery.
