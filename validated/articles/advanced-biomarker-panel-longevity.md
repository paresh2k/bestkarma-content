Most people leave their annual physical with a clean bill of health — and a lab report that says almost nothing useful about their future. The standard panel your doctor orders covers roughly six to eight markers: a basic metabolic panel, a complete blood count, maybe a lipid panel. It will catch acute problems and some chronic disease. What it won't catch is the slow metabolic unraveling that predicts cardiovascular disease, cancer, cognitive decline, and premature death decades before symptoms arrive. Longevity medicine has spent the last fifteen years building a different approach — a broader panel of twenty or so biomarkers that, together, describe not just whether you're sick today but how fast you're aging and what's likely to kill you.

What follows is a map of that panel: what each marker measures, what it predicts, and what a meaningful result looks like. Think of it less as a checklist and more as a dashboard — each gauge tells you something different about the engine.

## Cardiovascular Risk: Beyond LDL

**ApoB (Apolipoprotein B)**

Every atherogenic particle in your blood — LDL, VLDL, IDL, Lp(a) — carries exactly one ApoB molecule. That makes ApoB a direct count of the particles that can penetrate arterial walls and initiate plaque. A 2021 meta-analysis by **Sniderman et al.** in *JAMA Cardiology*, pooling data from over 600,000 participants across 14 prospective cohort studies, found that ApoB was a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than LDL-C in every subgroup analyzed, including patients on statins. **Optimal target: below 80 mg/dL; below 60 mg/dL if you have existing cardiovascular risk factors.**

**Lp(a) — Lipoprotein(a)**

Lp(a) is an LDL particle with an additional protein called apolipoprotein(a) attached. It's largely determined by genetics, it doesn't respond meaningfully to lifestyle change, and it independently raises cardiovascular and aortic valve disease risk. The **Copenhagen Heart Study (Kamstrup et al., JAMA 2009)**, following 9,330 people for up to 26 years, found that individuals in the top 20% of Lp(a) had a three-fold higher risk of myocardial infarction. Roughly one in five people have elevated Lp(a) and have no idea because it's not part of standard panels. **Target: below 50 mg/dL (or below 75 nmol/L).**

**hsCRP — High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein**

hsCRP measures systemic low-grade inflammation, the quiet driver behind cardiovascular disease, cancer progression, and accelerated cellular aging. The **JUPITER Trial (Ridker et al., NEJM 2008)** — a randomized trial of 17,802 people with normal LDL but elevated hsCRP — showed that reducing hsCRP with rosuvastatin cut major cardiovascular events by 44%. More importantly, it established that inflammation is an independent cardiovascular risk factor, not just a downstream consequence of other problems. **Optimal: below 1.0 mg/L; values above 3.0 mg/L indicate high risk.**

**Homocysteine**

Elevated homocysteine damages endothelial cells and promotes arterial stiffness. A 2002 meta-analysis in *JAMA* by **Wald et al.** of 12 prospective studies found that each 5 μmol/L increase in homocysteine raised coronary heart disease risk by about 20%. It also serves as a proxy for B-vitamin status — B6, B12, and folate are all required to clear homocysteine from the blood. **Optimal: below 9 μmol/L.**

## Metabolic Health: The Insulin Story Nobody Tells You

**Fasting Insulin**

This is perhaps the most underutilized marker in standard medicine. Insulin resistance — the condition where cells stop responding efficiently to insulin — develops years to decades before blood sugar rises. Your glucose can look perfectly normal while your pancreas is working five times as hard to keep it there. **Gerald Reaven's original research** on insulin resistance syndrome (published in *Diabetes* in 1988) established this, and subsequent work has confirmed that fasting insulin is a far earlier signal of metabolic dysfunction than HbA1c or fasting glucose. **Optimal fasting insulin: below 8 μIU/mL; many longevity clinicians prefer below 5.**

**HbA1c and Fasting Glucose**

Together, these form the standard metabolic snapshot — average blood sugar over three months (HbA1c) and a point-in-time fasting measure. A 2010 study by **Selvin et al.** in *NEJM*, following 11,092 adults over 15 years, found that HbA1c was strongly predictive of diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and death across a continuous gradient — meaning risk begins rising well before the clinical threshold for diabetes. **Optimal HbA1c: below 5.4%; fasting glucose: 70–90 mg/dL.**

**Uric Acid**

Uric acid is the metabolic waste product of purine breakdown, and chronically elevated levels don't just cause gout — they predict hypertension, kidney disease, and cardiovascular events. A 2013 study by **Borghi et al.** in *Journal of Hypertension* and subsequent analyses from the **ARIC cohort** linked serum uric acid above 6.0 mg/dL in women and above 7.0 mg/dL in men with significantly elevated cardiometabolic risk. Fructose metabolism is the primary dietary driver. **Optimal: below 5.5 mg/dL regardless of sex.**

**GGT — Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase**

GGT is best known as a marker of liver stress and alcohol intake, but within-range elevations carry independent predictive value. A large 2004 cohort study by **Brenner et al.** in *Annals of Internal Medicine*, following 163,944 Austrian adults over 17 years, found that GGT in the upper quartile of normal (even below the clinical threshold) was associated with a two-fold increase in cardiovascular mortality. It reflects oxidative stress and early hepatic dysfunction. **Optimal: below 20 U/L in women, below 25 U/L in men.**

## Hormonal and Anabolic Status

**Albumin**

Low albumin is one of the most robust predictors of mortality across populations. In a 2015 analysis of **NHANES data published in *PloS ONE*** covering over 9,000 adults, each 1 g/dL decline in albumin below the optimal range was associated with a 39% increase in all-cause mortality. It reflects nutritional status, hepatic function, and chronic inflammation — when albumin falls, something systemically is wrong. **Optimal: 4.5–5.0 g/dL.**

**Testosterone (Total and Free) + SHBG**

In men, declining testosterone predicts cardiovascular disease, depression, sarcopenia, and cognitive decline — not just libido and energy. A landmark study by **Khaw et al.** in *Circulation* (2007), following 11,606 men for up to 7 years, found that men in the lowest testosterone quartile had a 41% higher all-cause mortality risk than those in the highest quartile. Free testosterone matters as much as total because SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin) binds testosterone and renders it inactive; high SHBG can produce symptoms of low testosterone even when total levels look normal. Women also benefit from tracking these markers — testosterone is essential for bone density, muscle maintenance, and energy in women too. **Men: optimal total testosterone 600–900 ng/dL; free testosterone 15–25 pg/mL. Women: discuss with a clinician given lifecycle variation.**

**IGF-1 — Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1**

IGF-1 is the primary anabolic mediator of growth hormone. It's a double-edged marker: too low predicts sarcopenia and cognitive decline; too high may promote cancer cell proliferation. A 2012 study by **Renehan et al.** in *The Lancet Oncology*, a meta-analysis of 31 prospective studies covering over 21,000 cases, found statistically significant associations between high IGF-1 and colorectal, prostate, and breast cancer. **Optimal range: 100–200 ng/mL for adults — interpret in clinical context.**

**DHEA-S — Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate**

DHEA-S is the most abundant circulating steroid and a precursor to both testosterone and estrogen. It declines by approximately 80% between age 25 and 70. The **Baltimore Longitudinal Study of Aging** found that men with higher DHEA-S levels had significantly lower all-cause mortality over a 19-year follow-up. Its independent predictive value is modest, but as part of a hormonal picture, it's a useful proxy for biological age relative to chronological age. **Reference range varies significantly by age and sex — trend over time matters more than any single value.**

**TSH, Free T3, and Free T4**

TSH alone misses subclinical thyroid dysfunction. Measuring the full triad — TSH, free T3, and free T4 — gives a much more complete picture of thyroid axis health. Subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH with normal T4) affects up to 10% of adults and is associated with elevated LDL, depression, and fatigue that often goes unattributed. **Optimal TSH: 1.0–2.5 mIU/L; free T3 and T4 should fall in the mid-to-upper half of laboratory reference ranges.**

## Nutritional and Biological Age Markers

**Ferritin**

Ferritin stores iron, but it's also an acute-phase reactant — it rises with inflammation. Very low ferritin signals iron deficiency; very high ferritin indicates inflammation, metabolic syndrome, or iron overload. A 2013 study by **Ellervik et al.** in *Clinical Chemistry* found that ferritin above 200 ng/mL in women and above 300 ng/mL in men was independently associated with elevated cardiovascular and metabolic disease risk. **Optimal: 50–150 ng/mL for women; 75–150 ng/mL for men.**

**25-OH Vitamin D**

Vitamin D deficiency is associated with cardiovascular disease, autoimmune conditions, all-cause cancer mortality, and cognitive decline. The **VITAL Trial (Manson et al., NEJM 2019)** — a randomized trial of 25,871 adults over 5 years — found that vitamin D3 supplementation (2,000 IU/day) reduced cancer mortality by 17% and significantly reduced metastatic cancer incidence. Observational data from the **UK Biobank**, analyzed by **Jiang et al. in *BMJ* (2022)**, linked serum 25-OH vitamin D below 25 nmol/L with a 25% higher all-cause mortality. **Optimal: 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L).**

**Omega-3 Index**

The omega-3 index — the percentage of EPA and DHA in red blood cell membranes — is a far better proxy for tissue omega-3 status than dietary recall. A study by **Harris and Von Schacky** in *Preventive Medicine* (2004) coined the index and found that an index below 4% was associated with approximately a 10-fold higher risk of sudden cardiac death compared to an index above 8%. **Optimal: above 8%. Most Americans sit around 4–5%.**

**Epigenetic Age (DNA Methylation)**

The most conceptually powerful marker on this list isn't a standard blood test yet — but it's becoming one. Epigenetic age clocks, most notably the **Dunedin PACE clock and GrimAge clock** developed by **Belsky, Levine, and colleagues** and validated in longitudinal cohorts including the **Dunedin Study** (published in *eLife*, 2020), measure the pace of biological aging by analyzing methylation patterns on DNA from a blood sample. In a 2022 study in *Nature Aging*, **Higgins-Chen et al.** found that epigenetic age acceleration was independently predictive of mortality across multiple cohorts after controlling for all conventional risk factors. You can now order this commercially through providers like TruDiagnostic or Elysium. **Biological age that exceeds chronological age by more than 2–3 years warrants investigation of modifiable drivers.**

## How to Use This Panel

This isn't a shopping list for your next physical — it's a strategic framework. Not every marker needs annual measurement. Lp(a), once measured, rarely changes meaningfully and can be tested once. Epigenetic age makes more sense every two to three years as a trend marker. Fasting insulin, hsCRP, and ApoB, by contrast, respond to lifestyle interventions and are worth tracking annually if your metabolic health is a work in progress.

**Practical steps:**
- Print this list and bring it to your primary care physician or order through a direct-access lab (Ulta Lab Tests, Quest Diagnostics, and Function Health all offer most of these)
- Prioritize based on family history: Lp(a) and ApoB if cardiovascular disease runs in your family; insulin and GGT if metabolic syndrome does
- Don't optimize in isolation — elevated hsCRP means something different if your ApoB is also high versus when everything else looks clean
- Establish a baseline now, so you have trend data in ten years when it matters most

The goal of this panel is not to find disease — it's to intercept it. Most of what kills us in middle age was predictable twenty years earlier by the right markers. The standard panel just wasn't looking.
