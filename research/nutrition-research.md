## Article 1: The Mediterranean Diet — Why Every Large Study Points to the Same Eating Pattern

**Angle:** The most-studied dietary pattern in the world and what it consistently shows for cardiovascular disease, dementia prevention, and longevity — with practical implementation.

**Key Sources:**
- PubMed 39599734 — Mediterranean diet and cardiovascular outcomes (2024)
- PubMed 38431146 — Mediterranean diet adherence and all-cause mortality
- PREDIMED trial data (ongoing reference standard)
- Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health dietary guidelines

**Key Facts:**
- Consistently associated with reduced CVD, type 2 diabetes, Alzheimer's, certain cancers
- Key components: olive oil (EVOO), legumes, whole grains, fish 2-3×/week, moderate red wine
- Not a rigid diet — more of an eating pattern with cultural flexibility
- PREDIMED trial: 30% reduction in major cardiovascular events vs. low-fat diet

## Article 2: Time-Restricted Eating — What the Research Actually Says (And Doesn't)

**Angle:** A nuanced, evidence-based look at TRE: what studies show for metabolic health, weight, and longevity markers — and what remains overhyped.

**Key Sources:**
- PubMed 35939311 — TRE and metabolic improvements (2022 landmark trial)
- Frontiers in Nutrition 2025 — TRE in different populations
- Cell Metabolism 2020 — 10-hour TRE in metabolic syndrome patients
- Nature Reviews Endocrinology 2025 — Critical assessment of fasting/TRE evidence

**Key Facts:**
- 8-10 hour eating window most studied and most feasible
- Benefits: improved insulin sensitivity, reduced blood pressure, weight loss (modest)
- Aligning eating window with daylight (earlier in day) amplifies metabolic benefits
- Concern: 2024 NHANES analysis linked 8-hr TRE with 91% increased cardiovascular death risk — methodological critiques warranted; context matters
- Best evidence: in people with metabolic syndrome or insulin resistance

## Article 3: Ultra-Processed Foods — The Ingredient Hidden in Plain Sight

**Angle:** What NOVA classification reveals about food processing, how UPF consumption is measured, and the growing evidence linking high UPF intake to every major disease.

**Key Sources:**
- The Lancet 2023/2025 — UPF and mortality meta-analysis
- PubMed 38719536 — UPF and cardiovascular disease risk
- PubMed 38418082 — UPF and all-cause mortality cohort study
- BMJ 2024 — UPF and 32 adverse health outcomes (landmark umbrella review)

**Key Facts:**
- NOVA classification: Group 4 = ultra-processed (industrial formulations, 5+ ingredients, additives)
- ~57% of calories in US diet come from UPFs; UK ~56%
- BMJ umbrella review: higher UPF consumption linked to 32 adverse outcomes including mortality, cancer, CVD, depression, metabolic syndrome
- Mechanisms: displacement of whole foods, additive effects, appetite dysregulation via hyperpalatability
- Not about occasional treats — dose-response relationship

## Article 4: Protein and Muscle — Why Adequate Protein Is the Most Undervalued Longevity Tool

**Angle:** The sarcopenia epidemic, why older adults need more protein than current RDA suggests, and practical guidance on protein quantity, distribution, and quality.

**Key Sources:**
- PubMed 40093878 — Protein intake and sarcopenia prevention (2025)
- PMC 11978179 — Optimal protein distribution for muscle protein synthesis
- Journal of Nutrition 2025 — Per-meal protein thresholds for MPS
- PROT-AGE consensus statement

**Key Facts:**
- RDA of 0.8g/kg is a minimum, not an optimum — especially for those 65+
- Emerging evidence: 1.2-1.6g/kg optimal for preserving muscle; some researchers suggest higher for older adults
- Protein distribution matters: 30-40g per meal optimizes muscle protein synthesis better than skewing to dinner
- Animal vs. plant protein: complete amino acid profile and leucine content are key variables
- Sarcopenia starts at age 30 — prevention is better than rehabilitation

## Article 5: Omega-3s — Sorting EPA, DHA, and ALA From the Noise

**Angle:** What EPA and DHA actually do (vs. ALA), which conditions have the strongest evidence, how to assess your intake, and food vs. supplement sources.

**Key Sources:**
- Nature Scientific Reports 2025 — EPA/DHA and inflammation resolution
- PMC 11878108 — Omega-3 and cardiovascular outcomes meta-analysis 2025
- VITAL trial (ongoing reference) — fish oil supplementation at scale
- American Heart Association 2023 advisory on omega-3

**Key Facts:**
- EPA reduces triglycerides and inflammation; DHA critical for brain structure
- ALA (plant-based, flaxseed) converts to EPA/DHA at only 5-10% efficiency
- VITAL trial: 25% reduction in heart attack in those not eating fish
- REDUCE-IT (high-dose EPA): 25% reduction in cardiovascular events
- Best food sources: fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), some algae-based sources for vegans
- Omega-6:3 ratio in Western diet is ~15-20:1 vs. optimal ~4:1

## Article 6: Dietary Fiber — The Most Powerful Gut Microbiome Intervention You're Probably Not Getting Enough Of

**Angle:** The fiber gap, how different types of fiber feed different microbiome species, and why fiber diversity matters more than total quantity.

**Key Sources:**
- PubMed 29757343 — Dietary fiber and gut microbiome diversity (Sonnenburg lab)
- mSystems 2024 — Fiber variety and microbiome response
- Cell 2021 — High-fiber vs. high-fermented food RCT (Sonnenburg/Gardner Stanford) - DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2021.06.019
- AHRQ dietary fiber systematic review

**Key Facts:**
- Average US fiber intake: 15g/day vs. recommended 25-38g/day
- Not just quantity — variety of fiber types feeds broader microbial diversity
- Prebiotic fibers (inulin, FOS, resistant starch) specifically fuel beneficial species
- Fiber → short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate) → gut barrier integrity, anti-inflammatory signaling
- 30 different plants per week (Michael Pollan/Sonnenburg framework) as practical goal

## Article 7: Fermented Foods — The Cell 2021 Study That Changed How We Think About the Microbiome

**Angle:** Deep dive into the landmark Sonnenburg RCT that compared high-fiber vs. high-fermented food diets, what fermented foods actually contain, and practical guidance.

**Key Sources:**
- Cell 2021, PubMed 34256014 — Sonnenburg/Gardner Stanford RCT
- Frontiers in Microbiology 2025 — Fermented food mechanisms
- EFSA probiotic guidelines
- Harvard Health 2025 fermented foods overview

**Key Facts:**
- Sonnenburg RCT: 10-week high-fermented food diet increased microbiome diversity AND reduced inflammatory markers (18 proteins including IL-6 and IL-12) — high-fiber diet did NOT increase diversity
- Fermented foods: yogurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, kombucha, miso
- Live cultures must survive to the gut — viability varies by product
- Different from probiotic supplements: fermented foods contain diverse native organisms + fermentation metabolites
- Not all fermented foods are equal: heat-treated products lose live cultures

## Article 8: Vitamin D — The Deficiency You Probably Don't Know You Have

**Angle:** The prevalence of vitamin D deficiency, its roles beyond bone health (immune function, muscle strength, mood, cancer risk), and how to optimize levels.

**Key Sources:**
- Nature Reviews Endocrinology 2021 — Vitamin D comprehensive review
- PubMed 37111028 — Vitamin D deficiency global prevalence 2023
- VITAL trial vitamin D arm — cancer and cardiovascular outcomes
- Endocrine Society guidelines (2024 updated)

**Key Facts:**
- ~1 billion people worldwide are vitamin D deficient
- Deficiency defined as <20 ng/mL; many experts consider <30 ng/mL insufficient
- Roles: calcium absorption, immune modulation (T-cell activation), muscle function, mood regulation
- VITAL trial: 25% reduction in cancer mortality with vitamin D3 supplementation (2000 IU/day)
- Sunlight is primary source but latitude, skin tone, sunscreen all affect synthesis
- Food sources limited; supplementation often necessary in northern latitudes

## Article 9: Polyphenols — The Plant Compounds Behind the "Eat More Plants" Advice

**Angle:** What polyphenols are, how they work (antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, prebiotic), which food sources are richest, and the most evidence-backed specific compounds.

**Key Sources:**
- PubMed 41303557 — Polyphenols and aging biomarkers 2025
- PMC 12114605 — Polyphenol mechanisms and human health 2025
- Cell 2023 — Urolithin A and mitophagy
- Nature Medicine 2022 — Resveratrol, quercetin, EGCG evidence update

**Key Facts:**
- Polyphenols: 8,000+ compounds in plants — flavonoids, phenolic acids, stilbenes, lignans
- Act as prebiotics feeding Akkermansia, Bifidobacterium
- Urolithin A (pomegranate → gut bacteria product): activates mitophagy, cleared for human use
- EGCG (green tea): anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer evidence; bioavailability challenges
- Resveratrol hype largely not validated in human RCTs at relevant doses
- Best strategy: diverse whole foods rather than supplements

## Article 10: Blood Sugar Regulation — Why Glucose Stability Is the Foundation of Energy and Longevity

**Angle:** Why glucose variability (not just fasting glucose) matters, what continuous glucose monitoring reveals, and lifestyle approaches to improve insulin sensitivity.

**Key Sources:**
- Stanford CGM study (Sonnenburg/Gardner) — glucose variability in healthy adults
- Nature Metabolism 2024 — Postprandial glucose and mortality risk
- Frontiers in Endocrinology 2025 — Insulin resistance as a root cause of chronic disease
- CGM population studies (Levels, Abbott Libre data)

**Key Facts:**
- Insulin resistance precedes type 2 diabetes by 10-15 years
- Post-meal glucose spikes (above 140mg/dL) cause oxidative stress and glycation damage
- Even non-diabetics show highly variable glucose responses to identical meals (WEIZMANN personalized nutrition study)
- Interventions: exercise timing (post-meal walks), food order (vegetables/protein before carbs), vinegar
- Sleep deprivation dramatically impairs glucose regulation — glucose + sleep are linked
